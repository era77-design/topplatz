Removing mold from walls typically involves cleaning the affected area with a diluted bleach solution or vinegar, ensuring proper ventilation, and addressing the moisture source. Always wear appropriate protective gear to prevent exposure to mold spores.

## What You Need

- Rubber gloves
- Eye protection (goggles)
- N95 respirator mask
- Spray bottle
- Stiff-bristled scrub brush or sponge
- Buckets
- Old towels or rags
- White vinegar
- Household bleach (optional, for non-porous surfaces)
- Water
- Fan or dehumidifier

## Steps

### Step 1: Prepare the Area and Yourself

Before you begin, ensure the area is well-ventilated by opening windows and using fans. Turn off any HVAC systems to prevent mold spores from spreading. Lay down old towels or plastic sheeting to protect the floor and surrounding furniture. Don your protective gear: rubber gloves, eye protection, and an N95 respirator mask to avoid inhaling mold spores or coming into contact with cleaning solutions.

> 💡 **Tip:** Isolate the affected area by closing doors to other rooms. This helps contain spores during cleaning.

### Step 2: Identify the Mold Type and Source

Determine if the mold is superficial or if it's indicative of a deeper problem. Small, surface-level mold patches can often be handled yourself. If the mold covers a large area (more than 10 square feet), appears fuzzy or slimy, or if you suspect it's black mold, it's best to consult a professional. Crucially, identify and fix the moisture source causing the mold, such as a leaky pipe or high humidity, otherwise, it will return. For severe cases or if you suspect toxic varieties, consult our guide on [How to Get Rid of Black Mold Safely and Effectively](/en/how-to-get-rid-of-black-mold).

> 💡 **Tip:** If the mold is a result of contaminated water (e.g., sewage), do not attempt to clean it yourself; call a professional.

### Step 3: Choose Your Cleaning Solution

For most surface mold on walls, a simple white vinegar solution is highly effective and safer than bleach for porous materials. Fill a spray bottle with undiluted white vinegar and spray directly onto the mold. For non-porous surfaces where mold is particularly stubborn, you can use a diluted bleach solution: mix 1 cup of household bleach with 1 gallon of water. Never mix bleach with ammonia or other cleaners, as this creates toxic fumes.

> 💡 **Tip:** Test your chosen cleaning solution on an inconspicuous spot of the wall first to ensure it doesn't damage or discolor the surface.

### Step 4: Apply the Cleaning Solution

Generously spray or wipe your chosen cleaning solution directly onto the moldy area. For vinegar, allow it to sit for at least an hour to penetrate and kill the mold. If using a bleach solution, let it sit for 10-15 minutes. This dwell time is crucial for the solution to effectively kill the mold spores. Ensure the area remains wet with the solution during this period.

> 💡 **Tip:** Do not rinse the vinegar solution immediately after application, as its acidity helps inhibit future mold growth.

### Step 5: Scrub and Clean the Wall

After the solution has had time to work, use a stiff-bristled scrub brush or a sponge to thoroughly scrub the mold from the wall surface. Apply firm but gentle pressure to avoid damaging the wall. For stubborn spots, reapply the solution and scrub again. Ensure all visible mold is removed from the surface.

> 💡 **Tip:** Dispose of any sponges, rags, or brushes used for cleaning in a sealed plastic bag after you're done.

### Step 6: Rinse and Dry Thoroughly

Once the mold is removed, wipe down the area with a clean cloth dampened with plain water to remove any cleaning solution residue. It's crucial to dry the wall completely to prevent the mold from returning. Use old towels to blot the area dry, then use fans or a dehumidifier to ensure the wall is bone dry. Good air circulation is key to preventing future mold growth.

> 💡 **Tip:** Leave fans running in the room for several hours or even a day after cleaning to ensure complete dryness.

### Step 7: Prevent Future Mold Growth

To prevent mold from reappearing, address the root cause of the moisture. Repair any leaks, improve ventilation in damp areas like bathrooms and kitchens, and control indoor humidity levels (ideally below 60%) using a dehumidifier. Regularly clean and inspect areas prone to moisture. Consider using mold-inhibiting paints in high-risk areas.

> 💡 **Tip:** Monitor the humidity levels in your home with a hygrometer, especially in basements and bathrooms.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always wear protective gear, including gloves, eye protection, and an N95 mask, to avoid contact with mold spores and cleaning solutions.
- Never mix bleach with ammonia or any other household cleaners, as this can create dangerous toxic fumes.
- For small, superficial mold patches, white vinegar is a safe and effective natural alternative to bleach.
- Ensure the affected area is completely dry after cleaning to prevent mold from returning.
- If the mold covers an area larger than 10 square feet or if you suspect it's black mold, it's best to consult a professional mold remediation specialist.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never mix bleach with ammonia, vinegar, or any other cleaning products, as this can produce highly toxic and potentially fatal fumes.
- ⚠️ Always ensure adequate ventilation when cleaning mold, especially when using bleach-based solutions.
- ⚠️ If you experience respiratory issues, dizziness, or headaches during mold removal, stop immediately and seek fresh air. Consult a doctor if symptoms persist.
- ⚠️ Do not attempt to remove mold if you have a compromised immune system, respiratory conditions, or are pregnant; professional help is recommended.
- ⚠️ If mold is widespread or appears after significant water damage (e.g., flooding), professional mold remediation is essential.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you remove mold from painted walls?**

To remove mold from painted walls, use a mild cleaning solution like diluted white vinegar (undiluted for tougher spots) or a gentle, non-abrasive household cleaner. Apply the solution, let it sit, then gently scrub the area with a soft sponge or cloth to avoid damaging the paint. Rinse lightly with water and dry thoroughly.

**How do you remove mold from inside walls?**

Removing mold from inside walls typically requires opening up the wall to access the affected area. This is a more complex task that often indicates a significant, hidden moisture problem. It's usually best to consult a professional mold remediation specialist for mold inside walls, as it can be extensive and pose health risks.

**What kills mold on walls permanently?**

No single product can kill mold permanently without addressing the underlying moisture source. The most effective approach is to thoroughly clean and remove the mold, then eliminate the conditions that allowed it to grow, such as high humidity or water leaks. Controlling moisture is key to permanent prevention.

**Is it safe to remove mold yourself?**

For small areas of surface mold (less than 10 square feet), it is generally safe to remove yourself with proper protective gear like gloves, eye protection, and an N95 mask. However, for larger infestations, mold from contaminated water, or if you have health concerns, it's safer to consult a professional.

**Can I use bleach to clean mold from walls?**

Bleach can be effective for killing mold on non-porous surfaces, but it's less effective on porous materials like drywall as it often doesn't penetrate deeply enough to kill the roots. If using bleach, dilute it (1 cup bleach to 1 gallon water), ensure excellent ventilation, and never mix it with other cleaners.

**How can I prevent mold from growing on my walls?**

Prevent mold by controlling humidity levels (keep them below 60%), fixing any leaks promptly, ensuring good ventilation in bathrooms and kitchens, and allowing air to circulate around furniture. Using mold-inhibiting paints in high-moisture areas can also offer an extra layer of protection.

**When should I call a professional for mold removal?**

You should call a professional for mold removal if the mold covers an area larger than 10 square feet, if it's due to contaminated water (like sewage), if you suspect it's growing inside walls, or if you experience health issues related to mold exposure. Professionals have the tools and expertise for safe and thorough remediation.
