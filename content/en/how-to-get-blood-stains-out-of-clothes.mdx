To get blood stains out of clothes, act quickly by rinsing the stain with cold water immediately. For fresh stains, gently dab with a cloth soaked in hydrogen peroxide or a mild detergent solution. For dried stains, pre-soaking and then treating with an enzyme cleaner can be highly effective.

## What You Need

- Cold water
- Clean cloths or paper towels
- Mild laundry detergent
- Hydrogen peroxide (3%)
- Salt
- Enzyme pre-treater (optional, for dried stains)
- Old toothbrush (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Act Quickly with Cold Water

As soon as you notice a blood stain, rinse the affected area under cold running water from the back of the fabric. This helps to push the blood out rather than further into the fibers. Avoid hot water, as it can set the stain permanently, making it much harder to remove. The colder the water, the better for fresh stains.

> 💡 **Tip:** Always rinse from the back of the fabric to push the stain out.

### Step 2: Apply a Detergent Solution

If the stain persists after rinsing, mix a small amount of mild laundry detergent with cold water to create a solution. Apply this directly to the stain using a clean cloth or your fingers, gently working it into the fabric. Let it sit for a few minutes before rinsing thoroughly with cold water. Repeat if necessary.

> 💡 **Tip:** Liquid hand soap or dish soap can also work in a pinch for fresh stains.

### Step 3: Try Hydrogen Peroxide for Fresh Stains

For fresh blood stains, hydrogen peroxide (3%) can be very effective. Pour a small amount directly onto the stain, or dab it on with a clean cloth. You should see it fizz as it breaks down the blood. Rinse thoroughly with cold water after a few minutes, as hydrogen peroxide can bleach some darker fabrics if left too long. Always test on an inconspicuous area first.

> 💡 **Tip:** Hydrogen peroxide works best on fresh stains; test on a hidden seam first.

### Step 4: Use a Salt and Cold Water Paste (for stubborn fresh stains)

Create a thick paste by mixing salt with a small amount of cold water. Apply this paste directly onto the blood stain and let it sit for 10-15 minutes. The salt helps to draw the blood out of the fabric. After the time is up, gently scrub with an old toothbrush or your fingers and rinse thoroughly with cold water until the stain begins to lift.

> 💡 **Tip:** This method is particularly useful for delicate fabrics where harsh scrubbing is not advisable.

### Step 5: For Dried Stains, Pre-Soak and Use Enzyme Cleaner

For dried blood stains, start by soaking the item in cold water for several hours or even overnight. This helps to rehydrate the blood. Then, apply an enzyme-based laundry pre-treater directly to the stain. Enzyme cleaners are excellent at breaking down protein-based stains like blood. Allow it to sit for the time recommended on the product label.

> 💡 **Tip:** Look for laundry detergents or pre-treaters specifically labeled as 'enzyme-based' for best results on dried blood.

### Step 6: Launder as Usual (in cold water)

After treating the stain with your chosen method, wash the garment in your washing machine using cold water and your regular laundry detergent. It's crucial to check the stained area before drying the item. If any trace of the stain is still visible, do not put the item in the dryer, as the heat will set the stain permanently into the fabric fibers.

> 💡 **Tip:** Consider adding an oxygen-based bleach (safe for colors) to your wash cycle for extra stain-fighting power.

### Step 7: Air Dry and Reassess

Once washed, air dry the garment to ensure the stain is completely gone. Air drying allows you to clearly see if any discoloration remains. If any trace of the stain remains, do not put it in the dryer. Repeat the treatment process from the beginning until the stain is fully removed. Drying will permanently set any remaining stain, making it almost impossible to remove later.

> 💡 **Tip:** Sunlight can sometimes help to further bleach out faint remaining stains on white fabrics.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always use cold water for blood stains; hot water will set them permanently.
- Act as quickly as possible; fresh stains are significantly easier to remove than dried ones.
- Test any cleaning solution on an inconspicuous area of the garment first, especially for colored or delicate fabrics, to prevent damage or discoloration.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Hydrogen peroxide can bleach or lighten some fabrics; always test on an hidden area first, especially on dark or delicate materials.
- ⚠️ Avoid scrubbing vigorously, as this can spread the stain further or damage the fabric fibers, making the stain harder to remove.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can hot water remove blood stains?**

No, hot water will actually cook the proteins in the blood, causing the stain to set permanently into the fabric fibers. Always use cold water when treating blood stains to help lift them away effectively.

**What if the blood stain is already dry?**

For dried blood stains, start by soaking the item in cold water for several hours or overnight to rehydrate the stain. Then, treat it with an enzyme-based pre-treater or a paste of salt and cold water before washing in cold water.

**Is hydrogen peroxide safe for all fabrics?**

Hydrogen peroxide (3%) is generally safe for most white and colorfast fabrics, but it can cause bleaching or lightening on some darker or delicate materials. Always test it on an inconspicuous area first to ensure it won't damage the fabric.

**What household items can I use for blood stains?**

Common household items effective for treating blood stains include cold water, mild laundry detergent, salt, and 3% hydrogen peroxide. For best results, act quickly with these items before the stain has a chance to set.

**Should I put the stained item in the dryer?**

No, never put a garment with a blood stain in the dryer until you are absolutely certain the stain is completely removed. The heat from the dryer will permanently set any remaining stain, making it nearly impossible to remove later.

**What if the blood stain is on a delicate fabric like silk or wool?**

For delicate fabrics, avoid harsh scrubbing or strong chemicals like hydrogen peroxide. Gently blot with cold water and a mild, pH-neutral detergent specifically designed for delicates. For valuable items, consider professional dry cleaning to ensure proper care.

**How long can a blood stain sit before it's impossible to remove?**

While fresh stains are easiest, even dried blood stains can often be removed with proper treatment, though it may require more effort and time. The longer a stain sits, especially if exposed to heat, the harder it becomes, but it's rarely truly 'impossible' with persistence.
