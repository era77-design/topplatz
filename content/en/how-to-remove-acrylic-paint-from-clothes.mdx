Removing acrylic paint from clothes is often possible if you act quickly, especially while the paint is still wet. For dried paint, a combination of scraping, rubbing alcohol, and vigorous washing can help lift the stain from most fabric types.

## What You Need

- Paper towels or clean cloths
- Dull knife or spoon
- Rubbing alcohol (isopropyl alcohol)
- Acetone (nail polish remover, patch test first!)
- Liquid dish soap
- Liquid laundry detergent
- Old toothbrush or scrub brush
- Warm water

## Steps

### Step 1: Scrape Off Excess Paint

If the paint is still wet, gently blot away as much as possible using a clean paper towel or cloth, being careful not to spread the stain. For dried paint, use a dull knife, spoon, or even your fingernail to carefully scrape off any thick, crusted layers from the fabric surface. This initial removal prevents the paint from further embedding itself and makes subsequent steps more effective.

### Step 2: Apply Rubbing Alcohol

Turn the garment inside out and place a clean paper towel underneath the stain. Dampen a clean cloth or cotton ball with rubbing alcohol (isopropyl alcohol) and gently blot the stain from the back. The alcohol helps to dissolve the acrylic polymers. Continue blotting and replacing the paper towel underneath as it absorbs the paint, working from the outside of the stain inward to prevent spreading.

> 💡 **Tip:** Always test rubbing alcohol on an inconspicuous area of the fabric first to ensure it doesn't cause discoloration or damage.

### Step 3: Try Acetone for Stubborn Stains

For particularly stubborn or dried acrylic paint stains on robust fabrics like cotton or denim, acetone (found in most nail polish removers) can be very effective. Crucially, always perform a patch test on an hidden seam first, as acetone can damage synthetic fabrics like rayon, acetate, or modacrylic. If safe, apply a small amount to a clean cloth and blot the stain, following the same inside-out blotting technique as with rubbing alcohol.

### Step 4: Scrub with Dish Soap

Once the majority of the paint has been lifted by the alcohol or acetone, apply a small amount of liquid dish soap directly onto the remaining stain. Use an old toothbrush or a stiff-bristled scrub brush to gently work the dish soap into the fabric in small circular motions. This helps to further break down the paint and lift it from the fibers, preparing it for rinsing.

### Step 5: Rinse and Pre-treat with Laundry Detergent

Rinse the treated area thoroughly under warm running water. After rinsing, apply a small amount of your regular liquid laundry detergent directly to the damp stain. Allow it to sit on the stain for 5-10 minutes, letting the detergent's enzymes work to break down any remaining paint particles. This pre-treatment boosts the overall cleaning power before the garment goes into the washing machine.

### Step 6: Wash and Inspect Thoroughly

Wash the garment in your washing machine according to its care label instructions, using the hottest water temperature safe for the fabric. After the wash cycle, do not put the item in the dryer until you have thoroughly inspected the stain. Heat from a dryer will set any remaining paint, making it nearly impossible to remove later.

### Step 7: Repeat if Stain Persists

If the stain is still visible after the first wash and inspection, do not despair. Repeat the entire process from step 2 (applying rubbing alcohol) or step 3 (acetone, if appropriate) before washing again. Sometimes, multiple treatments are necessary to completely remove stubborn or large acrylic paint stains from clothing.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always test cleaning solutions on an inconspicuous area of the fabric first.
- Work from the outside of the stain inward to prevent spreading the paint.
- Use white cloths or paper towels to avoid transferring color to your garment.
- Act quickly – fresh, wet paint is significantly easier to remove than dried paint.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always check the garment's care label before using any cleaning agents or hot water.
- ⚠️ Acetone can damage or dissolve certain synthetic fabrics (like rayon, acetate, triacetate, modacrylic) and some plastics. Always patch test first.
- ⚠️ Ensure good ventilation when using strong solvents like rubbing alcohol or acetone.
- ⚠️ Do not mix cleaning agents unless explicitly instructed, as this can create dangerous fumes or ineffective solutions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How to remove dried paint from clothes?**

Removing dried acrylic paint from clothes requires a bit more effort but is often achievable. Start by carefully scraping off any thick, crusted paint, then treat the stain with rubbing alcohol or, for tougher stains on suitable fabrics, acetone. Follow up with dish soap and a machine wash.

**How to remove paint from clothes effectively?**

To effectively remove paint from clothes, especially acrylic, speed is key. Begin by blotting or scraping off excess paint, then use a solvent like rubbing alcohol. Follow with a dish soap scrub and a thorough machine wash, inspecting the garment before drying.

**What is the best way to remove fabric paint from clothes?**

Most fabric paints are acrylic-based, so the methods for removing acrylic paint are generally effective. Treat the stain with rubbing alcohol or acetone (if safe for the fabric), scrub with dish soap, and then wash the garment according to its care instructions.

**Can I remove oil paint from clothes using these methods?**

No, oil paint requires different removal techniques than acrylic paint. For oil paint, you'll typically need to use mineral spirits, turpentine, or a specific oil paint remover before washing. Always test these solvents on an inconspicuous area first.

**What if the acrylic paint stain is very old?**

Very old acrylic paint stains are more challenging but not always impossible to remove. You'll likely need to repeat the solvent (rubbing alcohol or acetone) and scrubbing steps multiple times, allowing the solvent to penetrate for longer periods. Persistence and patience are key for older stains.

**Will hot water set the paint stain?**

Yes, heat, especially from a clothes dryer, can permanently set a paint stain into the fabric fibers, making it extremely difficult, if not impossible, to remove. Always ensure the stain is completely gone before putting the garment in the dryer; air dry if necessary.

**Is there a natural way to remove acrylic paint?**

While some suggest vinegar or hairspray, these are generally less effective than rubbing alcohol or acetone for acrylic paint. For a more "natural" approach, dish soap and warm water can help with fresh stains, but solvents are usually required for dried or stubborn marks.
