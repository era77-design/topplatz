Growing potatoes from existing potatoes is a rewarding and straightforward process. By selecting healthy 'seed' potatoes and providing the right conditions, you can easily cultivate a fresh harvest right in your own garden or containers. This method allows you to turn a sprouted potato into a productive plant.

## What You Need

- Seed potatoes (sprouted or unsprouted)
- Sharp knife (optional, for cutting larger potatoes)
- Well-draining soil or potting mix
- Garden bed, raised bed, large containers, or grow bags
- Shovel or trowel
- Compost or balanced fertilizer
- Watering can or hose

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Choose and Chit Your Seed Potatoes

Select healthy, firm potatoes, preferably organic or certified 'seed potatoes,' as these are disease-free. To encourage sprouting, a process called 'chitting,' place them in a cool, bright spot (not direct sunlight) for 2-4 weeks. You'll see short, stubby green or purple sprouts emerge from the 'eyes' of the potato.

> 💡 **Tip:** Avoid potatoes with long, spindly white sprouts, as these indicate they've been stored in the dark too long and are weaker.

### Step 2: 2. Prepare Your Seed Potato Pieces

If your seed potatoes are larger than a chicken egg, you can cut them into pieces. Each piece should be at least 1.5 to 2 inches square and have at least one or two healthy sprouts (eyes). After cutting, allow the pieces to 'callus' or dry for 1-2 days in a cool, dry place. This prevents rot once planted.

> 💡 **Tip:** Smaller potatoes can be planted whole without cutting, which often reduces the risk of rot.

### Step 3: 3. Select Your Planting Location

Potatoes thrive in full sun, meaning at least 6-8 hours of direct sunlight per day. They prefer loose, well-draining soil that's slightly acidic (pH 5.0-6.0). Amend your garden bed or container with compost to improve fertility and drainage. Ensure the chosen spot has good air circulation.

> 💡 **Tip:** If planting in containers or grow bags, choose ones that are at least 15-20 gallons in size for optimal yield.

### Step 4: 4. Plant Your Seed Potatoes

Dig a trench or hole about 6-8 inches deep. Place your potato pieces cut-side down (or sprout-side up, if planting whole potatoes) approximately 10-12 inches apart. If planting in rows, space rows 2-3 feet apart. Cover the potato pieces with about 3-4 inches of soil, leaving a slight depression for future 'hilling'.

> 💡 **Tip:** Planting too deeply initially can delay sprouting; the shallow covering allows for easier hilling later.

### Step 5: 5. Water and Fertilize Consistently

Water thoroughly immediately after planting. Potatoes require consistent moisture, especially during flowering and tuber development, but avoid waterlogging. Once the plants are about 6 inches tall, you can apply a balanced granular fertilizer or side-dress with compost. Continue to water regularly, especially during dry spells.

> 💡 **Tip:** Mulching around the plants with straw or leaves can help retain soil moisture and suppress weeds.

### Step 6: 6. Hill Your Potatoes as They Grow

As the potato plants grow to about 8-10 inches tall, begin 'hilling' them. This involves mounding soil, compost, or straw around the base of the plant, covering the lower leaves and stems. Repeat this process every few weeks as the plants grow, leaving only the top 4-6 inches of foliage exposed. Hilling protects developing tubers from sunlight (preventing greening) and encourages more potatoes to form along the buried stems.

> 💡 **Tip:** Greening occurs when potatoes are exposed to light, producing solanine which is toxic. Hilling is crucial to prevent this.

### Step 7: 7. Harvest Your Potatoes

Potatoes are typically ready for harvest when the foliage begins to yellow and die back, usually 70-120 days after planting, depending on the variety. For 'new potatoes,' you can gently dig around the plant and remove a few small tubers while the plant is still green. For a main harvest, wait two weeks after the foliage has completely died back. Carefully dig up the tubers with a shovel or digging fork, being careful not to pierce them.

> 💡 **Tip:** Cure freshly harvested potatoes by leaving them in a cool, dark, humid place for 1-2 weeks before storing them to toughen their skins and improve storage life.


## Tips & Tricks

- Rotate your potato crops annually to different garden beds to help prevent soil-borne diseases and pest buildup.
- For a continuous harvest, plant a new batch of seed potatoes every 2-3 weeks in spring until early summer.
- Protect young potato plants from late spring frosts by covering them with row covers or blankets overnight.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not eat any potatoes that have turned green, as this indicates the presence of solanine, a toxic compound. Cut away any green parts if the potato is otherwise healthy, or discard entirely.
- ⚠️ Ensure your potato plants have good drainage; waterlogged soil can lead to fungal diseases and rot, significantly reducing your yield.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you grow potatoes from potatoes?**

To grow potatoes from potatoes, you start by 'chitting' existing potatoes to encourage sprouts. Then, cut larger potatoes into pieces with at least one eye, allow them to callus, and plant them in well-draining soil in a sunny location. Provide consistent water and 'hill' the plants as they grow.

**Can you grow potatoes at home without a large garden?**

Yes, you can easily grow potatoes at home in containers, grow bags, or even large bins. Ensure the container is at least 15-20 gallons to allow ample space for tuber development and good drainage. This method is perfect for small spaces like patios or balconies.

**Is the process different for how to grow red potatoes compared to other varieties?**

The general process for growing red potatoes is the same as for white, yellow, or purple varieties. The key differences lie in the specific variety's disease resistance, maturity time, and preferred growing conditions, but the planting, hilling, and harvesting steps remain consistent.

**What is the best way to grow potatoes indoors?**

To grow potatoes indoors, choose a large container (20+ gallons) and place it in a spot receiving at least 6-8 hours of direct sunlight, or supplement with grow lights. Use a well-draining potting mix, ensure good air circulation, and follow the same planting and hilling steps as for outdoor growing. Monitor moisture carefully, as indoor environments can dry out quickly.

**How long does it take to grow potatoes from potatoes?**

The time it takes to grow potatoes varies by variety, but generally, early varieties mature in 70-90 days, while main-crop varieties can take 90-120 days. You can harvest 'new potatoes' earlier, but for a full crop, wait until the foliage dies back.

**When is the best time to plant potatoes?**

The best time to plant potatoes is in early spring, about 2-4 weeks before your last anticipated frost date, as soon as the soil can be worked and its temperature reaches around 45°F (7°C). In warmer climates, you might also have a fall planting window.

**What are 'seed potatoes' and why are they important?**

'Seed potatoes' are simply potatoes specifically grown and certified to be disease-free. While you can grow from grocery store potatoes, certified seed potatoes reduce the risk of introducing diseases to your garden, leading to healthier plants and better yields. They ensure you start with vigorous, productive stock.
