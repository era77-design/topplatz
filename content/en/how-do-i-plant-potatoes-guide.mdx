To plant potatoes, first select certified seed potatoes and allow them to 'chit' or sprout in a cool, bright place. Prepare well-drained soil in a sunny location, then plant potato pieces with eyes facing up, about 4-6 inches deep. As the plants grow, consistently 'hill' them by mounding soil around the stems to protect developing tubers from sunlight.

## What You Need

- Certified seed potatoes
- Shovel or trowel
- Compost or aged manure
- Watering can or hose
- Gardening gloves (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Choose and Prepare Seed Potatoes

Select certified disease-free seed potatoes from a reputable supplier. Avoid using grocery store potatoes, as they may be treated to prevent sprouting and can carry diseases. For larger potatoes, cut them into pieces, ensuring each piece has at least 1-2 'eyes' (sprouts) and is roughly the size of a golf ball. Allow cut pieces to cure for 1-2 days in a dry, warm place to form a protective skin, which prevents rot after planting.

> 💡 **Tip:** 'Chitting' or pre-sprouting potatoes in a cool, bright area for a few weeks before planting can encourage earlier harvests.

### Step 2: 2. Select a Site and Prepare the Soil

Choose a location in your garden that receives full sun (at least 6-8 hours daily) and has well-drained soil. Potatoes prefer slightly acidic soil with a pH between 5.0 and 7.0. Loosen the soil to a depth of at least 10-12 inches using a shovel or garden fork. Incorporate a generous amount of compost or aged manure to enrich the soil and improve drainage, providing essential nutrients for healthy growth.

### Step 3: 3. Dig Trenches or Planting Holes

Dig trenches approximately 6-8 inches deep and 12-18 inches wide. If planting in rows, space the trenches about 2-3 feet apart. Alternatively, you can dig individual holes for each potato piece. Ensure the bottom of your trench or hole has loose soil for root development.

### Step 4: 4. Plant the Seed Potatoes

Place the prepared potato pieces, with the eyes facing upwards, at the bottom of your trench or holes. Space the pieces about 10-12 inches apart within the rows. If using individual holes, ensure adequate spacing between each plant. Gently cover the potato pieces with about 4-6 inches of loose soil.

> 💡 **Tip:** Water the newly planted area thoroughly after covering to help settle the soil and initiate growth.

### Step 5: 5. Water and Begin Hilling

Keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged, especially as the plants begin to grow. Once the potato plants emerge and reach about 6-8 inches tall, it's time to start hilling. Gently mound loose soil or compost around the base of the plants, covering most of the stem but leaving the top few inches of foliage exposed. This protects the developing tubers from sunlight, which can turn them green and inedible.

### Step 6: 6. Continue Hilling and Monitor Growth

Repeat the hilling process every 2-3 weeks, or whenever the plants grow another 6-8 inches. Continue to add soil or straw around the stems until the mound is about 10-12 inches high. This encourages more tubers to form along the buried stem. Monitor for pests and diseases, addressing any issues promptly to ensure a healthy crop.

### Step 7: 7. Harvest Your Potatoes

Potatoes are typically ready for harvest when the foliage begins to yellow and die back, usually 70-120 days after planting, depending on the variety. For 'new potatoes,' you can gently dig a few small tubers from the side of the mound before the plant fully dies back. For mature potatoes, wait until the foliage has completely died down, then carefully dig up the tubers with a garden fork, starting a foot or so away from the plant's base to avoid piercing them.

> 💡 **Tip:** Cure freshly dug potatoes in a cool, dark, well-ventilated area for 1-2 weeks before storing them to toughen their skins and improve storage life.


## Tips & Tricks

- Consider planting potatoes in grow bags or large containers for easy harvesting and space-saving.
- Rotate your potato planting locations each year to prevent the buildup of soil-borne diseases and pests.
- Mulch around your potato plants with straw or shredded leaves to help retain soil moisture, suppress weeds, and keep tubers cool.
- Plant early, mid-season, and late-season varieties to enjoy a continuous harvest throughout the growing season.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never eat green parts of potatoes, as they contain solanine, a toxic compound. If a potato has green spots, cut them off entirely before eating, or discard the potato.
- ⚠️ Do not use potatoes from the grocery store for planting, as they may be treated with sprout inhibitors and can introduce diseases to your garden.
- ⚠️ Always wear gloves when handling garden soil if you have open wounds, and wash hands thoroughly after gardening.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When is the best time to plant potatoes?**

The best time to plant potatoes is in early spring, about 2-4 weeks before the last expected frost date in your area. The soil temperature should ideally be around 45-50°F (7-10°C) for optimal growth. Planting too early in very cold, wet soil can lead to rot.

**Can I plant potatoes from the grocery store?**

It is generally not recommended to plant potatoes from the grocery store. These potatoes are often treated with sprout inhibitors and may carry diseases that could harm your garden. Always opt for certified disease-free seed potatoes from a reputable garden supplier.

**What is 'hilling' and why is it important?**

Hilling is the process of mounding soil or compost around the base of the potato plants as they grow. This practice is crucial because it protects the developing tubers from sunlight, which can turn them green and toxic, and encourages more potatoes to form along the buried stem.

**How much water do potatoes need?**

Potatoes need consistent moisture, especially during flowering and tuber development. Aim for about 1-2 inches of water per week, either from rain or irrigation. Avoid overwatering, which can lead to rot, and ensure the soil is well-drained.

**How long does it take for potatoes to grow?**

The time it takes for potatoes to grow varies by variety, but generally ranges from 70 to 120 days from planting to harvest. Early varieties mature faster, while late-season varieties take longer but often produce larger yields. You can harvest 'new potatoes' earlier for a tender treat.

**Can I grow potatoes in containers?**

Yes, potatoes grow very well in containers, grow bags, or even old tires. Ensure your container is large enough (at least 15-gallon capacity per plant) and has good drainage. Container gardening makes harvesting incredibly easy, as you can just tip out the contents.

**What should I do if my potatoes turn green?**

Green potatoes contain solanine, a natural toxin. If you find green spots on your harvested potatoes, cut off all green parts and discard them, or throw away the entire potato if the greening is extensive. Hilling properly during growth helps prevent this by keeping tubers covered.
