Changing a bathtub faucet can refresh your bathroom's look and improve water flow. This DIY guide provides clear instructions to help you replace your old faucet with a new one, ensuring a functional and aesthetically pleasing update to your tub.

## What You Need

- New bathtub faucet assembly
- Adjustable wrench
- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Plumber's putty or silicone sealant
- Pliers
- Utility knife or caulk remover tool
- Cleaning supplies (rag, mild cleaner)
- Bucket or towel
- Thread seal tape (Teflon tape)
- Safety glasses
- Flashlight (for dark spaces)
- Allen wrench set (if needed for set screws)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, locate the main water shut-off valve for your house or the specific bathroom. Turn the valve clockwise until the water supply is completely off. Once the water is off, open the old faucet to drain any remaining water from the pipes, reducing mess during the replacement.

> 💡 **Tip:** Test the water flow at another faucet in the house to confirm the main supply is indeed off.

### Step 2: Remove Old Faucet Handles and Trim

Depending on your faucet type, you'll need to remove the handles first. Look for screws under decorative caps or small set screws on the side of the handles. Use a screwdriver or appropriate Allen wrench to loosen and remove these, then pull off the handles. Next, carefully pry off any escutcheon plates or trim surrounding the handles and spout, often held by caulk or small screws.

> 💡 **Tip:** Use a utility knife to score around old caulk before prying to avoid damaging wall tiles.

### Step 3: Detach the Faucet Spout

Most bathtub spouts are either threaded onto a pipe coming from the wall or secured with a set screw underneath. If threaded, twist the spout counter-clockwise to unscrew it. If it has a set screw, loosen it with an Allen wrench or screwdriver and then pull the spout off. Be prepared for a small amount of residual water to drip out.

> 💡 **Tip:** Place a towel or bucket underneath the spout to catch any drips.

### Step 4: Access and Disconnect the Valve Body

For a full faucet replacement, you'll need to access the valve body behind the wall, usually through an access panel in an adjacent room or by carefully opening the wall. Once exposed, use an adjustable wrench to carefully loosen the nuts connecting the hot and cold water supply lines to the old valve body. If your old faucet was simply [leaking](/en/how-to-fix-leaky-bathtub-faucet), you might have considered a repair. However, replacing the entire assembly ensures all new components.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take photos of the existing plumbing connections before disconnecting to aid in reassembly.

### Step 5: Install the New Faucet Valve Body and Trim

Mount the new valve body into the wall opening, ensuring it's level and secure according to the manufacturer's instructions. Connect the hot and cold water supply lines to the appropriate inlets on the new valve, using thread seal tape on the threaded connections to prevent leaks. Secure the new trim plate and handles, ensuring they are aligned and tighten all screws.

> 💡 **Tip:** Apply plumber's putty or silicone sealant around the edges of the new trim plate where it meets the wall to create a watertight seal.

### Step 6: Attach the New Faucet Spout

If your new spout is threaded, apply thread seal tape to the pipe threads and screw the spout on clockwise until snug, ensuring it points downwards. If it's a set-screw type, slide it onto the pipe and tighten the set screw securely from underneath. Make sure the spout is firmly attached and doesn't wobble.

> 💡 **Tip:** Avoid over-tightening threaded spouts, as this can damage the pipe or the spout itself.

### Step 7: Restore Water Supply and Check for Leaks

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Inspect all new connections for any signs of leaks. Turn on the new faucet to test both hot and cold water flow and ensure proper operation. Check for drips around the handles and spout. If you find a leak, turn off the water, tighten the connection, and recheck.

> 💡 **Tip:** Keep a dry paper towel handy to easily spot any small leaks around connections.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always read the instructions provided with your new faucet, as installation methods can vary slightly between models.
- Keep a small bucket and towels nearby to catch any residual water during the removal process.
- If you encounter persistent leaks after installation, try re-applying thread seal tape or plumber's putty and ensuring all connections are snug.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the main water supply before starting any plumbing work to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Wear safety glasses to protect your eyes from debris or splashes.
- ⚠️ If you're uncomfortable accessing plumbing behind walls or dealing with complex connections, consider hiring a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I know what type of bathtub faucet I have?**

Bathtub faucets typically come in single-handle, two-handle, or three-handle configurations. The spout can be diverter or non-diverter, and the mounting style can be wall-mounted, deck-mounted, or Roman tub style. Visually inspect your current faucet to determine its type.

**Do I need special tools to change a bathtub faucet?**

While most common tools like screwdrivers and adjustable wrenches are needed, you might also require specialized tools like a basin wrench for tight spaces or an Allen wrench for specific set screws. Always check your new faucet's instructions for any unique tool requirements.

**Can I replace a two-handle faucet with a single-handle one?**

Yes, but it's a more complex job. It usually requires modifying the existing plumbing behind the wall to accommodate the new single-handle valve body and potentially patching wall openings. This often necessitates professional help.

**What should I do if my new faucet leaks after installation?**

First, turn off the water supply immediately. Then, re-examine all connections, ensuring they are tight and that thread seal tape or plumber's putty was applied correctly. Sometimes, simply tightening a connection or reapplying sealant can resolve the issue.

**How much does it cost to change a bathtub faucet?**

The cost varies widely depending on whether you do it yourself or hire a professional, and the price of the new faucet itself. DIY replacement can cost anywhere from $50 for a basic faucet to several hundred for high-end models, plus minimal tool costs.

**Is it better to repair or replace a leaky bathtub faucet?**

If the leak is minor and from a replaceable part like a washer or cartridge, repairing it can be cost-effective. However, if the faucet is old, corroded, or has multiple issues, replacement is often the better long-term solution for reliability and aesthetics. For repair details, see our guide on [How to Fix a Leaky Bathtub Faucet: DIY Repair Guide](/en/how-to-fix-leaky-bathtub-faucet).

**How long does a bathtub faucet typically last?**

The lifespan of a bathtub faucet can range from 10 to 20 years, depending on its quality, usage frequency, and water conditions. Regular maintenance and prompt repair of minor issues can help extend its life, but eventually, replacement becomes necessary.
