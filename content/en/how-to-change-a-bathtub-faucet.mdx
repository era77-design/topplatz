Changing a bathtub faucet can update your bathroom's look and fix persistent leaks. This guide provides practical steps for DIY replacement, saving you the cost of a professional plumber. With the right tools and a little patience, you can successfully install a new faucet.

## What You Need

- New bathtub faucet assembly
- Adjustable wrench
- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Plumber's tape
- Utility knife
- Caulk gun and silicone caulk
- Bucket and rags
- Penetrating oil (optional)
- Safety glasses

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Shut Off Water Supply and Gather Tools

Before starting, locate the main water shut-off valve for your home or the specific bathroom and turn off the water supply completely. This prevents accidental flooding during the replacement process. Gather all your necessary tools and the new faucet assembly to ensure everything is within reach.

> 💡 **Tip:** Open a nearby faucet to drain any residual water from the pipes after shutting off the main supply. Have a bucket and rags ready to catch any drips.

### Step 2: 2. Remove Old Faucet Handles and Trim

Depending on your faucet type, you'll typically need to remove decorative caps, screws, and then the handles. Use a screwdriver to remove any visible screws holding the handles in place. Carefully pry off the trim plate, which might be sealed with caulk; a utility knife can help break this seal if necessary. Be gentle to avoid damaging the tub surface.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take photos of the existing setup before disassembly to help with reassembly or troubleshooting later.

### Step 3: 3. Disconnect the Old Faucet Body

Access the back of the faucet from behind the wall (if you have an access panel) or from beneath the tub. Use an adjustable wrench to loosen the nuts securing the hot and cold water supply lines to the faucet body. Disconnect the spout assembly from its connection point. Be prepared for some water to drip out, even after draining the lines.

> 💡 **Tip:** If nuts are stubborn, apply penetrating oil and let it sit for a few minutes before trying to loosen them again. Always turn counter-clockwise to loosen.

### Step 4: 4. Clean the Mounting Surface

Once the old faucet is completely removed, thoroughly clean the area where it was mounted on the tub or wall. Scrape off any old caulk, dirt, mineral deposits, or corrosion from the surface. A clean and smooth mounting surface is crucial for creating a watertight seal with the new faucet and preventing future leaks.

> 💡 **Tip:** Use a non-abrasive cleaner to remove any residue without damaging the tub's finish.

### Step 5: 5. Install the New Faucet Body

Carefully follow the manufacturer's instructions for your specific new faucet model. Apply plumber's tape clockwise to the threaded pipes where the water supply lines will connect. Insert the new faucet body into the mounting holes and secure it from behind with the provided nuts and washers. Hand-tighten first, then use a wrench for a snug fit, being careful not to overtighten and strip the threads.

> 💡 **Tip:** Ensure the faucet is perfectly straight and centered before tightening the mounting nuts completely.

### Step 6: 6. Reconnect Supply Lines and Install Trim

Connect the hot and cold water supply lines to the new faucet body, tightening them securely with your adjustable wrench. Install the new trim plate and handles according to the manufacturer's instructions. Apply a continuous bead of silicone caulk around the base of the trim plate and any other necessary areas for a watertight seal, smoothing it out with your finger.

> 💡 **Tip:** Allow the caulk to cure for the recommended time (usually 24 hours) before exposing it to water for best results.

### Step 7: 7. Restore Water and Check for Leaks

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Open the new faucet to full hot and cold to purge any air from the lines and check for proper operation. Carefully inspect all connections and the base of the faucet for any signs of dripping or leakage. If you notice any leaks, tighten the connections slightly until the leak stops. For persistent drips, you may need to consult our guides on [How to Fix a Leaky Bathtub Faucet: DIY Guide](/en/how-to-fix-a-leaky-bathtub-faucet) or [How to Fix a Leaky Faucet DIY Guide](/en/how-to-fix-leaky-faucet-diy).

> 💡 **Tip:** Place dry paper towels around the connections and base of the faucet. Any moisture will be easily visible, helping you pinpoint leaks.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always read the manufacturer's instructions that come with your new faucet, as installation steps can vary slightly by model.
- Keep a small bucket or towel handy under the connections when working to catch any unexpected water drips.
- Consider upgrading to a drip-free ceramic disc faucet for greater durability and fewer future maintenance issues.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always shut off the main water supply before beginning any plumbing work to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Avoid overtightening nuts and connections, as this can strip threads or crack pipes and fixtures.
- ⚠️ Wear safety glasses to protect your eyes from debris or splashes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When should I replace my bathtub faucet instead of repairing it?**

You should consider replacement if your faucet is old, extensively corroded, or if repairs are consistently failing. If parts are difficult to find or the cost of repair approaches the cost of a new unit, replacement is often the more practical solution.

**What are the common types of bathtub faucets?**

Common types include single-handle faucets, two-handle faucets (separate hot and cold), and Roman tub faucets, which are often deck-mounted with a high-arc spout. The type you choose depends on your existing setup and aesthetic preference.

**Do I need a plumber to change a bathtub faucet?**

While a plumber can certainly do the job, changing a bathtub faucet is a manageable DIY project for homeowners with basic plumbing knowledge and the right tools. Following a detailed guide like this can help you complete the task successfully.

**How much does it cost to change a bathtub faucet?**

The cost largely depends on the price of the new faucet itself, which can range from $50 for basic models to several hundred dollars for high-end or designer options. If you do it yourself, you save on labor costs, typically $150-$300 for professional installation.

**What is plumber's tape used for?**

Plumber's tape, also known as PTFE tape or Teflon tape, is used on threaded pipe connections to create a watertight seal and prevent leaks. It lubricates the threads, making it easier to tighten and loosen pipes, and fills tiny gaps for a secure connection.

**How do I choose the right size replacement faucet?**

Measure the distance between the existing faucet's hot and cold water inlets (the 'spread') and the number of holes in your tub or deck. Most faucets come in standard sizes, but verifying these measurements ensures compatibility with your current setup.

**What if my new faucet leaks after installation?**

If your new faucet leaks, first recheck all connections to ensure they are snug but not overtightened. Ensure plumber's tape was applied correctly and generously. Sometimes, disassembling and reassembling a specific connection can resolve a persistent drip. If the leak persists, the faucet itself might be defective, or a washer might be improperly seated.
