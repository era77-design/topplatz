Changing a bathtub faucet typically involves shutting off the water, carefully removing the old fixture, and then installing the new one according to the manufacturer's instructions. This DIY project can refresh your bathroom's look and improve water flow. Sometimes a replacement is necessary if a repair isn't sufficient, or if you simply want to update your bathroom's aesthetic.

## What You Need

- New bathtub faucet assembly
- Adjustable wrench
- Screwdriver set (Phillips and flathead)
- Allen wrench set (often included with new faucet)
- Plumber's putty or thread seal tape
- Utility knife
- Rags or towels
- Bucket
- Silicone caulk (bathroom-grade)
- Safety glasses

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Shut Off Water and Prepare Workspace

Before you begin, turn off the main water supply to your home. Open the existing bathtub faucet to drain any remaining water from the pipes and release pressure. Place towels or a bucket below the faucet to catch any residual drips and protect your tub. Gather all your necessary tools and the new faucet assembly, ensuring you have adequate lighting and space to work comfortably.

### Step 2: 2. Remove Faucet Handles or Knobs

Carefully pry off any decorative caps on the faucet handles, which usually conceal a screw underneath. Use a Phillips or flathead screwdriver to remove these screws, then gently pull or wiggle the handles off. If your faucet has a single lever, look for a small set screw on the side or underneath the handle; loosen or remove this screw with an Allen wrench to detach the handle.

### Step 3: 3. Disconnect the Old Faucet Spout

Most bathtub spouts are either threaded onto a pipe extending from the wall or secured with a set screw on the underside. For threaded spouts, twist counter-clockwise with an adjustable wrench or by hand to unscrew it. If it has a set screw, use an Allen wrench or screwdriver to loosen it, then pull the spout straight off the pipe. Be prepared for a small amount of water to drip out.

### Step 4: 4. Clean the Area and Install New Spout

Thoroughly clean the area around the pipe, removing any old caulk, debris, or mineral buildup with a utility knife and a rag. Apply plumber's putty or thread seal tape to the threads of the new spout or the pipe, as recommended by the manufacturer's instructions. Carefully thread the new spout onto the pipe by turning it clockwise until snug, or slide it onto the pipe and tighten its set screw. Ensure it's level and facing the correct direction.

### Step 5: 5. Install New Faucet Handles or Trim

Follow the new faucet's specific instructions to install the new handles, cartridges, or trim. This typically involves placing new cartridges into the valve body openings, securing them with nuts or clips, and then attaching the new handles with screws. Ensure all components are securely fastened and aligned properly. Apply a thin bead of silicone caulk around the base of any trim plates that meet the wall for a watertight seal.

### Step 6: 6. Restore Water and Test for Leaks

Once all components are securely installed, slowly turn the main water supply back on. Carefully check all new connections for any signs of leaks. Turn on both the hot and cold water at the new faucet to ensure proper operation and to flush out any air in the lines. Continue to monitor for leaks around the spout, handles, and any newly installed trim for several minutes.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always read the installation instructions provided with your new faucet, as designs can vary.
- Keep a bucket and plenty of old towels handy throughout the process to manage any water spills.
- When applying plumber's putty or thread seal tape, use it sparingly but ensure a complete seal for connections.
- Consider upgrading to a water-saving faucet model to conserve water and reduce utility bills.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always shut off the main water supply before beginning any plumbing work to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten connections, as this can strip threads or crack pipes and fixtures.
- ⚠️ Be cautious when working with old plumbing, as pipes can be fragile and prone to breaking.
- ⚠️ Wear safety glasses to protect your eyes from debris or splashes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does it take to change a bathtub faucet?**

For a DIY enthusiast with basic tools, changing a bathtub faucet typically takes 1 to 2 hours. This can vary based on your experience level and any unforeseen issues with existing plumbing.

**What tools do I need to change a bathtub faucet?**

You'll generally need an adjustable wrench, screwdrivers (Phillips and flathead), an Allen wrench set, plumber's putty or thread seal tape, and a utility knife. Towels and a bucket are also essential for cleanup.

**Do I need to turn off the main water supply?**

Yes, it is crucial to turn off the main water supply to your home before starting this project. This prevents flooding and allows you to work safely without water pressure.

**Can I replace just the spout or handles?**

Yes, in many cases, you can replace individual components like the spout or handles if they are compatible with your existing valve body. This can be a simpler solution if the internal valve is still in good condition.

**Should I repair or replace my leaky bathtub faucet?**

If your faucet is old, severely damaged, or beyond simple repair, replacement is often the best option. For minor issues like drips, you might be able to fix it first. See our guide on [How to Fix a Leaky Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Repair Guide](/en/how-to-fix-a-leaky-bathtub-faucet) for repair tips.

**What kind of caulk should I use?**

Use a silicone-based caulk designed for bathrooms and wet environments. This type of caulk provides a durable, waterproof seal and resists mold and mildew, ensuring longevity.

**When should I call a professional plumber?**

If you encounter significant plumbing issues like corroded pipes, difficulty accessing the valve, or if you're uncomfortable with any step, it's best to call a professional. They have the expertise and specialized tools to handle complex situations safely.
