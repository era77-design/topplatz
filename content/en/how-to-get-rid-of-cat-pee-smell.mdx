To effectively get rid of cat pee smell, immediately clean the affected area using an enzymatic cleaner specifically designed for pet odors. Blot the urine, saturate with the cleaner, and allow it to air dry, repeating if necessary. This process breaks down the odor-causing uric acid crystals at their source.

## What You Need

- Paper towels or old rags
- Rubber gloves
- Enzymatic pet odor cleaner
- White vinegar (optional, for hard surfaces)
- Baking soda (optional, for deodorizing)
- Spray bottle
- Urine-detecting UV light (optional)
- Stiff brush (for carpets, optional)
- Plastic sheeting or foil (to prevent re-soiling)

## Steps

### Step 1: Locate All Affected Areas

Even dried urine can emit a strong odor. If the accident isn't fresh, use a UV (blacklight) flashlight in a darkened room to pinpoint dried urine stains, which often glow yellowish-green. This is crucial as dried urine can be invisible to the naked eye but still emit strong odors, and finding all spots is key to completely [How to Get Rid of Cat Pee Smell Permanently & Effectively](/en/how-to-get-rid-of-cat-pee-smell).

### Step 2: Blot Fresh Urine Immediately

For fresh accidents, put on rubber gloves and use paper towels or old rags to blot up as much urine as possible. Press firmly and replace towels until no more liquid transfers. Avoid rubbing, as this can spread the urine and push it deeper into fibers, making it harder to remove.

### Step 3: Rinse and Blot (if applicable)

For surfaces that can tolerate water, gently rinse the area with a small amount of cool water, then blot again to dilute and remove more urine. This step helps to flush out some of the odor-causing compounds before applying a specialized cleaner. Ensure you blot thoroughly to remove excess moisture.

### Step 4: Apply Enzymatic Cleaner Generously

Saturate the soiled area thoroughly with an enzymatic pet odor cleaner, following the product's instructions. These cleaners contain enzymes that break down the uric acid crystals in cat urine, which are responsible for the persistent smell. Ensure the cleaner penetrates as deeply as the urine did, especially in carpets or upholstery, for [How to Get Rid of Cat Pee Smell: Quick & Effective Solutions](/en/how-to-remove-cat-pee-smell-quickly).

### Step 5: Allow to Air Dry Completely

Do not scrub or blot up the enzymatic cleaner immediately after application. Let the area air dry completely, which can take several hours or even a day, as the enzymes continue to work as long as the area remains damp. Once dry, if an odor persists, repeat the application process.

### Step 6: Deodorize with Baking Soda (Optional)

Once the area is completely dry, if a faint odor remains, sprinkle a generous amount of baking soda over the spot. Let it sit for several hours or overnight to absorb any residual odors. Afterward, simply vacuum up the baking soda, which will leave the area smelling fresher.

### Step 7: Address Hard Surfaces and Walls

For hard surfaces like tile, wood, or concrete, clean with an enzymatic cleaner, then wipe down with a solution of 1 part white vinegar to 1 part water. For walls, ensure they are thoroughly cleaned and potentially sealed and repainted with an odor-blocking primer if the smell has deeply permeated the surface.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always test any cleaning solution on an inconspicuous area first to check for colorfastness or damage.
- Prevent re-soiling by placing foil or plastic sheeting over the cleaned area for a few days, or use pet deterrents.
- Consider professional cleaning for severe or widespread urine contamination in carpets or upholstery.
- Ensure proper ventilation when cleaning by opening windows or using fans to air out the space.
- Address the root cause of your cat's inappropriate urination with your veterinarian to prevent future accidents.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never use ammonia-based cleaners, as their scent can mimic urine and attract your cat back to the same spot.
- ⚠️ Avoid steam cleaners on cat urine, as the heat can permanently bond the odor to fibers, making it much harder to remove.
- ⚠️ Always wear rubber gloves when handling urine to protect yourself from bacteria and ensure hygiene.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is cat pee smell so hard to get rid of?**

Cat urine contains uric acid crystals that are insoluble in water and bind tightly to surfaces. These crystals reactivate with moisture, continuously releasing a strong, pungent odor that is difficult to remove with standard cleaners.

**What is the best product for cat pee smell?**

Enzymatic cleaners are generally considered the most effective. They contain beneficial bacteria that produce enzymes to break down the uric acid and other components of cat urine, eliminating the odor at its source rather than just masking it.

**Can I use homemade remedies like vinegar or baking soda?**

White vinegar and baking soda can help neutralize and absorb some odors, especially for minor or fresh stains. However, for deep-seated or old cat urine smells, an enzymatic cleaner is usually necessary for complete and permanent removal.

**How do I get cat pee smell out of a couch or mattress?**

For couches and mattresses, it's crucial to saturate the affected area with an enzymatic cleaner, ensuring it penetrates as deeply as the urine. Blotting, repeating the process, and allowing it to air dry completely are key steps. You may need to repeat the process multiple times for full efficacy.

**Does cat pee smell ever truly go away?**

Yes, with the correct cleaning methods and products, especially enzymatic cleaners, cat pee smell can be completely eliminated. The key is to break down the uric acid crystals rather than just masking the odor, ensuring thorough treatment of all affected areas.

**How do I find old, dried cat urine stains?**

The most effective way to find old, dried cat urine stains is to use a UV (blacklight) flashlight in a darkened room. Urine stains will fluoresce a yellowish-green under the UV light, making them visible for targeted treatment.

**What if the smell persists after cleaning?**

If the smell persists, it likely means the urine has penetrated deeper or wider than initially treated, or not all uric acid crystals were broken down. Re-treat the area thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner, ensuring deep saturation, and allow ample drying time. Consider using a UV light to confirm all areas are found and treated.
