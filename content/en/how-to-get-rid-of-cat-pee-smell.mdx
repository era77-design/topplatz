To get rid of cat pee smell, act quickly by blotting up fresh urine, then saturate the area with an enzymatic cleaner. Allow the cleaner to air dry completely, and repeat the process if necessary to neutralize the odor at its source.

## What You Need

- Paper towels or old rags
- Rubber gloves
- Enzymatic cleaner specifically for pet odors
- White vinegar (optional)
- Baking soda (optional)
- Stiff brush (for carpets)
- Plastic wrap or foil (optional)
- UV blacklight (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Locate the Source

The first and most crucial step is to pinpoint exactly where the cat has urinated. Fresh stains are easier to treat, but older, dried stains can be invisible to the naked eye. A UV blacklight can be incredibly helpful for revealing hidden urine spots, especially on carpets and upholstery. Darken the room and scan surfaces to find glowing areas.

> 💡 **Tip:** Check less obvious spots like walls, baseboards, and under furniture.

### Step 2: Blot Up Fresh Urine Immediately

If the urine is fresh, act fast! Put on rubber gloves and use a stack of paper towels or a clean, absorbent cloth to blot the area. Press down firmly to absorb as much liquid as possible. Avoid rubbing, as this can spread the urine and push it deeper into fibers. Replace saturated towels with fresh ones until no more moisture can be absorbed.

### Step 3: Pre-Treat with Vinegar (Optional)

For carpets and upholstery, a solution of equal parts white vinegar and water can help break down some urine components before applying an enzymatic cleaner. Spray the solution onto the blotted area, let it sit for 10-15 minutes, then blot again thoroughly. Test the vinegar solution on an inconspicuous area first to ensure it doesn't cause discoloration.

> 💡 **Tip:** Vinegar is excellent for neutralizing ammonia, but it's not a substitute for enzymatic cleaners.

### Step 4: Apply an Enzymatic Cleaner

This is the most critical step for eliminating cat pee smell permanently. Enzymatic cleaners contain beneficial bacteria that 'eat' the odor-causing proteins in cat urine. Saturate the stained area thoroughly with the enzymatic cleaner, ensuring it reaches as deep as the urine penetrated. Follow the product instructions carefully regarding application and dwell time. For a comprehensive approach, refer to this [How to Get Rid of Cat Pee Smell: A Step-by-Step Guide](/en/how-to-get-rid-of-cat-pee-smell-guide).

> 💡 **Tip:** Do not use other cleaners or chemicals before or immediately after applying the enzymatic cleaner, as they can deactivate the enzymes.

### Step 5: Allow to Air Dry Completely

After applying the enzymatic cleaner, it's vital to let the area air dry completely. This allows the enzymes sufficient time to work their magic and break down all the uric acid crystals. This process can take several hours to a few days, depending on the humidity and the saturation level. You can place a fan nearby to speed up drying.

> 💡 **Tip:** Covering the damp area with plastic wrap or aluminum foil can deter your cat from re-soiling the spot while it dries.

### Step 6: Repeat if Necessary & Absorb Odors

For stubborn or old stains, you might need to repeat the enzymatic cleaning process multiple times. Once the area is completely dry, sprinkle a generous amount of baking soda over it. Let the baking soda sit for several hours or overnight to absorb any residual odors, then vacuum it up. > 💡 **Tip:** If the smell persists after multiple treatments, the urine may have seeped into subflooring or padding, requiring professional attention or removal.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always test cleaning solutions on an inconspicuous area first to prevent damage or discoloration.
- Ventilate the area well by opening windows or using fans during and after cleaning.
- Consider professional steam cleaning for heavily soiled carpets or upholstery, but ensure they use an enzymatic pre-treatment.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never use ammonia-based cleaners, as their smell can attract your cat to pee in the same spot again.
- ⚠️ Avoid steam cleaning fresh urine stains before enzymatic treatment, as the heat can set the stain and odor.
- ⚠️ Wear gloves and ensure good ventilation when using cleaning products.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is cat pee smell so hard to remove?**

Cat urine contains uric acid crystals, which are not water-soluble and reactivate with moisture, releasing a strong, pungent odor. It also contains pheromones that attract cats to re-mark the same spot.

**What is the best cleaner for cat pee?**

Enzymatic cleaners are the most effective because they contain specialized enzymes that break down the uric acid crystals and other odor-causing compounds at a molecular level, truly eliminating the smell rather than just masking it.

**Can I use bleach to clean cat urine?**

No, you should never use bleach to clean cat urine. Bleach can react with the ammonia in urine to create dangerous fumes. It also doesn't effectively remove the uric acid crystals, and its strong scent can attract your cat back to the spot.

**How do I get rid of old, dried cat pee smell?**

For old, dried stains, use a UV blacklight to locate them. Then, thoroughly saturate the area with an enzymatic cleaner, ensuring it penetrates as deep as the urine. Allow it to air dry completely, and repeat the process multiple times if needed.

**What if the smell is in my subfloor or wall?**

If the smell has seeped into porous materials like subflooring, drywall, or concrete, the affected material may need to be sealed with an odor-blocking primer or even replaced. For severe cases, professional remediation might be necessary.

**How can I prevent my cat from peeing outside the litter box?**

Ensure your cat has a clean litter box (scoop daily), enough litter boxes (one per cat plus one extra), and prefers the type of litter used. Stress, medical issues, or changes in the home can also cause inappropriate urination, so consult a vet if the problem persists.

**Does baking soda really work for cat pee smell?**

Baking soda can absorb some surface odors and moisture, making it a good follow-up treatment after enzymatic cleaning. However, it does not break down the uric acid crystals that cause the stubborn cat pee smell, so it's not a standalone solution.
