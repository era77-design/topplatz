To get blood out of clothes, act quickly by rinsing the stain with cold water as soon as possible. For fresh stains, gently dab with a cloth soaked in hydrogen peroxide or an enzyme cleaner. For dried stains, pre-soak in cold water with an enzymatic cleaner before washing.

## What You Need

- Cold water
- Clean cloth or paper towels
- Hydrogen peroxide (3%)
- Laundry detergent (enzyme-based if possible)
- Salt
- Baking soda (optional)
- Soft-bristled brush (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Act Fast & Rinse with Cold Water

As soon as you notice a blood stain, immediately rinse the affected area under cold running water from the back of the fabric. This helps push the blood out of the fibers rather than deeper into them. Avoid hot water, as it can set the stain permanently.

> 💡 **Tip:** Keep the water pressure steady but not too strong to avoid spreading the stain.

### Step 2: Blot with Hydrogen Peroxide (for fresh stains)

For fresh blood stains on white or colorfast fabrics, apply a small amount of 3% hydrogen peroxide directly to the stain using a clean cloth or cotton ball. The peroxide will bubble, breaking down the blood. Blot gently, do not rub. Rinse thoroughly with cold water and repeat if necessary until the stain is gone.

> 💡 **Tip:** Always test hydrogen peroxide on an inconspicuous area first, especially on colored garments, as it can cause discoloration.

### Step 3: Apply Salt Paste (alternative for fresh stains)

If hydrogen peroxide isn't available or suitable, create a thick paste using cold water and table salt. Apply this paste directly onto the fresh blood stain and let it sit for 10-20 minutes. The salt helps to draw the blood out of the fabric. Gently rub the paste into the stain, then rinse thoroughly with cold water.

> 💡 **Tip:** This method is particularly effective for delicate fabrics or when you're on the go.

### Step 4: Soak in Enzyme Cleaner (for dried or stubborn stains)

For dried blood stains or more stubborn fresh ones, pre-soak the garment in cold water with an enzyme-based laundry detergent or a dedicated enzyme pre-soak product. Follow the product instructions for dilution and soaking time, which can range from 30 minutes to several hours, or even overnight. Enzymes break down protein-based stains like blood effectively. Ensure the entire stained area is submerged.

> 💡 **Tip:** For extremely dried-on stains, you might need to gently scrape off excess dried blood with a dull knife before soaking.

### Step 5: Wash as Usual (Cold Cycle)

After treating the stain and ensuring it's mostly or entirely gone, wash the garment in your washing machine on a cold water cycle. Use your regular laundry detergent. Double-check the care label for specific washing instructions for the fabric.

> 💡 **Tip:** Do not put the garment in the dryer until you are absolutely certain the stain is completely removed. Heat will set any remaining blood stain, making it much harder, if not impossible, to remove later.

### Step 6: Air Dry and Re-evaluate

Once washed, air dry the garment. This allows you to visually inspect the area where the stain was without the risk of heat setting it. If any trace of the blood stain remains, repeat the treatment steps from the beginning. Continue treating and air drying until the stain is no longer visible. Only then should you machine dry the garment if appropriate for the fabric.

> 💡 **Tip:** Be patient! Sometimes multiple treatments are needed for stubborn or dried stains.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always use cold water for blood stains; hot water will set them permanently.
- Test any cleaning product on an inconspicuous area of the fabric first to check for colorfastness and prevent damage.
- For delicate fabrics like silk or wool, opt for gentler methods like salt paste or specialized fabric cleaners, and avoid hydrogen peroxide unless specifically approved for the fabric.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not use hydrogen peroxide on delicate fabrics, dark colors, or wool/silk without testing first, as it can bleach or damage the material.
- ⚠️ Never put a blood-stained item in a hot dryer until the stain is completely removed, as heat will permanently set the stain.
- ⚠️ Avoid rubbing the stain vigorously, as this can spread it and push it deeper into the fabric fibers, making it harder to remove.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why should I only use cold water for blood stains?**

Hot water causes the proteins in blood to coagulate and bond with the fabric fibers, effectively 'cooking' the stain into the material. Cold water, however, keeps the proteins loose, making them much easier to flush out and remove from the fabric.

**Can dried blood stains be removed?**

Yes, dried blood stains can often be removed, though they might require more effort and patience. Pre-soaking in cold water with an enzyme-based cleaner or a paste of salt and water is usually the most effective first step for dried stains before further treatment.

**Is hydrogen peroxide safe for all fabrics?**

Hydrogen peroxide is generally safe for white and colorfast cottons, but it can bleach or damage delicate fabrics, dark colors, wool, and silk. Always perform a patch test on an an inconspicuous area first to prevent accidental discoloration or damage.

**What if I don't have hydrogen peroxide?**

If hydrogen peroxide isn't available, you can use a paste of salt and cold water, an enzyme-based laundry detergent, or even a diluted solution of white vinegar as alternatives. The key is to act quickly and always use cold water for rinsing and washing.

**How do enzyme cleaners work on blood?**

Enzyme cleaners contain special enzymes that are designed to break down protein-based stains like blood, grass, and food. They essentially digest the stain molecules, breaking them into smaller, more soluble components that are easier to wash away from the fabric fibers.

**Can I use bleach on blood stains?**

While bleach can remove stains, it's generally not recommended for blood as it can set the protein stain if not used correctly, and it can also damage or discolor many fabrics. It's best to try gentler methods like cold water, hydrogen peroxide, or enzyme cleaners first.

**What if the blood stain is on a non-washable item?**

For non-washable items, gently blot the stain with a clean cloth dampened with cold water and a tiny bit of mild soap, being careful not to over-wet the fabric. For delicate or valuable items, it's often best to consult a professional dry cleaner who specializes in stain removal to avoid damage.
