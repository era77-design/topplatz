To get rid of carpet beetles, thoroughly vacuum all carpets, rugs, upholstery, and cracks, then wash all infested fabrics in hot water. Clean hard surfaces with soap and water, and consider using diatomaceous earth or targeted insecticides in hidden areas, always focusing on eliminating their food sources and entry points.

## What You Need

- Vacuum cleaner with attachments
- Laundry detergent
- Hot water
- All-purpose cleaner
- Microfiber cloths or sponges
- Trash bags
- Gloves
- Diatomaceous earth (food-grade, optional)
- Insecticide spray (pyrethrin-based, optional, read labels carefully)
- Caulk or sealant (for cracks)

## Steps

### Step 1: Identify and Inspect the Infestation

Begin by confirming you have carpet beetles, not another pest. Look for tiny, oval-shaped adult beetles near windows or larvae (fuzzy, bristly, carrot-shaped) in dark areas. Carefully inspect carpets, rugs, upholstered furniture, clothing, pantry items, and even pet food to pinpoint the extent and source of the infestation. Knowing their hiding spots will guide your cleaning efforts.

> 💡 **Tip:** Check forgotten corners, under furniture, and in air vents for hidden larvae.

### Step 2: Thoroughly Vacuum All Affected Areas

Use a powerful vacuum cleaner with appropriate attachments to deep clean all carpets, rugs, and upholstery. Pay special attention to edges, seams, cracks, and areas under furniture where debris and larvae accumulate. Immediately empty the vacuum bag or canister into a sealed trash bag and dispose of it outdoors to prevent re-infestation.

> 💡 **Tip:** Vacuum daily for a week in heavily infested areas to effectively remove eggs and larvae.

### Step 3: Launder or Clean Infested Fabrics

Collect all washable infested items, such as clothing, bedding, curtains, and small rugs. Wash them in hot water with laundry detergent, as high temperatures kill beetles and their larvae. For non-washable items like delicate fabrics or large rugs, consider professional dry cleaning or steam cleaning services that can effectively eliminate pests.

> 💡 **Tip:** Dry cleaning is an effective option for items that cannot withstand hot water washing, ensuring pest eradication without damage.

### Step 4: Clean Hard Surfaces and Storage Areas

Wipe down all hard surfaces, including shelves, drawers, baseboards, and floors, using an all-purpose cleaner or soap and water. Empty and clean out closets, cabinets, and pantry areas, removing any spilled food or debris that could serve as a food source for beetles. This step helps eliminate eggs and larvae that might be hiding on non-fabric surfaces.

> 💡 **Tip:** Don't forget to clean inside dresser drawers and under beds, as these are common hiding spots.

### Step 5: Apply Diatomaceous Earth or Targeted Insecticides (Optional)

For persistent infestations, food-grade diatomaceous earth can be lightly sprinkled in cracks, crevices, and under furniture where beetles hide, acting as a natural desiccant. Alternatively, a pyrethrin-based insecticide spray can be used in targeted, non-visible areas, following product instructions carefully. Always prioritize safety and ventilation when using any chemical treatments.

> 💡 **Tip:** Diatomaceous earth works by dehydrating insects; leave it for a few days before vacuuming for maximum effectiveness.

### Step 6: Seal Entry Points and Implement Prevention

Seal any cracks or crevices in walls, floors, and around windows and doors with caulk to block potential entry points for beetles. Store natural fiber items like wool, silk, and furs in airtight containers or garment bags. Regular maintenance, such as prompt spill cleanup and storing dry goods in sealed containers, further deters future infestations.

> 💡 **Tip:** Install screens on windows and doors, and repair any damaged screens to prevent adult beetles from flying inside.

### Step 7: Monitor and Maintain Regular Cleaning

After the initial cleanup, continue to monitor for any signs of returning beetles. Maintain a consistent routine of thorough vacuuming, especially in previously affected areas. Regularly inspect stored items and clean out forgotten corners to prevent new infestations from taking hold. Persistent vigilance is key to long-term eradication.

> 💡 **Tip:** Consider using pheromone traps to monitor for adult carpet beetles, helping you detect any new activity early.


## Tips & Tricks

- Regular vacuuming is your best defense against carpet beetles, as it removes eggs, larvae, and food sources.
- Store natural fiber items like wool, silk, and furs in airtight containers or garment bags to protect them from infestation.
- Inspect secondhand furniture, clothing, and rugs thoroughly before bringing them into your home to avoid introducing pests.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always read and follow all instructions and safety warnings on insecticide product labels carefully; ensure proper ventilation during and after application.
- ⚠️ Wear gloves and a mask when cleaning or applying diatomaceous earth or any chemical treatments to avoid irritation.
- ⚠️ If using insecticides, keep children and pets away from treated areas until they are completely dry and safe according to product instructions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What causes carpet beetles?**

Carpet beetles are often attracted to homes by natural fibers like wool, silk, leather, and feathers, which serve as their food source. They can also enter through open windows, cracks, or on infested secondhand items. Pet dander, lint, and even dried food spills can also attract them.

**Where do carpet beetles hide?**

Carpet beetles typically hide in dark, undisturbed areas. Common hiding spots include under carpets and rugs, in closets, behind baseboards, inside upholstered furniture, and in air vents. They are also found in pantries if there are stored dry goods.

**Are carpet beetles harmful to humans?**

While carpet beetles do not bite humans, their larvae can cause an allergic reaction in some individuals, leading to itchy rashes or skin irritation. They are primarily a nuisance pest due to the damage they inflict on natural fibers and stored products.

**How do I prevent carpet beetles from returning?**

Prevention involves consistent cleanliness, regular vacuuming, and storing susceptible items in airtight containers. Sealing cracks and crevices, promptly cleaning up spills, and inspecting new items before they enter your home are also crucial preventative measures.

**Can carpet beetles infest clothes?**

Yes, carpet beetles can infest clothes, especially those made of natural fibers such as wool, silk, cashmere, and fur. They are attracted to items that have been stored undisturbed for long periods, feeding on the fabric and leaving irregular holes.

**What does carpet beetle damage look like?**

Carpet beetle damage appears as irregular holes or worn spots on natural fibers like carpets, rugs, clothing, and upholstery. You might also find shed larval skins or fecal pellets (tiny, sand-like droppings) in the damaged areas. The damage is often more widespread and less defined than moth damage.

**Is professional help necessary for carpet beetles?**

For small, localized infestations, DIY methods are often effective. However, if the infestation is widespread, persistent despite your efforts, or difficult to locate, professional pest control services can provide more comprehensive treatment and advice. They have access to stronger treatments and expertise.
