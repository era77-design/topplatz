To plant an acorn, start by selecting viable acorns, often by performing a float test. Many species require cold stratification before planting. Once prepared, plant the acorn pointed-side down in a suitable pot with well-draining soil, ensuring it's covered by about an inch of soil.

## What You Need

- Fresh acorns (from suitable oak species)
- Small pots or seedling trays (at least 6-8 inches deep)
- Well-draining potting mix
- Shovel or trowel (for outdoor planting)
- Watering can or spray bottle
- Plastic bag or container (for stratification)
- Sand or peat moss (for stratification)

## Steps

### Step 1: Collect and Select Viable Acorns

Gather fresh acorns in the fall, ideally shortly after they drop from the tree. Look for plump, healthy acorns without holes or signs of mold. Different oak species have different dormancy requirements, so identify your acorn's species if possible. Avoid acorns that are green; they are not yet mature.

> 💡 **Tip:** Collect more acorns than you intend to plant, as some may not be viable or sprout.

### Step 2: Perform a Float Test

To check for viability, place your collected acorns in a bucket of water. Discard any acorns that float, as these are usually non-viable, hollow, or infested with pests. Healthy, viable acorns will sink to the bottom. This simple test helps improve your success rate significantly.

### Step 3: Stratify Your Acorns (Cold Treatment)

Many oak species require a period of cold stratification to break dormancy and encourage germination. Mix your sinking acorns with a moist medium like sand, peat moss, or sawdust in a plastic bag or container. Store them in a refrigerator (not freezer) at 35-40°F (1-4°C) for 60-90 days. Keep the medium consistently moist.

> 💡 **Tip:** Check for mold periodically during stratification. If mold appears, rinse the acorns and replace the moist medium.

### Step 4: Prepare Your Planting Medium

Choose a well-draining potting mix for your acorns. A good blend might include peat moss, perlite, and a small amount of compost. Fill your chosen pots or seedling trays, ensuring they have drainage holes. The depth of the pot is important to accommodate the taproot of the developing oak seedling.

### Step 5: Plant the Acorn

Once stratification is complete, plant each acorn pointed-side down in its own pot. Cover the acorn with about 1 inch (2.5 cm) of potting mix. Planting pointed-side down is generally recommended as it allows the root to grow downwards and the shoot upwards more efficiently. Water thoroughly after planting.

> 💡 **Tip:** If you're unsure which side is 'pointed', plant the acorn on its side; it will still find its way.

### Step 6: Provide Optimal Growing Conditions

Place your planted acorns in a location that receives bright, indirect sunlight. Keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged. Germination can take several weeks or even months after stratification. Protect young seedlings from pests like squirrels, which are fond of acorns and young shoots. ### Step 7: Transplant Your Seedling (Optional but Recommended)

Once your oak seedling has developed a few sets of true leaves and is about 6-12 inches tall, it can be transplanted to a larger pot or directly into its permanent outdoor location. Choose a spot with ample sunlight and well-draining soil, considering the mature size of an oak tree. Dig a hole twice as wide as the root ball and just as deep.


## Tips & Tricks

- Choose acorns from native oak species for your region, as they are best adapted to local conditions.
- Protect newly planted acorns and young seedlings from squirrels, rodents, and deer using wire cages or netting.
- Patience is key! Oak trees grow slowly, especially in their early years. Don't be discouraged if germination takes time.
- Ensure pots have excellent drainage to prevent root rot, which is fatal to young seedlings.
- If planting directly outdoors, choose a sheltered spot and mark the location so you don't accidentally disturb it.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not let acorns dry out after collection or during stratification, as this can kill the embryo.
- ⚠️ Be vigilant against mold during stratification; excessive mold can harm the acorns. If seen, clean and re-moisten the medium.
- ⚠️ Protect young seedlings from extreme temperatures, both hot and cold, especially during their first year.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When is the best time to plant an acorn?**

The best time to plant stratified acorns is in late winter or early spring, after their cold treatment period is complete. If planting directly outdoors, fall is ideal, allowing nature to stratify them.

**How deep should I plant an acorn?**

Plant acorns about 1 inch (2.5 cm) deep in the soil. This provides enough cover to protect them while allowing the seedling to emerge without too much effort.

**What type of soil is best for planting acorns?**

Well-draining potting mix is crucial. A mix designed for seedlings or general purpose potting soil amended with perlite or sand for extra drainage works well.

**How long does it take for an acorn to sprout?**

After proper stratification, an acorn can sprout anywhere from a few weeks to several months. Some species are naturally slower than others, so patience is important.

**Can I plant acorns directly outside?**

Yes, you can plant acorns directly outside in the fall. Nature will provide the necessary cold stratification. However, they will need protection from animals like squirrels and rodents.

**How do I protect my planted acorns from squirrels?**

To protect planted acorns from squirrels, you can cover the planting site with a wire mesh cage, hardware cloth, or a cloche. This prevents animals from digging them up.

**What if my acorns don't sprout after stratification?**

If your acorns don't sprout, they might not have been viable, stratification might have been insufficient, or they may have dried out. Try again with a fresh batch, ensuring proper moisture and temperature during stratification.
