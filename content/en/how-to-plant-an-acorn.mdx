To plant an acorn, first select healthy, viable acorns and prepare them through stratification, a process mimicking winter conditions. Once stratified, plant the acorn shallowly in a pot or directly in the ground, ensuring it has adequate moisture and protection from pests. With proper care, your acorn will germinate and begin its journey to becoming an oak tree.

## What You Need

- Healthy acorns
- Potting mix (if starting indoors)
- Small pots or seed trays (if starting indoors)
- Shovel or trowel
- Watering can
- Plastic bag
- Damp peat moss, sand, or paper towels (for stratification)
- Mulch (optional)
- Wire mesh or screen (for pest protection, optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Collect and Select Healthy Acorns

Gather acorns in the fall, ideally shortly after they drop from the tree. Look for acorns that are plump, firm, and free of holes, cracks, or mold. Perform a float test by placing them in water; viable acorns will sink, while those that float are likely non-viable and should be discarded. Collect more than you think you'll need, as not all will germinate.

### Step 2: Stratify Your Acorns

Most oak species require a period of cold, moist stratification to break dormancy and encourage germination. Place viable acorns in a plastic bag with damp peat moss, sand, or paper towels, ensuring the medium is moist but not soaking wet. Store them in the refrigerator (not freezer) for 60-90 days, checking periodically to ensure the medium stays damp and no mold grows. This mimics the natural winter cycle.

### Step 3: Prepare Your Planting Site or Pots

Choose a planting location that receives ample sunlight (at least 6 hours daily) and has well-draining soil, or prepare small pots with a good quality potting mix if starting indoors. Ensure the chosen site has enough space for a mature oak tree, considering its eventual size. If planting multiple acorns, space them appropriately to avoid overcrowding later on.

### Step 4: Plant the Acorn

Plant the stratified acorn on its side, about 1-2 inches (2.5-5 cm) deep. If planting in a pot, ensure the pot has drainage holes to prevent waterlogging. If planting directly in the ground, gently press it into the soil. The root will emerge from the pointed end, so orienting it horizontally or slightly on its side is ideal for initial root development.

### Step 5: Water and Protect the Acorn

After planting, water the soil thoroughly but gently. Keep the soil consistently moist but never waterlogged. To protect the acorn from pests like squirrels, rodents, or birds, consider placing a small wire mesh cage or screen over the planting site, especially if planting outdoors. This physical barrier can be crucial for successful germination.

### Step 6: Provide Initial Care

Monitor the planting site regularly for moisture levels and signs of germination. Germination can take several weeks to months after stratification. Once a seedling emerges, continue to provide consistent moisture and protect it from weeds, which can compete for nutrients, much like caring for young [How Do You Plant Garlic? A Complete Guide to Growing](/en/how-do-you-plant-garlic) or other garden plants. A layer of mulch can help retain soil moisture and suppress weeds.

### Step 7: Transplant (if started indoors) or Continue Outdoor Care

If you started your acorn in a pot, transplant the seedling to its permanent outdoor location once it has developed a few sets of true leaves and the risk of frost has passed. When transplanting, be very careful not to disturb the delicate root system, as oaks are sensitive to root disturbance. Continue providing regular water and protection from pests and weeds as the young tree grows and establishes itself.


## Tips & Tricks

- Choose acorns from a healthy, mature oak tree native to your local area for the best chance of success and ecological fit.
- Ensure good drainage in pots to prevent root rot, as acorns are susceptible to excessive moisture.
- Mark your planting spot with a stake or tag, especially if planting directly in the ground, so you don't forget where it is.
- Be patient; growing an oak tree from an acorn is a long-term project that requires time for germination and growth.
- Consider planting several acorns to increase your chances of at least one successfully growing into a sapling.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Be gentle when handling acorns and especially young seedlings, as they are fragile and sensitive to root disturbance.
- ⚠️ Protect young seedlings from pests like deer, rabbits, squirrels, and voles, which can quickly destroy them before they establish.
- ⚠️ Avoid overwatering, which can lead to fungal diseases and rot; the soil should be moist, not soggy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What kind of acorns should I plant?**

You should plant acorns from a healthy, mature oak tree that is native to your region. Look for plump, firm acorns without any holes or signs of disease. The float test (submerging in water) helps identify viable acorns, as good ones will sink.

**Do all acorns need stratification?**

Most oak species, especially those in temperate climates, require a period of cold stratification to break dormancy and encourage germination. This process mimics natural winter conditions. Without it, germination rates can be very low or non-existent.

**How long does it take for an acorn to sprout?**

After proper stratification, an acorn can take anywhere from a few weeks to several months to sprout once planted. The exact time depends on the oak species, environmental conditions, and the success of the stratification process. Patience is key.

**Can I plant an acorn directly in the ground?**

Yes, you can plant an acorn directly in the ground, provided you have chosen an appropriate site with good soil and sunlight. Ensure it's protected from pests like squirrels and rodents, and mark the spot so you can monitor its progress.

**How deep should I plant an acorn?**

Acorns should be planted relatively shallowly, about 1 to 2 inches (2.5 to 5 cm) deep. Planting them too deep can prevent the seedling from reaching the surface, while planting too shallowly might leave them exposed to drying out or pests.

**How often should I water a newly planted acorn?**

You should keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged. Check the soil regularly; if the top inch feels dry, it's time to water. Reduce watering frequency as the seedling establishes, but ensure it receives adequate moisture, especially during dry periods.

**What pests should I watch out for when growing an acorn?**

Common pests include squirrels, rodents (like mice and voles), and deer, all of which can eat acorns or young seedlings. Insects can also pose a threat. Using protective measures like wire mesh cages or tree shelters can help deter these animals.
