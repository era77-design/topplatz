A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by a worn-out washer, O-ring, or cartridge. Fixing it usually involves turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet handle, and replacing the faulty component. This common DIY repair can save water, prevent further damage, and eliminate an annoying drip.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver set (flathead and Phillips)
- Adjustable wrench
- Penetrating oil (optional, for stuck parts)
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge (match existing type)
- Plumber's grease
- Utility knife or small flathead screwdriver (for caps)
- Rags or towels
- Pliers (optional, for stubborn nuts)
- Flashlight (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Turn Off the Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, locate the main water shut-off valve for your house or the specific valve for the bathroom. Turn it off completely to prevent flooding and water damage during the repair. Test the faucet afterward to confirm the water is off.

> 💡 **Tip:** If you can't find a bathroom-specific shut-off, the main house valve is usually in the basement, near the water meter, or outside the house.

### Step 2: 2. Cover the Drain and Protect the Tub

Place a rag or stopper over the bathtub drain to prevent small screws or parts from falling in and getting lost. Lay a towel on the bottom of the tub to protect it from dropped tools or components. This simple step can save you a lot of hassle and potential damage to your tub.

> 💡 **Tip:** Keeping all removed parts in a small container or on a clean cloth will help prevent them from getting misplaced.

### Step 3: 3. Remove the Faucet Handle

Most faucet handles have a decorative cap that covers a screw. Carefully pry off this cap with a utility knife or a small flathead screwdriver. Use a Phillips or flathead screwdriver to loosen and remove the screw, then pull the handle straight off. Some handles may simply pull off or be held by a set screw on the side.

> 💡 **Tip:** If the handle is stuck, a little penetrating oil applied to the screw or base can help loosen it. Allow it a few minutes to work.

### Step 4: 4. Access and Remove the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

Once the handle is off, you'll see the faucet stem or cartridge. Depending on your faucet type, you might need to unscrew a packing nut with an adjustable wrench to remove the stem. For cartridge faucets, there's usually a retaining clip or nut to remove before pulling out the cartridge. Pay attention to the orientation of the cartridge before removal.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take a photo of the assembly before you start disassembling it. This will be invaluable when putting it back together correctly.

### Step 5: 5. Replace Worn Components

Examine the removed stem or cartridge for worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or seals. Replace any brittle, cracked, or flattened components with new ones that precisely match the original size and type. For cartridge faucets, replace the entire cartridge if it's faulty. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings before installation to ensure a good seal.

> 💡 **Tip:** It's often a good idea to replace all washers and O-rings at once, even if only one looks bad, as they tend to wear out similarly. Take the old parts to a hardware store to ensure you get exact matches.

### Step 6: 6. Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully reassemble the faucet in reverse order of disassembly. Ensure all parts are seated correctly and screws are tightened snugly, but do not overtighten, as this can strip threads or damage components. Make sure the stem or cartridge is oriented correctly before securing it with its clip or nut.

> 💡 **Tip:** Double-check that all connections are firm and all components are in their correct positions before turning the water back on.

### Step 7: 7. Restore Water Supply and Test

Once everything is reassembled, slowly turn the main water supply back on. Check for any leaks around the faucet base or handle. Turn the faucet on and off several times to ensure it's working properly and the drip has stopped. If the leak persists, you may need to re-evaluate the components or consider a full faucet replacement. For guidance on a full replacement, see our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A Step-by-Step DIY Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).

> 💡 **Tip:** If the leak continues or the issue is more complex, don't hesitate to call a professional plumber.


## Tips & Tricks

- Work slowly and methodically. Rushing through the process can lead to stripped screws, damaged parts, or overlooked details.
- Always have a few rags and a bucket nearby to catch any residual water that might escape during disassembly.
- Regularly clean your faucet to prevent mineral buildup, which can accelerate the wear and tear on internal components and contribute to leaks over time.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always ensure the main water supply is completely off before beginning any work to avoid unexpected sprays or significant water damage.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten screws or nuts, especially plastic ones, as this can strip threads, crack components, or cause new leaks.
- ⚠️ Older plumbing fixtures can be brittle. Exercise extra caution when disassembling to avoid breaking pipes or other parts that could lead to a larger repair job.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, you typically need to turn off the water supply, remove the handle, access the internal stem or cartridge, and replace any worn-out washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge. Reassemble the faucet carefully and test for leaks.

**What causes a bathtub faucet to leak?**

The most common causes for a leaky bathtub faucet are worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge. Over time, these components degrade, allowing water to seep past and create a drip. Mineral buildup can also contribute to seal deterioration.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky faucet?**

You'll generally need a screwdriver set (flathead and Phillips), an adjustable wrench, replacement washers/O-rings/cartridge, plumber's grease, and possibly a utility knife or pliers. Having rags and a flashlight can also be very helpful.

**Can I fix a leaky faucet myself?**

Yes, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet is a common DIY task that most homeowners can accomplish with basic tools and a little patience. The key is correctly identifying the type of faucet and getting the right replacement parts.

**How long does it take to fix a leaky faucet?**

For a typical leaky bathtub faucet, the repair usually takes about 30 minutes to an hour. This time includes gathering tools, disassembling, replacing parts, and reassembling. More stubborn or complex faucets might take a bit longer.

**How do you fix bathtub faucet drip?**

To fix a bathtub faucet drip, the process involves replacing the internal components responsible for sealing the water flow. This almost always means replacing worn-out washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge, which are the parts that degrade over time and cause the drip.

**When should I call a professional plumber?**

You should call a professional plumber if you're unable to identify the cause of the leak, if the repair seems too complex for your skill level, if the leak persists after your attempts, or if you encounter issues like stripped pipes or severely corroded fixtures that require specialized tools or expertise.
