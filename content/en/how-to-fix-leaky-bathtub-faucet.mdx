A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by worn-out washers or O-rings inside the faucet mechanism. Fixing it typically involves turning off the water supply, carefully disassembling the faucet handle, and replacing the faulty rubber components. This straightforward DIY repair can save water, prevent further damage, and eliminate an annoying drip.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and/or flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge kit (specific to your faucet type)
- Utility knife or small pry tool (optional, for decorative caps)
- Rag or towel
- Penetrating oil (optional, for stubborn screws/nuts)
- Plumber's grease (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off Water Supply

Locate the main water shut-off valve for your home, typically found in the basement, utility closet, or near the water meter. For a single bathroom leak, you might find a shut-off valve under the sink or behind an access panel for the tub. Turn the valve clockwise until the water flow completely stops to prevent flooding during the repair.

> 💡 **Tip:** If you're unsure which valve controls the bathroom, it's safest to turn off the main house supply.

### Step 2: Drain Remaining Water

With the water supply off, open the bathtub faucet handles to their full 'on' position. This action will release any residual water pressure in the pipes and drain any remaining water from the faucet spout. Keep a rag or small bucket handy to catch any drips during this process.

### Step 3: Remove Faucet Handle

Most faucet handles have a decorative cap that covers a screw. Gently pry off this cap with a small flathead screwdriver or utility knife. Unscrew the exposed screw (it could be a Phillips or flathead) and carefully pull the handle straight off. Some older handles may simply pull off without a visible screw.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take a photo of the handle's orientation before removal to ensure correct reassembly later.

### Step 4: Disassemble Faucet Stem or Cartridge

Once the handle is off, you'll see the faucet stem or cartridge. Depending on your faucet type, you might need an adjustable wrench to loosen a packing nut or a specialized cartridge tool to remove the cartridge itself. Carefully unscrew or pull out the stem/cartridge, noting how it sits within the faucet body. This component most likely houses the worn parts causing the leak.

### Step 5: Replace Worn Components

Inspect the removed stem or cartridge for worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or seals, as these are typically the culprits behind leaks. Replace these with new, identical parts from a repair kit or specific replacements for your faucet model. If you have a single-handle cartridge faucet, you will usually replace the entire cartridge unit.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take the old components to a hardware store to ensure you purchase the correct replacements, or note down your faucet's brand and model.

### Step 6: Reassemble Faucet

Carefully reverse the disassembly process. Insert the new washer(s) or cartridge, ensuring everything aligns correctly within the faucet body. Tighten the packing nut or cartridge retainer with your adjustable wrench, but avoid overtightening, which can damage new components. Replace the faucet handle and secure it with its screw and decorative cap.

### Step 7: Test for Leaks

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Check for any drips or leaks around the faucet handle, spout, and behind the wall if accessible. Run the faucet through its hot and cold settings to ensure proper function and no further leaks. If a drip persists, re-check the tightening of components or ensure all parts are correctly seated.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always have a bucket or towel ready to catch any residual water during the repair process.
- If screws are seized, apply a small amount of penetrating oil and let it sit for a few minutes before trying to loosen them.
- When buying replacement parts, it's helpful to know your faucet's brand and model, or bring the old parts to the store.
- Clean any mineral deposits from the faucet's internal components before reassembly to ensure a smooth operation and proper seal.
- Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings for better sealing and to ease future disassembly.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the water supply completely before starting any plumbing repair to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Be careful not to strip screws or overtighten nuts, as this can cause further damage to the faucet.
- ⚠️ Avoid using excessive force when disassembling or reassembling components, as plastic or brass parts can break easily.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, first turn off the water supply, then remove the handle and access the internal stem or cartridge. Replace any worn-out washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge, and then carefully reassemble the faucet.

**What causes a bathtub faucet to leak?**

Bathtub faucets typically leak due to worn-out rubber components like washers, O-rings, or seals inside the faucet's stem or cartridge. Over time, these parts degrade from water pressure and mineral deposits, losing their ability to create a watertight seal.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

You'll generally need a screwdriver (Phillips and/or flathead), an adjustable wrench, replacement washers or an O-ring kit, and possibly plumber's tape. A utility knife or small pry tool might also be helpful for removing decorative caps.

**How do I know if I need to replace a washer or a cartridge?**

If you have a two-handle compression faucet, you'll likely be replacing washers or O-rings. If you have a single-handle faucet, it usually contains a cartridge, which you'll replace as a whole unit. Disassembly will reveal which type your specific faucet uses.

**Can I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself, or do I need a plumber?**

Most leaky bathtub faucets can be fixed by a DIY enthusiast with basic tools and knowledge. The process usually involves replacing inexpensive worn parts. However, if the leak persists after your repair or if you're uncomfortable with the process, calling a plumber is recommended.

**How do I fix bathtub faucet if it's still leaking after replacing parts?**

If your bathtub faucet is still leaking after replacing components, double-check that all parts are correctly installed and tightened, but not overtightened. Ensure you used the correct size and type of replacement parts. If the issue persists, the faucet body itself might be damaged, or it might be time to consider a full replacement. For a complete overhaul, consult our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).

**How long does it take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For an average DIYer, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet typically takes about 30 minutes to an hour, depending on your experience and the complexity of your faucet. Gathering the right replacement parts might add extra time to the overall process.
