A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by worn-out washers or O-rings inside the handle assembly. Fixing it typically involves turning off the water, disassembling the faucet, replacing the faulty parts, and reassembling it carefully. This common DIY repair can save water and prevent further damage.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver set (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench or pliers
- Replacement washers/O-rings kit (specific to faucet type if known)
- Plumber's grease
- Utility knife or small flathead screwdriver (for decorative caps)
- Rag or towel
- Bucket (optional, for residual drips)
- Flashlight (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Turn Off the Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, it's crucial to shut off the water supply to your bathtub. Look for the shut-off valves typically located under the sink, behind an access panel, or for the main water shut-off valve for your entire home. Turn the faucet on briefly after shutting off the water to relieve any remaining pressure in the lines.

### Step 2: 2. Remove Faucet Handles and Decorative Trim

Most faucet handles have a decorative cap that can be pried off with a utility knife or small flathead screwdriver, revealing a screw underneath. Unscrew this screw and gently pull the handle straight off. If there's a escutcheon plate or trim, it might also be held by screws or simply pull off, exposing the faucet body.

### Step 3: 3. Disassemble the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

Depending on your faucet type (compression, cartridge, or ceramic disc), you'll need to remove the stem or cartridge. For compression faucets, use an adjustable wrench to unscrew the packing nut and then the stem. For cartridge or ceramic disc faucets, you might need to remove a retaining clip or nut before pulling the cartridge straight out. Pay attention to the orientation of parts as you remove them.

### Step 4: 4. Inspect and Replace Worn Parts

Carefully examine the removed stem or cartridge for worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or seals. These are the most common culprits for leaks. Replace any brittle, cracked, or flattened components with new ones from your repair kit. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings and threads to ensure a good seal and smooth operation.

### Step 5: 5. Reassemble the Faucet Components

Reinsert the new washers, O-rings, and the stem or cartridge back into the faucet body, ensuring all parts are correctly aligned and seated. Tighten the packing nut or retaining clip firmly, but avoid overtightening, which can damage new components. Reattach the faucet handles and decorative trim, securing them with their screws and caps.

### Step 6: 6. Restore Water Supply and Test

Slowly turn the water supply back on, checking for any immediate leaks around the faucet base. Turn the faucet on and off a few times to flush out any air and ensure it's functioning correctly. If the leak persists, you may need to re-examine the components or consider a more extensive repair. If the faucet is old and beyond repair, you might consider how to replace it entirely. Learn more about replacement options in our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A Comprehensive DIY Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).


## Tips & Tricks

- Before purchasing replacement parts, try to identify your faucet's brand and model or take the old parts to a hardware store for an exact match.
- Keep a small container or rag nearby to hold tiny screws and parts, preventing them from falling into the drain.
- Avoid overtightening screws and nuts during reassembly, as this can strip threads or crack plastic components.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always ensure the water supply is completely turned off before attempting any repairs to prevent water damage or injury.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I identify what's causing my bathtub faucet to leak?**

Most bathtub faucet leaks are caused by worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet assembly. A constant drip often indicates a problem with these internal sealing components that have deteriorated over time.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

You'll typically need a set of screwdrivers (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench or pliers, replacement washers/O-rings or a new cartridge, and possibly plumber's grease. A utility knife can be helpful for removing decorative caps.

**Can I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself, or do I need a plumber?**

Many leaky bathtub faucets can be fixed by a DIY enthusiast with basic tools and knowledge. If you're comfortable with simple repairs and identifying faucet parts, you can likely do it yourself. However, for complex issues or if you're unsure, it's always best to consult a professional plumber.

**How to fix bathtub faucet when it's constantly dripping?**

To fix a constantly dripping bathtub faucet, you'll need to turn off the water, disassemble the handle and stem/cartridge, and replace the worn-out internal rubber washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge. These components are responsible for creating a watertight seal when the faucet is closed.

**How long does it typically take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For a common compression faucet, replacing a washer can take as little as 15-30 minutes. More complex cartridge-style faucets might take 30-60 minutes, especially if identifying the correct replacement part takes time. It's a relatively quick DIY project.

**What are the common types of bathtub faucets, and how do they differ in repair?**

Common types include compression (uses rubber washers), cartridge (uses a cylindrical cartridge), and ceramic disc (uses two ceramic discs). Compression faucets typically require replacing washers, while cartridge and ceramic disc faucets usually require replacing the entire cartridge or disc assembly to fix leaks.
