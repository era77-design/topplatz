A leaky bathtub faucet is a common household nuisance, but it's often a straightforward fix. Most drips are caused by worn-out washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet handle. With a few basic tools and a little patience, you can easily stop the drip yourself.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Utility knife or small flathead screwdriver
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge kit (matching your faucet type)
- Plumber's grease (silicone-based)
- Old rags or towels
- Penetrating oil (optional, for stuck screws)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, locate the main water shut-off valve for your home or, if available, the shut-off valves for your bathroom. Turn the water off completely to prevent flooding. Once the water is off, open the bathtub faucet to drain any remaining water from the pipes.

### Step 2: Cover the Drain and Remove Handle Cap

Place a rag or stopper over the bathtub drain to prevent small screws or parts from falling into it. Use a utility knife or small flathead screwdriver to gently pry off the decorative cap on top of the faucet handle. This cap usually conceals a screw.

### Step 3: Remove Faucet Handle and Trim

Use the appropriate screwdriver (usually Phillips) to remove the screw holding the faucet handle in place. Pull the handle straight off. You may also need to remove any escutcheon plates or trim that cover the faucet body, typically held on by screws or simply threading off.

### Step 4: Disassemble the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

Depending on your faucet type, you'll either see a stem (common in older compression faucets) or a cartridge (common in newer single-lever faucets). Use an adjustable wrench to carefully loosen and remove the packing nut or retaining clip holding the stem or cartridge in place. Gently pull out the stem or cartridge.

### Step 5: Replace Worn Components

Inspect the removed stem or cartridge. For compression faucets, replace any worn rubber washers at the bottom and O-rings around the stem. Apply a thin coat of plumber's grease to new O-rings. For cartridge faucets, replace the entire cartridge with a new one that matches your old one exactly. Take the old parts to a hardware store to ensure you get the correct replacements.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully insert the new washers/O-rings or cartridge back into the faucet body. Tighten the packing nut or secure the retaining clip. Reattach any trim and then the faucet handle, making sure the screw is snug but not overtightened. Replace the decorative cap.

### Step 7: Test for Leaks

Slowly turn the water supply back on. Check the faucet for any drips or leaks. Turn the handle on and off a few times to ensure everything is functioning correctly. If the leak persists, you may need to recheck your work or consider replacing the entire faucet if it's too old or damaged. If you decide to replace it, consult our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet - DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).


## Tips & Tricks

- Take photos with your phone at each step of disassembly. This makes reassembly much easier, especially if you have many small parts.
- Always use plumber's grease on new O-rings and threads to ensure a good seal and make future repairs easier.
- When buying replacement parts, it's often helpful to bring the old, worn-out parts to the hardware store for an exact match.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the main water supply before attempting any plumbing repairs to avoid water damage.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten screws or nuts, as this can strip threads or crack plastic components.
- ⚠️ If you encounter significant resistance or are unsure about any step, it's best to consult a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What causes a leaky bathtub faucet?**

Most leaky bathtub faucets are caused by worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet mechanism. Over time, these components degrade, leading to drips and leaks.

**Can I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself?**

Yes, in many cases, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet is a relatively easy DIY project. With basic tools and the correct replacement parts, you can often resolve the issue without needing to call a professional.

**What tools do I need to fix a bathtub faucet?**

To fix a bathtub faucet, you'll typically need screwdrivers (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, a utility knife, and the appropriate replacement washers, O-rings, or a new cartridge for your specific faucet model.

**How do I know if I need new washers, O-rings, or a cartridge?**

The type of repair depends on your faucet's design. Compression faucets usually require new washers and O-rings, while single-lever faucets typically use a cartridge. Disassembling the faucet will reveal which component is causing the leak and needs replacement.

**How long does it typically take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For most DIYers, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet can take anywhere from 30 minutes to an hour, depending on your experience and how easily the parts come apart. Having the right replacement parts on hand beforehand can significantly speed up the process.

**When should I consider replacing the entire faucet instead of just fixing a leak?**

You might consider replacing the entire faucet if it's very old, extensively corroded, if replacement parts are difficult or impossible to find, or if you simply want to upgrade its appearance or functionality. Sometimes, the cost of multiple repairs can approach the cost of a new, more efficient faucet.

**What should I do if my bathtub faucet continues to leak after I've tried to fix it?**

If your bathtub faucet still leaks after attempting a DIY repair, double-check that all components are correctly installed and tightened. Ensure you used the right replacement parts for your specific faucet model. If the leak persists, it might indicate a more complex issue, and it's advisable to consult a professional plumber.
