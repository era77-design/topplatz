A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by worn-out washers or O-rings inside the faucet assembly. Fixing it yourself can save money, prevent water waste, and is a common DIY plumbing task. This guide will walk you through the simple steps to diagnose and repair the leak, restoring peace to your bathroom.

## What You Need

- Adjustable wrench
- Screwdriver set (Phillips and flathead)
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge kit (specific to your faucet model)
- Plumber's grease
- Utility knife or small flathead screwdriver (for prying)
- Rags or towels
- Bucket
- Flashlight (optional)
- Penetrating oil (for stubborn screws)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing repair, locate and turn off the main water supply to your house, or the shut-off valve specifically for the bathtub. This prevents flooding once you begin disassembling the faucet. Turn on the faucet briefly to drain any remaining water from the lines, ensuring the pressure is relieved.

### Step 2: Cover the Drain and Disassemble Handles

Place a rag or stopper over the bathtub drain to prevent small parts from falling in. Use a screwdriver to remove the decorative caps (if any) on the faucet handles. Underneath, you'll find screws that secure the handles; unscrew these and carefully pull the handles off the faucet stems. Some handles may just pull straight off once the cap is removed.

### Step 3: Remove the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

Once the handles are off, you'll see a escutcheon plate or a packing nut surrounding the faucet stem or cartridge. Use your adjustable wrench to carefully loosen and remove this nut. Gently pull out the faucet stem or cartridge assembly. Note its orientation as you remove it, or take a photo for easier reassembly.

### Step 4: Inspect and Replace Worn Components

Examine the removed stem or cartridge for any visible signs of wear, such as cracked rubber washers, flattened O-rings, or a corroded cartridge body. Remove the old, worn parts using a utility knife or a small screwdriver. Replace them with new, identical washers, O-rings, or a new cartridge from your repair kit. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new rubber components to ensure a good seal and easier installation.

### Step 5: Clean Valve Seats (If Applicable)

If your faucet uses traditional compression washers, the valve seats inside the faucet body can also become corroded or pitted, leading to leaks. Use a valve seat wrench (if you have one) to remove and inspect the valve seats. Clean any mineral deposits or replace them if they are heavily damaged. This step is not usually necessary for cartridge-style faucets.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully reverse the disassembly process. Insert the newly repaired stem or cartridge into the faucet body, ensuring it's oriented correctly. Hand-tighten the packing nut or escutcheon plate, then use the adjustable wrench to tighten it snugly, but do not overtighten. Reattach the handles and their decorative caps.

### Step 7: Restore Water Supply and Test for Leaks

Slowly turn the water supply back on, checking for any immediate leaks around the faucet base and handles. Turn the faucet on and off several times, testing both hot and cold water. Watch for any drips or persistent leaks. If a leak persists, you may need to re-tighten components or re-evaluate the replacement parts.


## Tips & Tricks

- Take photos with your phone at each stage of disassembly to help with reassembly.
- Always use genuine replacement parts specific to your faucet's brand and model for the best fit and longevity.
- Keep a small container of plumber's grease on hand; it's useful for many plumbing tasks.
- If you're replacing parts on one handle (e.g., hot), consider replacing the corresponding parts on the other handle (cold) at the same time, as they likely have similar wear.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the water supply before attempting any faucet repair to prevent water damage.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten nuts or screws, as this can strip threads or crack plastic components.
- ⚠️ If you encounter significant resistance or are unsure about a step, it's safer to consult a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

Most leaky bathtub faucets are caused by worn-out washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge. The repair typically involves turning off the water, disassembling the faucet, replacing the worn components, and then reassembling it carefully.

**How can I fix bathtub faucet drips?**

To stop bathtub faucet drips, you'll generally need to replace the internal rubber washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge that controls water flow. These parts wear down over time, leading to leaks and drips.

**What are the common causes of a leaky bathtub faucet?**

The most common causes include worn-out rubber washers, deteriorated O-rings, a damaged faucet cartridge, or corrosion on valve seats. Mineral deposits can also accumulate and interfere with proper sealing, causing leaks.

**Do I need special tools to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

Basic tools like an adjustable wrench, screwdrivers (Phillips and flathead), and a utility knife are usually sufficient. However, a specialized cartridge puller might be needed for some specific faucet types or stubborn cartridges.

**How long does it typically take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For a DIYer, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet can take anywhere from 30 minutes to an hour, depending on your experience and the specific faucet model. Gathering the correct replacement parts might add extra time to the overall process.

**When should I consider replacing the entire faucet instead of just fixing it?**

If your faucet is old, heavily corroded, or if you're unable to find replacement parts, it might be more cost-effective and efficient to replace the entire unit. You can learn more about this process in our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).

**Can I fix a leaky single-handle bathtub faucet?**

Yes, single-handle faucets often leak due to a worn cartridge. The repair process is similar to a two-handle faucet, involving turning off the water, disassembling the handle, removing the old cartridge, and installing a new one specific to your faucet model.
