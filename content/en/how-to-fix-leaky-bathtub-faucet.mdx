A leaky bathtub faucet can be a constant annoyance and a waste of water. Most common leaks are caused by worn-out washers, O-rings, or cartridges, which are relatively easy and inexpensive to replace yourself. This guide will walk you through the steps to quickly fix your dripping faucet.

## What You Need

- Screwdrivers (Philips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Utility knife (optional, for sealant)
- Replacement O-rings, washers, or cartridge (specific to your faucet model)
- Plumber's grease
- Cleaning cloth
- White vinegar (for cleaning mineral deposits)
- Safety glasses

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, it's crucial to shut off the water supply to your bathtub. Look for the shut-off valves typically located on the wall behind the access panel, or turn off the main water supply to your house. Once the water is off, open the faucet briefly to drain any remaining water in the lines and relieve pressure, preventing unexpected sprays.

> 💡 **Tip:** Place a towel or stopper in the drain to prevent small parts from falling down during the repair.

### Step 2: Remove the Faucet Handle

Most faucet handles are secured by a screw hidden under a decorative cap. Carefully pry off the cap using a flathead screwdriver or utility knife. Once exposed, use a Philips or flathead screwdriver to loosen and remove the screw. With the screw removed, the handle should lift straight off. If it's stuck, a gentle wiggle or a tap might help.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take a photo of the handle's position before removing it to help with reassembly later.

### Step 3: Access the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

After removing the handle, you'll see the faucet stem or cartridge. There might be a decorative escutcheon (trim plate) or a bonnet nut holding it in place. Use an adjustable wrench to carefully unscrew the bonnet nut or remove any retaining clips. Be gentle to avoid damaging the faucet body or connections. Once loose, you can usually pull out the stem or cartridge.

> 💡 **Tip:** Note the orientation of the stem or cartridge before removing it, especially if it has multiple holes or tabs.

### Step 4: Identify and Replace Worn Parts

Examine the removed stem or cartridge for worn-out rubber O-rings or washers, which are common culprits for leaks. If you have a compression faucet, you'll likely find a rubber washer at the base of the stem. For disc or cartridge faucets, you might need to replace the entire cartridge. Take the worn parts to a hardware store to ensure you purchase exact matches for your faucet model.

> 💡 **Tip:** Consider buying a multi-pack of O-rings and washers, as they are inexpensive and useful to have on hand for future repairs.

### Step 5: Clean and Lubricate Components

Before installing new parts, thoroughly clean all internal components of the faucet, including the faucet body and any metal parts. Use a cleaning cloth and, if necessary, a solution of white vinegar to remove mineral deposits or grime that could impede proper sealing. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings, washers, or the cartridge to ensure smooth operation and a better seal.

> 💡 **Tip:** Plumber's grease helps prevent premature wear and makes reassembly easier.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully insert the new O-rings, washers, or cartridge into the faucet body, ensuring they are correctly seated. Reverse the removal steps to reassemble the faucet. Tighten the bonnet nut with your adjustable wrench, but avoid over-tightening, which can damage the parts. Reattach the handle and secure it with its screw and decorative cap.

> 💡 **Tip:** Ensure all parts are snug but not excessively tight to prevent future damage or difficulty in disassembling.

### Step 7: Restore Water Supply and Test

Once everything is reassembled, slowly turn the water supply back on. Check for any immediate leaks around the faucet base or handle. Open and close the faucet several times to ensure it operates smoothly and that the leak has stopped. If a drip persists, you may need to re-evaluate the parts or tighten connections slightly.

> 💡 **Tip:** If the leak persists or the faucet is old and corroded, it might be time for a full replacement. See our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet) for more details.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always identify your faucet type (compression, cartridge, ball, disc) before buying replacement parts. Photos can help.
- Keep a small container or stopper in the drain when working to catch any dropped screws or small parts.
- If you can't find exact replacement parts, take the old ones to a specialty plumbing supply store.
- Consider replacing both hot and cold-side washers/O-rings even if only one side is leaking, as they likely have similar wear.
- Use plumber's tape on threaded connections if you suspect leaks from those areas, but typically not needed for O-rings/washers.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the water supply before attempting any repairs to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Do not over-tighten screws or nuts, as this can strip threads or crack plastic components.
- ⚠️ Wear safety glasses to protect your eyes from potential splashes or flying debris.
- ⚠️ If you encounter significant resistance or are unsure about a step, consider calling a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is my bathtub faucet leaking?**

Most bathtub faucet leaks are caused by worn-out rubber components like washers, O-rings, or seals inside the faucet's stem or cartridge. Over time, these parts degrade due to water pressure, mineral deposits, and regular use, leading to drips and leaks.

**How to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, you typically need to turn off the water, disassemble the faucet handle and housing, identify the worn-out part (usually a washer, O-ring, or cartridge), replace it with a new one, and then reassemble the faucet. Cleaning and lubricating internal components during the process also helps.

**How do you fix a leaky single-handle bathtub faucet?**

Fixing a leaky single-handle bathtub faucet usually involves replacing the internal cartridge. After turning off the water and removing the handle, you'll typically remove a retaining nut or clip to extract the old cartridge. Insert a new, matching cartridge, then reassemble the faucet, ensuring a proper seal.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

You'll generally need screwdrivers (Philips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, pliers, and potentially a utility knife. For the repair itself, you'll need the specific replacement parts (O-rings, washers, or a new cartridge) for your faucet model, along with plumber's grease and a cleaning cloth.

**How long does it take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For most DIYers, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet can take anywhere from 30 to 60 minutes. This includes time for gathering tools, identifying the faucet type, replacing the worn parts, and reassembling everything. The time can vary depending on your experience and the complexity of your faucet model.

**When should I replace my bathtub faucet instead of fixing it?**

Consider replacing your bathtub faucet if it's very old, corroded, or if you can't find suitable replacement parts. If the faucet body itself is damaged, or if repairs are becoming frequent and costly, a full replacement can be a more durable and aesthetically pleasing solution. Our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet) can assist you.

**Is it hard to fix bathtub faucet leaks?**

For most common issues like worn washers or O-rings, fixing bathtub faucet leaks is a relatively easy DIY task that doesn't require specialized plumbing skills. With the right tools and replacement parts, many homeowners can successfully tackle this repair themselves. However, more complex issues might warrant professional help.
