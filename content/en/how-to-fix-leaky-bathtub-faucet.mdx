Fixing a leaky bathtub faucet typically involves replacing worn-out components like rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge. This DIY guide will walk you through the process, helping you stop the drip, save water, and avoid costly plumbing calls with basic tools and a bit of patience.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Utility knife or small flathead screwdriver (for prying caps)
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge (specific to your faucet type)
- Plumber's grease (silicone-based)
- Rag or towel
- Wire brush or old toothbrush (for cleaning)
- Flashlight (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing repair, locate the main water shut-off valve for your house or, if available, the specific shut-off valves for your bathtub. Turn them off completely to prevent water flow. Open the faucet handles to drain any remaining water from the pipes and relieve pressure, then close the drain.

### Step 2: Disassemble the Faucet Handle

Most bathtub faucet handles have a decorative cap that can be pried off to reveal a screw. Use a utility knife or small flathead screwdriver to gently remove this cap. Then, use a Phillips or flathead screwdriver to remove the screw holding the handle in place and carefully pull the handle off.

### Step 3: Remove the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

Once the handle is off, you'll see the faucet stem or cartridge. Depending on your faucet type (compression, cartridge, ceramic disc, or ball), the removal method varies. For most, an adjustable wrench will be needed to unscrew the packing nut or retaining clip holding the stem or cartridge in place. Carefully pull out the entire assembly.

### Step 4: Inspect and Replace Worn Components

Examine the removed stem or cartridge for any visible wear, cracks, or deterioration on rubber washers, O-rings, or seals. These are the most common culprits for leaks. Replace any damaged rubber washers or O-rings with new ones of the exact same size. If you have a cartridge faucet, it's often easiest to replace the entire cartridge with a new, identical unit.

### Step 5: Clean and Lubricate Internal Parts

Before reassembly, use a wire brush or old toothbrush to clean any mineral deposits or grime from the faucet body's interior and the stem/cartridge. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new washers, O-rings, and the moving parts of the stem or cartridge. This helps create a better seal and extends the life of the components.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully insert the cleaned and newly fitted stem or cartridge back into the faucet body, ensuring it's oriented correctly. Hand-tighten the packing nut or retaining clip, then use your adjustable wrench to tighten it snugly, but do not overtighten, as this can damage components. Replace the handle and secure it with its screw and decorative cap.

### Step 7: Restore Water and Test for Leaks

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Check the faucet for any signs of leakage. Turn the faucet on and off several times, testing both hot and cold water to ensure proper function and that the drip has stopped. If a slight drip persists, you may need to gently tighten the packing nut a bit more.


## Tips & Tricks

- Take photos with your phone at each step of disassembly to help with reassembly.
- Keep all removed parts organized in the order they were taken off to avoid confusion.
- When buying replacement parts, bring the old ones to the hardware store to ensure an exact match.
- Don't overtighten screws or nuts; snug is sufficient to prevent damage to plastic or brass components.
- If you have an older faucet, consider replacing both the hot and cold stems/cartridges simultaneously for consistent performance.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the water supply before attempting any faucet repair to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Be careful when prying off decorative caps or old components, as they can be brittle.
- ⚠️ If you encounter significant resistance or cannot remove a part, do not force it, as you could cause further damage to the plumbing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, you typically need to turn off the water, disassemble the handle, remove the internal stem or cartridge, and replace worn components like rubber washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge. After replacing the faulty parts, clean and reassemble the faucet, then restore the water supply.

**What causes a bathtub faucet to leak?**

The most common causes for a bathtub faucet leak are worn-out rubber washers, deteriorated O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet assembly. Mineral deposits can also build up, preventing a tight seal. These components degrade over time due to normal wear and tear and water pressure.

**Can I fix my bathtub faucet myself?**

Yes, fixing a bathtub faucet is a common DIY project that most homeowners can tackle with basic tools and a little patience. The process involves identifying the type of faucet, replacing a few inexpensive parts, and reassembling it. If the problem is more complex or you're unsure, a professional plumber can always help.

**What tools do I need to fix a bathtub faucet?**

You'll typically need a screwdriver (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, pliers, a utility knife, and the appropriate replacement parts (washers, O-rings, or a new cartridge). Plumber's grease and a rag are also helpful for cleaning and lubrication.

**How long does it take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

A typical leaky bathtub faucet repair can take anywhere from 15 to 45 minutes, depending on your experience level and the type of faucet. Most of the time is spent on disassembly, identifying the worn part, and careful reassembly.

**When should I consider replacing the entire faucet instead of repairing it?**

You should consider replacing the entire faucet if the repair parts are difficult or impossible to find, if the faucet body itself is corroded or cracked, or if you've attempted multiple repairs without success. Sometimes, replacing an old, inefficient faucet with a new one can be more cost-effective in the long run. For a full replacement guide, see [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).

**What if my bathtub faucet is still leaking after the repair?**

If your bathtub faucet is still leaking after repair, double-check that all components were reassembled correctly and that the new parts are the exact match for your faucet model. Ensure all nuts are snug (but not overtightened) and that no debris is preventing a proper seal. If the leak persists, the issue might be more complex, such as a damaged valve seat or a deeper plumbing problem, and it may be time to consult a professional plumber.
