A leaky bathtub faucet is a common household nuisance that wastes water and can lead to higher utility bills. Most leaks are caused by worn-out internal components like washers or O-rings, which are relatively easy to replace with basic tools. This guide will walk you through the process of identifying the leak source and fixing it yourself.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Utility knife or small flathead screwdriver
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or a new cartridge (specific to your faucet type)
- Plumber's grease (silicone-based)
- Rag or towel
- Penetrating oil (optional)
- Wire brush (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Locate the main water shut-off valve for your house or the specific shut-off valves for the bathtub if available. Turn off both hot and cold water supplies to the faucet you're working on. Open the faucet to drain any remaining water and relieve pressure in the pipes.

> 💡 **Tip:** Ensure all water flow has stopped before proceeding to prevent accidental flooding.

### Step 2: Access the Faucet Handles

Most bathtub faucets have decorative caps covering the screws that hold the handles in place. Carefully pry off these caps using a small flathead screwdriver or utility knife. Unscrew the handle screws and pull the handles straight off the faucet stem to expose the internal components.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take photos as you disassemble to help remember the correct order for reassembly.

### Step 3: Remove the Faucet Cartridge or Stem

Once the handles are off, you'll see the faucet's internal components. Depending on your faucet type (compression, disc, ball, or cartridge), you'll need to remove the escutcheon plate and then the stem or cartridge. Use an adjustable wrench to carefully unscrew the retaining nut or cartridge nut. If it's a cartridge, pull it straight out, noting its orientation.

> 💡 **Tip:** If the cartridge is stuck, gently wiggle it while pulling or use a cartridge puller tool.

### Step 4: Identify and Replace Worn Parts

Examine the removed stem or cartridge for worn-out washers, O-rings, or seals. These are usually the culprits for leaks and appear cracked, flattened, or brittle. Take the old parts to a hardware store to ensure you get exact replacements. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings and seals before installation to ensure a good seal and smooth operation.

> 💡 **Tip:** It's often wise to replace all accessible washers and O-rings, even if only one appears worn, as they tend to wear out at similar rates.

### Step 5: Clean and Reassemble

Before putting everything back together, use a wire brush or old toothbrush to clean any mineral deposits or debris from the faucet body. Reinsert the new washers, O-rings, or cartridge in the exact reverse order you removed them. Tighten all nuts and screws, being careful not to overtighten, which can damage new parts.

> 💡 **Tip:** Ensure all components are seated correctly and snugly before tightening to prevent future leaks.

### Step 6: Test for Leaks and Finish Up

Turn the water supply back on slowly. Check the faucet for any immediate leaks around the handles or spout. If none are present, turn the faucet on and off a few times to ensure smooth operation and no dripping. Once confirmed, reattach the handles and decorative caps. If the leak persists, you might need to consider replacing the entire faucet. For guidance on a full replacement, consult [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).

> 💡 **Tip:** Listen for any unusual noises when turning the water back on, which could indicate trapped air or a new issue.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always have a rag or towel handy to catch drips or spills during the repair process.
- If screws are stubborn, apply penetrating oil and let it sit for a few minutes before attempting to unscrew them.
- When buying replacement parts, it's always best to take the old part with you to the hardware store for an exact match.
- Consider replacing both hot and cold side components even if only one is leaking, as they likely have similar wear and tear.
- Keep your work area clean and organized; small parts can easily get lost.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the main water supply to your house or the specific fixture before starting any plumbing repair to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten screws or nuts, as this can strip threads, crack plastic components, or damage new parts.
- ⚠️ If you encounter significant resistance, are unsure about any step, or the leak persists after your repair, it's best to consult a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

Fixing a leaky bathtub faucet typically involves turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet handles and internal components, identifying and replacing worn washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge, and then reassembling everything. Most leaks stem from these easily replaceable parts.

**How do you fix bathtub faucet issues like dripping?**

Dripping from a bathtub faucet is usually caused by degraded internal seals. To fix this, you'll need to access the faucet's stem or cartridge, remove the old, worn-out washers or O-rings, and replace them with new ones. Cleaning out any mineral deposits during the process can also help ensure a tight seal.

**What causes a bathtub faucet to leak?**

The most common causes of a leaky bathtub faucet are worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet. Mineral deposits can also accumulate and prevent a tight seal, leading to drips and leaks over time.

**Can I fix a leaky faucet myself or do I need a plumber?**

Many leaky bathtub faucets can be fixed by a DIY enthusiast with basic tools and a little patience. If you can identify the type of faucet and access its internal components, replacing a washer or cartridge is often straightforward. However, if you're uncomfortable or the leak persists, calling a plumber is advisable.

**How do I know what type of faucet I have?**

Bathtub faucets typically fall into four categories: compression, cartridge, ceramic disc, or ball type. The design of your handles and how they operate (e.g., turning a knob vs. lifting a lever) can give clues, but disassembling it is the best way to identify the internal mechanism and thus the correct replacement parts.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

You'll generally need a screwdriver (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, pliers, a utility knife, and the specific replacement parts (washers, O-rings, or a new cartridge). Plumber's grease and a rag are also very helpful for most repairs.

**How much does it cost to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

The cost to fix a leaky bathtub faucet yourself is usually minimal, often just the price of replacement washers, O-rings, or a new cartridge, which can range from a few dollars to $30-$50. Hiring a professional plumber can cost significantly more, typically $100-$300 or more depending on the complexity and location.
