A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by worn-out washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge. Fixing it typically involves turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet handle, and replacing the faulty components. This guide will walk you through the process step-by-step, helping you save water and avoid further damage.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge (matching your faucet type)
- Plumber's grease (silicone-based)
- Rag or towel
- Utility knife (optional, for stubborn caps)
- Flashlight (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Shut Off the Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, it's crucial to turn off the water. Locate the main shut-off valve for your home, usually in the basement, utility closet, or near the water heater. If your bathroom has individual shut-off valves for the tub, you can use those. Turn on the faucet briefly after shutting off the water to drain any remaining water from the lines.

### Step 2: 2. Remove the Faucet Handle

Most faucet handles have a decorative cap that conceals a screw. Gently pry off this cap with a flathead screwdriver or utility knife. Once exposed, use the appropriate screwdriver (usually Phillips) to unscrew the handle screw. Carefully pull the handle straight off the stem; it might require a little wiggling.

### Step 3: 3. Access the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

Beneath the handle, you'll find a decorative escutcheon plate or a packing nut/bonnet nut. Remove any screws holding the escutcheon plate, then carefully pull it away. Use an adjustable wrench to loosen and remove the large packing nut or bonnet nut that holds the faucet stem or cartridge in place. Turn counter-clockwise to loosen.

### Step 4: 4. Inspect and Replace Worn Components

Carefully pull out the faucet stem or cartridge. Examine the rubber washers, O-rings, and seals for signs of wear, cracking, or deterioration. These are the most common culprits for leaks. Replace any worn parts with new, identical ones. If your faucet uses a cartridge, you'll need to replace the entire cartridge unit.

### Step 5: 5. Clean and Lubricate

Before reassembling, take the opportunity to clean any mineral deposits or grime from the faucet body and the stem/cartridge. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings and threaded parts. This helps create a better seal and makes future repairs easier.

### Step 6: 6. Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully insert the newly repaired stem or cartridge back into the faucet body, ensuring it's properly aligned. Hand-tighten the packing nut or bonnet nut, then use your adjustable wrench to tighten it snugly, but do not overtighten. Reattach the escutcheon plate, faucet handle, and decorative cap in reverse order of disassembly.

### Step 7: 7. Restore Water and Test for Leaks

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Return to the bathtub and check the faucet for any leaks. Turn the faucet on and off a few times to ensure it operates smoothly and that the leak has stopped. If the leak persists, you may need to re-check your work or consider replacing the entire faucet if the body is corroded. In some cases, a full replacement may be more effective. ## Tips & Tricks

- Before purchasing replacement parts, take photos of your faucet and the removed components, or even bring the old parts to the hardware store to ensure you get exact matches.
- Place a towel or rag over the drain opening to prevent small screws or parts from falling down the drain during disassembly.
- If your faucet is old or severely corroded, it might be challenging to remove parts. Apply penetrating oil and let it sit for a while before trying again.
- Consider replacing the washers/O-rings on both the hot and cold handles, even if only one is leaking, as they are likely the same age and will wear out similarly.
- Avoid overtightening screws or nuts, as this can strip threads or crack plastic components.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the main water supply before attempting any faucet repair to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ Do not use excessive force when prying or tightening components, as this can cause damage to the faucet or plumbing.
- ⚠️ Be careful with small parts; they can easily be lost or fall down the drain.
- ⚠️ If you are unsure at any point, it's best to consult a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

Fixing a leaky bathtub faucet typically involves turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet handle and stem, identifying and replacing worn-out components like washers, O-rings, or the cartridge, and then reassembling the faucet. This guide provides step-by-step instructions to help you through the process.

**What causes a leaky bathtub faucet?**

The most common causes of a leaky bathtub faucet are worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet mechanism. Mineral deposits can also build up, preventing a proper seal. Over time, these parts naturally degrade and lose their ability to prevent water from dripping.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

You'll generally need a screwdriver (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, pliers, replacement washers/O-rings/cartridge specific to your faucet model, and plumber's grease. A rag or towel is also useful for cleanup and preventing parts from falling into the drain.

**Can I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself?**

Yes, most leaky bathtub faucets can be fixed by a DIY enthusiast with basic tools and some patience. The process usually involves replacing a few inexpensive parts. If you follow a clear guide and take precautions, you can successfully repair your faucet and save on plumber costs.

**How to fix bathtub faucet if it's still dripping after repair?**

If your bathtub faucet is still dripping after a repair, double-check that all replacement parts are the correct size and type for your faucet. Ensure all nuts and screws are tightened snugly (but not overtightened). You might have missed a worn O-ring, or the faucet body itself could be corroded, indicating a need for full replacement.

**How long does it take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For a typical leaky bathtub faucet, the repair process usually takes between 30 minutes to an hour for someone with basic DIY experience. This time can vary depending on the complexity of your faucet model and how easily the old components can be removed.
