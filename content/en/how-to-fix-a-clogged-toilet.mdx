Fixing a clogged toilet usually involves a few straightforward steps, often starting with a plunger. If that doesn't work, household items like dish soap and hot water can often break down blockages, or you might need to use a toilet auger for more stubborn clogs.

## What You Need

- Rubber gloves
- Old towels or newspaper
- Flange-style plunger
- Dish soap
- Hot water (not boiling)
- Toilet auger/snake (optional)
- Bucket

## Steps

### Step 1: Prepare the Area and Prevent Overflow

Before you begin, put on rubber gloves to maintain hygiene. Lay down old towels or newspaper around the base of the toilet to protect your bathroom floor from splashes and potential overflows. If the water level in the bowl is already very high, immediately turn off the water supply valve located behind the toilet at the wall to prevent further filling and spillage.

### Step 2: Use the Right Plunger and Technique

Ensure you are using a flange-style plunger, which has an extended rubber lip designed to create a better seal in the toilet's drain opening. Position the plunger firmly over the drain, ensuring it forms a complete seal. This seal is crucial for creating the necessary suction and pressure to dislodge the clog effectively.

### Step 3: Plunge with Vigor and Consistency

Push the plunger down with force to expel air, then pull it back up sharply without breaking the seal. Repeat this vigorous plunging motion 10-15 times. The goal is to create a strong back-and-forth pressure wave in the drain that can loosen and push the blockage through the pipe. After several plunges, quickly pull the plunger up to see if the water drains.

### Step 4: Try the Soap and Hot Water Method (No Plunger)

If plunging doesn't work or you don't have a plunger, pour about half a cup of dish soap into the toilet bowl. Follow this with a gallon of hot (but not boiling) water, poured from waist height to add some force. Let it sit for 20-30 minutes; the soap helps lubricate the clog, and the hot water helps break it down, allowing it to slide through the pipes. This method is often effective for organic clogs.

### Step 5: Consider a Toilet Auger for Stubborn Clogs

For very stubborn clogs that resist plunging and the soap/water method, a toilet auger (also known as a closet auger or toilet snake) is your next best tool. This specialized tool is designed with a protective sleeve to prevent scratching the porcelain. Insert the auger into the drain opening, crank the handle to feed the cable, and gently push through the clog until it breaks up or you can pull it out. Be careful not to force it.

### Step 6: Flush and Verify the Clog is Gone

Once you believe the clog is cleared, if you turned off the water supply, turn it back on. Then, flush the toilet to verify that the water drains freely and the clog is completely gone. You may need to flush a couple of times to ensure everything is flowing smoothly and the bowl refills correctly. If the water level looks normal, you've succeeded.

### Step 7: Clean Up and Sanitize

After successfully fixing the clog, it's important to clean and sanitize the area. Dispose of any used towels or newspaper, clean your plunger and auger thoroughly with disinfectant, and wipe down the toilet and surrounding floor with an appropriate bathroom cleaner. This ensures proper hygiene and leaves your bathroom fresh.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always use a flange-style plunger designed specifically for toilets for the best seal and effectiveness.
- Never flush again if the water level is high after an initial flush; it will likely overflow.
- Lay down old towels or newspaper around the toilet base before starting to protect your floor from splashes.
- Keep the bathroom well-ventilated, especially if using any cleaning agents.
- For preventive maintenance, avoid flushing non-biodegradable items like wet wipes, paper towels, or feminine hygiene products.
- If using a toilet auger, feed it slowly and gently to avoid damaging the toilet porcelain.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not use chemical drain cleaners unless they are specifically labeled as safe for toilets, as they can damage pipes, porcelain, or septic systems.
- ⚠️ Never pour boiling water into a toilet, as the extreme temperature difference can crack the porcelain bowl.
- ⚠️ If the toilet is actively overflowing, immediately turn off the water supply valve located behind the toilet to stop the flow.
- ⚠️ Avoid using a standard plumbing snake (often used for sinks) in a toilet, as its exposed metal can scratch or damage the porcelain finish.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How to fix a clogged toilet without a plunger?**

You can often fix a clogged toilet without a plunger by using a combination of dish soap and hot water. Pour about half a cup of dish soap into the bowl, followed by a gallon of hot (not boiling) water, and let it sit for 20-30 minutes. The soap lubricates the clog, and the hot water helps break it down.

**How to fix a clogged toilet with poop?**

To fix a clogged toilet with poop, start by trying a flange-style plunger vigorously. If that doesn't work, the dish soap and hot water method can often help break down organic matter. For very stubborn clogs, a toilet auger is effective at clearing out solid waste without damaging the toilet.

**What causes a toilet to clog in the first place?**

Toilets commonly clog due to flushing too much toilet paper at once, or flushing non-flushable items like wet wipes, paper towels, feminine hygiene products, or small toys. Sometimes, a clog can also indicate a deeper issue in your plumbing system or septic tank.

**When should I consider calling a professional plumber for a clogged toilet?**

You should call a professional plumber if your toilet remains clogged after trying several DIY methods, especially if it's a recurring issue or if multiple drains in your home are slow or clogged. This could indicate a more serious blockage in your main sewer line that requires professional tools and expertise.

**Are there any everyday items I can use to unclog a toilet?**

Yes, aside from a plunger, common household items can help. Dish soap, as detailed in the steps, is very effective. You can also try a mixture of baking soda and vinegar (pour a cup of baking soda, wait a few minutes, then add two cups of vinegar), though this method is generally less powerful than soap and hot water.

**How can I prevent my toilet from getting clogged again?**

To prevent future clogs, only flush human waste and toilet paper. Avoid flushing anything else, including 'flushable' wipes, which often don't break down adequately. Educate everyone in your household about proper toilet usage, and consider using less toilet paper per flush if over-flushing is an issue.

**Is it safe to use chemical drain cleaners in a toilet?**

It is generally not recommended to use standard chemical drain cleaners in a toilet. Many are too harsh and can damage the porcelain finish or the internal components of your toilet. They can also be harmful to your plumbing pipes or septic system. If you must use a chemical solution, ensure it is specifically labeled as safe for toilets.
