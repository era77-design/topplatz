Fixing a clogged toilet can be a frustrating but often simple DIY task. Most clogs can be resolved with basic tools and household items, saving you the expense of a plumber. Follow these straightforward steps to get your toilet flushing properly again.

## What You Need

- Plunger (flange plunger recommended)
- Rubber gloves
- Old towels or newspaper
- Bucket (optional, for scooping water)
- Dish soap
- Hot water (not boiling)
- Baking soda
- White vinegar
- Toilet brush (for cleaning)
- Toilet auger/snake (for stubborn clogs)

## Steps

### Step 1: Prepare the Area Safely

Before you begin, lay down old towels or newspaper around the base of the toilet. This will protect your bathroom floor from any potential splashes or overflows. Put on a pair of rubber gloves to maintain hygiene throughout the process.

### Step 2: Assess the Water Level

If the toilet bowl is nearly overflowing, do not flush again. Instead, carefully scoop some water out into a bucket until the water level is normal. This prevents messy overflows while you work and allows you to add hot water later if needed. Locate the water shut-off valve behind the toilet and turn it clockwise to stop water from refilling the bowl if there's a risk of overflow.

### Step 3: Plunge with Proper Technique

Place the plunger over the toilet drain, ensuring you create a tight seal. A flange plunger, designed with an extended rubber lip, is ideal for toilets. Push down slowly to expel air, then pull up sharply to create suction, repeating this vigorous motion 15-20 times. Break the seal occasionally to allow air to escape, then re-seal and continue plunging until the water drains.

### Step 4: Try Dish Soap and Hot Water

If plunging doesn't work or you don't have a plunger, pour about half a cup of liquid dish soap into the toilet bowl. Follow this by carefully pouring 2-3 cups of very hot (but not boiling) water into the bowl. The hot water helps soften the clog, and the soap acts as a lubricant, allowing the blockage to slide through the pipes. Let it sit for at least 20-30 minutes, or even a few hours, before attempting to flush.

### Step 5: Use Baking Soda and Vinegar

For another chemical-free alternative, pour one cup of baking soda into the toilet bowl. Follow it with two cups of white vinegar. The combination will create a fizzing reaction that can help break down the clog. Allow this mixture to sit for at least 30 minutes, or ideally overnight, before attempting to flush the toilet.

### Step 6: Employ a Toilet Auger (Snake)

If the previous methods fail, a toilet auger (also known as a closet auger or toilet snake) is the next step for stubborn clogs. Carefully insert the auger's end into the toilet drain, feeding the cable while cranking the handle. Once you feel resistance, you've hit the clog; continue cranking to break it up or hook it, then slowly retract the auger. This tool is specifically designed to navigate the toilet's curved traps without damaging the porcelain.

### Step 7: Flush and Clean Up

Once you believe the clog is clear, flush the toilet to test if the water drains normally. If it does, give it a few more flushes to ensure the clog is fully gone. Clean up any spills, disinfect the toilet, and store your tools. If the clog persists after trying all these methods, it may indicate a deeper issue requiring a professional plumber.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always use a flange plunger for toilets, as its design creates a better seal than a cup plunger.
- Wear rubber gloves and eye protection to protect yourself from germs and splashes.
- Be patient; sometimes, methods like dish soap and hot water need time to work effectively.
- Never flush anything other than human waste and toilet paper to prevent future clogs.
- Keep a dedicated bucket nearby for scooping out excess water if the toilet threatens to overflow.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not use chemical drain cleaners in toilets, as they can damage pipes, harm septic systems, and produce dangerous fumes.
- ⚠️ Avoid pouring boiling water into the toilet bowl, as the extreme temperature can crack the porcelain.
- ⚠️ If the toilet is overflowing, immediately turn off the water supply valve located behind the toilet to prevent flooding.
- ⚠️ If multiple drains in your home are backing up, or you hear gurgling from other fixtures, it may indicate a main sewer line clog, which requires professional help.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you fix a clogged toilet without a plunger?**

You can often fix a clogged toilet without a plunger by using a combination of dish soap and hot water, or baking soda and vinegar. These methods help to lubricate and break down the clog, allowing it to pass through the pipes naturally.

**How do you fix a clogged toilet with poop?**

A clogged toilet with poop can usually be cleared with a good quality plunger. If plunging doesn't work, try the dish soap and hot water method to help lubricate and break down the waste, making it easier for it to flush away.

**What is the best way to fix a severely clogged toilet?**

For a severely clogged toilet, after trying plunging and the dish soap method, a toilet auger (also known as a closet auger or toilet snake) is often the most effective tool. It's designed to navigate the toilet's curves and break up or retrieve stubborn blockages without damaging the bowl.

**How long should I wait for dish soap and hot water to work on a clog?**

After pouring dish soap and hot water into the bowl, wait at least 20-30 minutes, or even up to a few hours, for it to work. The soap needs time to lubricate the clog, and the hot water helps to soften it, making it easier to dislodge.

**Can I use bleach to clear a clogged toilet?**

No, you should not use bleach to clear a clogged toilet. Bleach is not effective at breaking down clogs and can create dangerous fumes when mixed with other cleaning agents or chemicals already present in the toilet bowl.

**When should I call a plumber for a clogged toilet?**

You should call a plumber if you've tried all the DIY methods (plunging, soap, auger) and the toilet remains clogged, or if the clog recurs frequently. Also, call a plumber if you suspect a main sewer line issue, indicated by multiple drains backing up or sewage odors.

**What causes a toilet to clog frequently?**

Frequent toilet clogs are often caused by flushing non-flushable items like 'flushable' wipes, excessive toilet paper, feminine hygiene products, or paper towels. Low-flow toilets can also be more prone to clogs if not flushed properly or maintained.
