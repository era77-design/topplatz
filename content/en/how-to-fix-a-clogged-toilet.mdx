A clogged toilet can be a frustrating problem, but many clogs can be fixed with basic tools and a little know-how. Most often, a sturdy plunger is all you need to clear common obstructions. For more stubborn clogs, household items like dish soap or a toilet auger can come to the rescue.

## What You Need

- Rubber gloves
- Flange plunger (toilet plunger)
- Old towels or newspaper
- Bucket
- Dish soap
- Hot water (not boiling)
- Toilet auger or snake (optional)
- All-purpose cleaner and disinfectant

## Steps

### Step 1: Assess the Situation and Prepare

First, resist the urge to flush again, as this could cause the toilet to overflow. Lift the toilet tank lid and close the flapper to stop water from entering the bowl if it's nearing the rim. Lay down old towels or newspaper around the base of the toilet to protect your floor from splashes or potential overflows.

### Step 2: Plunge with Purpose

Position your flange plunger over the drain hole, ensuring a complete seal. Push down slowly to expel air, then pull up sharply to create suction, repeating this vigorous plunging motion 15-20 times. Maintain the seal as best as possible throughout the process, then remove the plunger quickly to break the suction and see if the water drains.

### Step 3: Try the Dish Soap and Hot Water Trick

If plunging doesn't work or you don't have a plunger, pour about half a cup of liquid dish soap into the toilet bowl. Follow this with a gallon of hot (not boiling) water, poured from waist height to add some force. Let it sit for 20-30 minutes; the soap helps lubricate the clog, and the hot water can help break it down.

### Step 4: Use a Toilet Auger or Snake

For stubborn clogs that are deeper in the drain, a toilet auger (also known as a closet auger or toilet snake) is the next step. Insert the auger's coiled wire into the drain hole, pushing and cranking the handle to navigate the bends of the toilet trap. Continue until you feel resistance, then rotate and push to break up or retrieve the obstruction.

### Step 5: Flush and Confirm the Fix

Once you believe the clog is clear, carefully flush the toilet to test the drainage. If the water flows freely, the clog is gone. If the water still rises too high, repeat the appropriate step or consider calling a professional plumber.

### Step 6: Clean Up and Disinfect

Thoroughly clean and disinfect your plunger, auger, and any tools used. Dispose of soiled towels and use an all-purpose cleaner to wipe down the toilet, floor, and surrounding areas. If an overflow occurred, ensure you thoroughly clean and dry the surrounding area to prevent issues like mold growth. For persistent moisture or mold concerns, consult our guide on [How to Remove Mold from Walls - Expert DIY Guide](/en/how-to-remove-mold-from-walls).

### Step 7: Prevent Future Clogs

Adopt good toilet habits to avoid future clogs. Only flush human waste and toilet paper, and avoid flushing excessive amounts of paper at once. Never flush items like wet wipes, cotton balls, dental floss, or feminine hygiene products, as these are common culprits for stubborn blockages.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always wear rubber gloves to protect yourself from germs and dirty water.
- A flange plunger, with its extended rubber flap, is more effective for toilets than a cup-style plunger.
- Patience is key; some methods require time for the clog to loosen or dissolve.
- Keep a dedicated bucket nearby for any water overflow or to hold your tools.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never use chemical drain cleaners in your toilet. They can damage pipes, harm your septic system, and release dangerous fumes.
- ⚠️ Do not use boiling water, as it can crack the porcelain bowl or melt PVC pipes.
- ⚠️ If the clog is persistent, or if you suspect a deeper issue in your plumbing system, it's best to call a licensed plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How to fix a clogged toilet without a plunger?**

You can often fix a clogged toilet without a plunger by using a combination of dish soap and hot water. Pour about half a cup of liquid dish soap into the bowl, followed by a gallon of hot (not boiling) water, and let it sit for 20-30 minutes. If this doesn't work, a toilet auger (snake) is the next best tool.

**How to fix a clogged toilet with poop?**

To fix a clogged toilet with poop, the most effective method is usually a flange plunger. Create a strong seal over the drain and plunge vigorously to dislodge the material. If plunging doesn't work, the dish soap and hot water method can help break down organic matter, or a toilet auger can physically clear it.

**What causes a toilet to clog?**

Toilets commonly clog due to flushing non-flushable items like wet wipes, paper towels, excessive toilet paper, or feminine hygiene products. Sometimes, older toilets with weaker flushing power or issues with the plumbing vent can also contribute to frequent clogs.

**When should I call a plumber for a clogged toilet?**

You should call a plumber if you've tried multiple DIY methods and the clog persists, or if your toilet frequently clogs. Also, if multiple drains in your home are slow or clogged, it could indicate a main sewer line issue requiring professional attention.

**Can I use chemical drain cleaner in my toilet?**

No, it is strongly advised not to use chemical drain cleaners in your toilet. These harsh chemicals can damage the toilet's porcelain finish, corrode pipes, harm your septic system, and pose significant health risks due to fumes.

**How can I prevent my toilet from clogging again?**

To prevent future clogs, only flush human waste and toilet paper. Avoid flushing anything else, including 'flushable' wipes, cotton balls, dental floss, or excessive amounts of toilet paper. Regularly clean your toilet and ensure its flushing mechanism is working efficiently.

**What's the difference between a regular plunger and a toilet plunger?**

A 'regular' plunger (cup plunger) is typically flat-bottomed and designed for flat surfaces like sink drains. A toilet plunger, or flange plunger, has an extended rubber flap or 'flange' that fits snugly into the toilet's drain hole, creating a much better seal for effective plunging in toilets.
