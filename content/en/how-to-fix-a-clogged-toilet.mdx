Fixing a clogged toilet often requires just a few simple tools and techniques. Most minor clogs can be resolved quickly using methods like plunging, hot water, or dish soap. Before starting, assess the water level to prevent overflow and prepare your workspace.

## What You Need

- Rubber gloves
- Old towels or newspapers
- Toilet plunger (flange plunger recommended)
- Liquid dish soap
- Hot water (from tap, not boiling)
- Baking soda (optional)
- White vinegar (optional)
- Wire coat hanger or toilet auger (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Stop the Water and Protect Your Floor

Before attempting any fix, lift the toilet tank lid and locate the flapper. If the water level is rising, push the flapper down to stop the flow into the bowl. Alternatively, turn the water supply valve off at the wall behind the toilet. Lay down old towels or newspapers around the base of the toilet to catch any spills or splashes and protect your flooring.

### Step 2: Use a Plunger Effectively

Position your plunger over the drain hole, ensuring a tight seal around the opening. Push down slowly to remove air from the plunger, then plunge vigorously with an up-and-down motion for 15-20 seconds. Maintain the seal as much as possible throughout the process. On the last upstroke, break the seal quickly to create a strong suction force. Repeat this process several times, flushing only once if the water level has dropped.

### Step 3: Try Dish Soap and Hot Water

If plunging doesn't work or you don't have one, pour about half a cup of liquid dish soap into the toilet bowl. Follow this with a gallon of hot (but not boiling) tap water. The hot water helps break down the clog, and the dish soap acts as a lubricant, helping the clog slide down. Let it sit for at least 20-30 minutes, or even a few hours for stubborn clogs, before attempting to flush.

### Step 4: Create a Baking Soda and Vinegar Solution

For a natural effervescent reaction, pour one cup of baking soda into the toilet bowl, followed by two cups of white vinegar. The fizzing action can help break apart some organic clogs. Let this mixture sit for at least 30 minutes, or preferably overnight, before attempting to flush or pour more hot water into the bowl. This method is particularly effective for softer, organic blockages.

### Step 5: Fashion a DIY Toilet Snake

Unbend a wire coat hanger until it's mostly straight, keeping a small hook at one end. Wrap a cloth or rag around the hooked end to prevent scratching the porcelain of your toilet bowl. Gently push the hanger into the drain opening, twisting and prodding to break up or pull out the clog. Once you feel the clog give way, carefully remove the hanger and flush.

### Step 6: Patience and Repeat if Necessary

Sometimes, a stubborn clog just needs more time or a repeated attempt with the chosen method. If the water level has gone down after an hour, try flushing once to see if the clog has cleared. If it's still slow or partially clogged, repeat one of the non-plunger methods. Avoid repeated flushing if the bowl remains full, as this risks overflow and makes the situation worse. Give methods like dish soap and hot water ample time to work effectively.

### Step 7: When to Call a Professional Plumber

If you've tried several methods and the toilet remains stubbornly clogged, or if multiple toilets and drains in your home are backed up, it's time to call a professional plumber. A persistent clog can indicate a deeper issue in your plumbing system, such as a main sewer line blockage, which requires specialized tools and expertise to resolve safely and effectively.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always wear rubber gloves to protect your hands from germs and dirty water.
- Invest in a high-quality flange plunger; they create a better seal for toilet drains than standard cup plungers.
- Never flush anything other than human waste and toilet paper to prevent future clogs.
- Keep a small trash can next to the toilet for disposing of wipes, cotton balls, dental floss, and other non-flushable items.
- If you have a persistent slow-draining toilet, consider having your main drain line inspected by a professional.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never use chemical drain cleaners. They can damage pipes, are corrosive, and release toxic fumes that are harmful to your health.
- ⚠️ Do not use boiling water in your toilet bowl, as the extreme temperature difference can crack the porcelain.
- ⚠️ Avoid flushing repeatedly if the toilet bowl is full, as this will almost certainly lead to an overflow.
- ⚠️ Be cautious when using a wire coat hanger to avoid scratching the ceramic of your toilet bowl, which can lead to permanent damage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I fix a clogged toilet?**

To fix a clogged toilet, start by using a plunger effectively to create suction. If that doesn't work, try pouring a mixture of dish soap and hot water into the bowl, allowing it to sit and break down the clog. For tougher clogs, a baking soda and vinegar solution or a carefully fashioned DIY toilet snake can be helpful in dislodging the blockage.

**How to fix a clogged toilet without a plunger?**

You can fix a clogged toilet without a plunger by using dish soap and hot water. Pour about half a cup of dish soap into the bowl, followed by a gallon of hot tap water, and let it sit for 20-30 minutes to lubricate the clog. Another effective method involves using a mixture of baking soda and vinegar, or carefully snaking the drain with a straightened wire coat hanger to manually clear the obstruction.

**How to fix a clogged toilet with poop?**

If your toilet is clogged with poop, the best initial approach is to use a plunger vigorously to try and dislodge it. If a plunger isn't available or effective, pour dish soap and hot (not boiling) water into the bowl. The soap helps lubricate, and the hot water aids in breaking down the waste, making it easier for it to pass. Give it ample time to work before attempting another flush.

**Can I use bleach or chemical drain cleaners to fix a clogged toilet?**

No, it is strongly advised against using bleach or chemical drain cleaners. These products can be corrosive to your pipes, potentially causing damage, and they release toxic fumes that are harmful to your health. They are often ineffective against stubborn toilet clogs and can make the situation more dangerous for you or a plumber.

**How long does it take for a clogged toilet to drain on its own?**

A minor clogged toilet, such as one with just a small amount of toilet paper, might eventually drain on its own within an hour or two. However, more significant clogs caused by larger items or excessive waste may take several hours or even overnight to slowly drain, or they might not drain at all without manual intervention. Patience is key, but don't wait indefinitely if the clog persists.

**When should I call a professional plumber for a clogged toilet?**

You should call a professional plumber if you've tried multiple DIY methods (plunging, soap/water, baking soda/vinegar, DIY snake) and the toilet remains stubbornly clogged. Additionally, if you notice that multiple toilets, sinks, or drains in your home are backing up simultaneously, it's a strong indicator of a more serious issue like a main sewer line blockage, which requires professional equipment and expertise to resolve.
