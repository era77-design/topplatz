To fix a clogged toilet, first prevent overflow by shutting off the water supply. Then, use a flange plunger with firm, consistent thrusts to dislodge the blockage. For clogs without a plunger, a mixture of dish soap and hot water can often help break down the obstruction.

## What You Need

- Flange plunger
- Rubber gloves
- Old towels or newspaper
- Liquid dish soap
- Hot water (not boiling)
- Bucket (optional, for water removal)
- Toilet auger (drain snake)

## Steps

### Step 1: Stop the Water Flow

If your toilet bowl is filling up and threatening to overflow, immediately lift the tank lid and push down the flapper to stop water from entering the bowl. Then, locate the small knob on the wall or floor behind the toilet, which is the water shut-off valve, and turn it counter-clockwise until the water flow stops completely. This crucial first step prevents a potential mess.

### Step 2: Prepare Your Workspace

Lay down old towels or newspaper around the base of the toilet to protect your bathroom floor from splashes and spills. Put on a pair of sturdy rubber gloves to maintain hygiene and protect your hands from dirty water. Ensure the bathroom is well-ventilated, especially if you anticipate using any cleaning agents.

### Step 3: Plunge with Purpose

Position the flange plunger firmly over the drain hole, ensuring you create a complete seal. Push down slowly to expel any air, then pull up and push down vigorously 15-20 times, maintaining the seal throughout. The goal is to create strong suction and pressure that will dislodge the clog. After several thrusts, quickly pull the plunger up to break the seal and see if the water begins to drain.

### Step 4: Try the Dish Soap and Hot Water Method

If plunging doesn't work or you don't have a plunger, pour about half a cup of liquid dish soap into the toilet bowl. Follow this with a gallon of very hot (but not boiling) water, pouring it from a height to add some force. Let the mixture sit for 20-30 minutes; the dish soap helps lubricate the clog, and the hot water aids in breaking it down. After waiting, try to flush.

### Step 5: Use a Toilet Auger (Drain Snake)

For more stubborn clogs that resist plunging and the soap method, a toilet auger (also known as a closet auger) is highly effective. Carefully feed the auger's cable into the toilet drain, turning the handle as you go to navigate bends and break up the obstruction. Continue until you feel the clog give way, then slowly retract the auger, cleaning it as you pull it out.

### Step 6: Flush and Confirm

Once you believe the clog is clear, flush the toilet carefully. If the water drains normally and the bowl refills without issue, the clog is gone. If the water still drains slowly or partially, you may need to repeat the plunging or auger steps. Sometimes, a second or third attempt is necessary to completely clear all debris.

### Step 7: Clean and Sanitize

After successfully clearing the clog, clean your plunger and any other tools used with a disinfectant. Dispose of any soiled towels or newspapers properly. Sanitize the toilet area, including the bowl, seat, and floor, to maintain a hygienic bathroom environment after the task is complete.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always use a flange plunger designed specifically for toilets, as it creates a better seal than a standard cup plunger.
- Avoid using chemical drain cleaners in toilets; they can damage pipes, are often ineffective for toilet clogs, and can create dangerous fumes.
- Prevent future clogs by only flushing human waste and toilet paper. Never flush wipes (even 'flushable' ones), paper towels, or feminine hygiene products.
- If you have a low-flow toilet and experience frequent clogs, consider flushing twice to ensure all waste is cleared effectively.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never use boiling water to unclog a toilet, as the extreme heat can crack the porcelain bowl.
- ⚠️ Do not mix different chemical cleaners, as this can create dangerous and toxic fumes. It's best to avoid harsh chemicals altogether in toilets.
- ⚠️ Always wear rubber gloves to protect your hands from germs and dirty water during the unclogging process.
- ⚠️ If the toilet remains clogged after multiple attempts, or if you suspect a deeper issue within your plumbing system, it's best to call a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you fix a clogged toilet without a plunger?**

You can try the dish soap and hot water method. Pour about half a cup of liquid dish soap into the bowl, followed by a gallon of very hot (but not boiling) water. Let it sit for 20-30 minutes to help lubricate and dissolve the clog before attempting to flush.

**What do you do if your toilet is clogged with poop?**

If your toilet is clogged with poop, first try plunging vigorously with a flange plunger. If that doesn't work, the dish soap and hot water method can help break down organic matter. For very stubborn clogs, a toilet auger is highly effective at clearing solid blockages.

**How do you fix a stubborn clogged toilet that won't flush?**

For a stubborn clog that resists plunging, a toilet auger (or closet auger) is usually the most effective tool. Carefully feed it into the drain, turning the handle to break up the obstruction. You may need to repeat plunging after using the auger to fully clear the debris.

**Can I use dish soap to unclog a toilet?**

Yes, dish soap can be an effective and safe way to unclog a toilet, especially for minor clogs. The soap acts as a lubricant, helping the blockage slide down the drain. Always follow it with hot (not boiling) water and give it time to work.

**When should I call a plumber for a clogged toilet?**

You should call a plumber if your toilet remains clogged after trying several DIY methods, if you suspect a main drain line issue, or if multiple toilets or drains in your home are experiencing clogs. Recurring clogs also indicate a need for professional inspection to identify underlying problems.

**Is it safe to use chemical drain cleaners in a toilet?**

It is generally not recommended to use chemical drain cleaners in toilets. They can be harsh on pipes, may not be effective for all types of clogs, and can release dangerous fumes. Manual methods like plunging or augering are safer and often more effective for toilet blockages.

**How can I prevent toilet clogs in the future?**

To prevent future clogs, only flush human waste and toilet paper. Avoid flushing items like 'flushable' wipes, paper towels, cotton balls, dental floss, or feminine hygiene products. Consider using less toilet paper per flush if clogs are frequent, especially with older or low-flow toilets.
