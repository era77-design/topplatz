A clogged toilet is a common household problem that can often be resolved with basic tools and techniques. From using a plunger to more advanced methods, most clogs can be cleared without calling a plumber, saving you time and money.

## What You Need

- Plunger (flange type recommended)
- Rubber gloves
- Old towels or rags
- Bucket
- Liquid dish soap
- Hot water (not boiling)
- Toilet auger (optional)
- Baking soda (optional)
- White vinegar (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Prepare the Area and Prevent Overflow

Immediately lift the tank lid and push down the flapper if water is rising to stop the flow, preventing an overflow onto your bathroom floor. Lay old towels or rags around the base of the toilet to protect the floor from any splashes or spills. Always put on rubber gloves to maintain hygiene and protect your hands throughout the process.

### Step 2: Try the Plunger Method First

Ensure there's enough water in the bowl to cover the plunger's rubber cup completely. If not, add some water. Position the plunger firmly over the drain hole, creating a tight seal, then push down gently to release air. Plunge vigorously with an up-and-down motion for 15-20 seconds, breaking the seal quickly on the last pull to create strong suction. Repeat this process several times until the water drains freely.

### Step 3: Use Dish Soap and Hot Water (No Plunger Needed)

If you don't have a plunger or for minor clogs, squirt about half a cup of liquid dish soap directly into the toilet bowl. Let the soap sit for 5-10 minutes to lubricate the clog. Slowly pour a gallon of hot (but not boiling) water into the bowl from waist height; the heat helps break down the clog, and the soap acts as a lubricant. Wait 20-30 minutes for it to work before attempting to flush.

### Step 4: Apply Baking Soda and Vinegar Solution

For a natural and eco-friendly approach, pour one cup of baking soda into the toilet bowl. Follow this with two cups of white vinegar; the mixture will fizz and bubble, which helps to break down organic blockages. Let this solution sit for at least 30 minutes, or preferably overnight, before attempting to flush and check if the clog has cleared.

### Step 5: Employ a Toilet Auger for Stubborn Clogs

If plunging and other methods fail, a toilet auger (also known as a closet auger or snake) is designed to clear deeper clogs without damaging the porcelain. Carefully insert the auger's end into the toilet drain, feeding the cable by turning the handle. When you feel resistance, rotate the handle to break up or hook the clog. Slowly pull the auger back out, clearing any material caught on the end, then flush to check.

### Step 6: Assess and Repeat or Call a Professional

After attempting a method, wait a few minutes, then flush the toilet once to see if the clog has cleared. If it drains slowly or not at all, do not flush again immediately to avoid overflow. You can repeat a method or try another from this guide. If multiple attempts fail and the toilet remains stubbornly clogged, it might indicate a more serious blockage further down the drainpipe, requiring a professional plumber.


## Tips & Tricks

- Do not flush the toilet multiple times if it's already clogged, as this will likely lead to an overflow.
- Invest in a high-quality plunger, specifically a flange plunger designed for toilets, as it creates a better seal.
- Prevent future clogs by only flushing human waste and toilet paper. Avoid 'flushable' wipes, feminine hygiene products, or excessive paper.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never use harsh chemical drain cleaners in your toilet. They can damage pipes, harm the environment, and create dangerous fumes or splashes.
- ⚠️ Use hot water, not boiling water, as extremely hot water can crack the porcelain bowl.
- ⚠️ Always wear rubber gloves to protect your hands from unsanitary conditions and potential chemical splashes if you've already tried a cleaner.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you fix a clogged toilet?**

You can fix a clogged toilet by first trying a plunger, which is often the most effective method. If that doesn't work, you can use a mixture of dish soap and hot water, or baking soda and vinegar. For very stubborn clogs, a toilet auger is a reliable tool.

**How to fix a clogged toilet without a plunger?**

To fix a clogged toilet without a plunger, pour about half a cup of liquid dish soap into the bowl, let it sit for 5-10 minutes, then slowly add a gallon of hot (not boiling) water. Alternatively, you can use a combination of one cup baking soda followed by two cups white vinegar, allowing it to sit for at least 30 minutes.

**How to fix a clogged toilet with poop?**

If your toilet is clogged with poop, the most effective first step is typically to use a plunger with a strong, consistent motion. If plunging doesn't clear it, try the dish soap and hot water method to help break down the material. For persistent blockages, a toilet auger can directly tackle and clear the clog.

**What should I do if my toilet is clogged and overflowing?**

Immediately lift the tank lid and push down the rubber flapper at the bottom of the tank to stop water from entering the bowl. This will prevent further overflow. Once the water flow stops, you can then begin to address the clog using a plunger or other methods.

**Can I use chemical drain cleaner for a clogged toilet?**

It is generally not recommended to use harsh chemical drain cleaners for clogged toilets. These chemicals can be corrosive to your plumbing, harmful to the environment, and dangerous if they splash back. They often don't work effectively on toilet clogs and can pose a risk to anyone who subsequently tries to clear the clog manually.

**How can I prevent my toilet from getting clogged?**

To prevent clogs, only flush human waste and toilet paper. Avoid flushing items such as so-called 'flushable' wet wipes, feminine hygiene products, cotton balls, dental floss, or excessive amounts of toilet paper, as these are common culprits for blockages.

**When should I call a professional plumber?**

You should call a professional plumber if your toilet remains stubbornly clogged after trying several DIY methods, or if you notice multiple toilets or drains in your home are experiencing issues. These symptoms can indicate a more serious blockage further down the main sewer line, which requires expert attention.
