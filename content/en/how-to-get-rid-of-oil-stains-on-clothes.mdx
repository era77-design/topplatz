To get rid of oil stains on clothes, act quickly by blotting excess oil and then applying an absorbent powder like baking soda or cornstarch. Follow up with a grease-cutting dish soap before washing the garment as usual. Always check the stain's removal before drying, as heat can set the stain permanently.

## What You Need

- Paper towels or clean cloth
- Baking soda or cornstarch
- Old toothbrush or small brush
- Liquid dish soap (grease-cutting)
- Laundry detergent
- Washing machine

## Steps

### Step 1: Blot Excess Oil Immediately

As soon as you notice an oil stain, use a clean paper towel or cloth to gently blot away any excess oil. Do not rub the stain, as this can spread it and push the oil deeper into the fabric fibers. The goal is to absorb as much surface oil as possible without further embedding it.

> 💡 **Tip:** Act fast! The sooner you treat an oil stain, the easier it will be to remove.

### Step 2: Apply an Absorbent Powder

Sprinkle a generous amount of an absorbent powder like baking soda, cornstarch, or even baby powder directly onto the oil stain. Ensure the entire stained area is covered. These powders are excellent at drawing out oil from fabric fibers.

> 💡 **Tip:** For delicate fabrics, use a very light touch when applying the powder.

### Step 3: Let the Powder Absorb

Allow the absorbent powder to sit on the stain for at least 15-30 minutes, or even longer for larger or older stains. As it sits, the powder will work to soak up the grease. You might even see the powder clump up slightly as it absorbs the oil.

> 💡 **Tip:** For very stubborn or set-in stains, let the powder sit for several hours or overnight.

### Step 4: Brush Off the Powder

After the powder has had time to absorb the oil, use an old toothbrush or a small brush to gently brush off the powder. You should see a noticeable reduction in the stain's appearance. If the stain is still prominent, you can repeat the absorbent powder step.

> 💡 **Tip:** Brush the powder into a trash can to avoid spreading it around.

### Step 5: Apply Dish Soap to the Stain

Apply a small amount of liquid dish soap directly onto the remaining oil stain. Dish soap is designed to cut through grease. Gently rub the soap into the stain with your fingers or a soft brush, working it into the fabric. Let it sit for 5-10 minutes.

> 💡 **Tip:** Use a clear or light-colored dish soap to avoid adding new color to the fabric.

### Step 6: Wash the Garment

Wash the garment in the hottest water safe for the fabric, along with your regular laundry detergent. Check the care label for specific temperature recommendations. Avoid washing with other clothes if the stain is particularly large or oily, to prevent transfer.

> 💡 **Tip:** Consider adding a cup of white vinegar to the wash cycle for extra stain-fighting power.

### Step 7: Inspect Before Drying

Before placing the garment in the dryer, carefully inspect the stained area to ensure the oil stain is completely gone. If any trace of the stain remains, repeat the entire process from applying dish soap onwards. Drying a garment with an oil stain will set it, making it much harder, if not impossible, to remove later.

> 💡 **Tip:** Air dry the garment if you are unsure if the stain is completely gone, as this prevents heat from setting it.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always test any stain removal product on an inconspicuous area of the fabric first to ensure it doesn't cause discoloration.
- For delicate fabrics like silk or wool, avoid harsh scrubbing and opt for gentle blotting and soaking methods.
- If the stain is old and set, a pre-treatment with a commercial stain remover designed for grease might be necessary before following these steps.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not put clothes with oil stains into the dryer until you are absolutely certain the stain is gone, as the heat will permanently set the stain.
- ⚠️ Avoid using harsh chemical solvents on delicate fabrics without first testing them or consulting a professional cleaner.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can these methods remove old or set-in oil stains?**

Yes, these methods can be effective for old or set-in oil stains, but they might require multiple applications or longer soaking times. For very stubborn stains, you might need to repeat the absorbent powder and dish soap steps several times.

**What if I don't have baking soda or cornstarch?**

If you don't have baking soda or cornstarch, you can use other absorbent powders like baby powder, chalk (crushed), or even plain white flour as a substitute. The key is to use something that will draw the oil out of the fabric.

**Is it safe to use dish soap on all fabrics?**

Generally, a small amount of mild, clear liquid dish soap is safe for most washable fabrics. However, always test it on an inconspicuous area first, especially for delicate materials like silk or rayon, to ensure no discoloration or damage occurs.

**Can I use boiling water to remove oil stains?**

While hot water is good for oil, boiling water can damage certain fabrics or set other types of stains. It's best to use the hottest water temperature recommended on the garment's care label, rather than boiling water.

**Why is it important not to dry the garment if the stain is still there?**

Drying a garment with an oil stain will expose it to high heat, which causes the oil to bond more deeply and permanently with the fabric fibers. Once set by heat, oil stains become significantly more difficult, if not impossible, to remove.

**What if the oil stain is on a 'dry clean only' item?**

For 'dry clean only' items, it's best to blot the excess oil and then take the garment to a professional dry cleaner as soon as possible. Point out the stain to them, as they have specialized solvents and techniques for such fabrics.
