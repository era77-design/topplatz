Removing a sink stopper is a straightforward task that can help resolve slow drains, facilitate thorough cleaning, or allow for replacement. Most common pop-up stoppers are removed by disconnecting a pivot rod assembly located under the sink. This guide will walk you through the process step-by-step.

## What You Need

- Bucket or small container
- Pliers (slip-joint or channel-lock)
- Adjustable wrench (optional)
- Rag or towel
- Gloves (optional)
- Flashlight (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Prepare Your Workspace

Before you begin, clear out any items from the cabinet directly beneath your sink. Place a bucket or small container directly under the P-trap and drain assembly to catch any residual water or debris that might escape. Lay down a rag or towel to protect the cabinet floor and absorb spills. A flashlight can be helpful for better visibility in tight spaces.

### Step 2: Identify Your Stopper Type

Most bathroom sink stoppers are 'pop-up' types, controlled by a lift rod behind the faucet. However, some might be 'lift-and-turn' (unscrew a knob and then the stopper), 'push-pull' (just unscrew a knob), or simple 'drop-in' stoppers. This guide primarily focuses on the pop-up type, which requires working under the sink. If you have a different type, often you can simply unscrew or lift the stopper directly from the drain opening.

### Step 3: Locate the Pivot Rod Assembly

For a pop-up stopper, look under the sink at the back of the drainpipe. You'll see a horizontal metal rod, known as the pivot rod, entering the side of the drain assembly. This rod is usually connected to a vertical strap with multiple holes, called the clevis strap, which in turn connects to the lift rod coming from behind the faucet.

### Step 4: Disconnect the Clevis Strap and Pivot Rod

First, locate the spring clip or nut that connects the pivot rod to the clevis strap. Remove this clip or loosen the nut and detach the clevis strap from the pivot rod. This will free the vertical lift rod from the stopper mechanism. Ensure your bucket is still in place, as some water might drip out during this step.

### Step 5: Remove the Pivot Rod Retaining Nut

Next, you'll need to loosen and remove the large retaining nut (sometimes called a slip nut or packing nut) that holds the pivot rod in place where it enters the drainpipe. You can typically do this by hand, but if it's too tight, gently use pliers or an adjustable wrench. Be careful not to overtighten if reassembling later, as this can damage the plastic components.

### Step 6: Pull Out the Pivot Rod and Stopper

Once the retaining nut is off, carefully pull the pivot rod straight out from the side of the drain assembly. As you pull it out, the stopper inside the sink drain will become free. You can then lift the sink stopper directly out of the drain opening from above. Be prepared for any accumulated hair or gunk to come with it.

### Step 7: Clean and Reinstall (Optional)

With the stopper removed, you can thoroughly clean the stopper itself and the drain opening. This is an excellent opportunity to clear any hair or debris causing slow drainage. To reinstall, simply reverse the steps: insert the stopper, slide the pivot rod back in (ensuring it engages the stopper's hole), tighten the retaining nut, and reconnect the clevis strap to the pivot rod. Test the stopper's function and check for leaks.


## Tips & Tricks

- If your stopper is heavily caked with grime, soak it in a solution of warm water and white vinegar for easier cleaning.
- Always have a bucket or towel ready under the sink to catch any drips or spills when working on plumbing.
- When working under the sink, take a moment to inspect the area for any signs of dampness or mold, which can thrive in humid environments. If you spot any, consult guides like [How to Remove Mold from Walls Safely & Effectively](/en/how-to-remove-mold-from-walls) for proper remediation.
- Lubricate the pivot rod with a little plumber's grease or petroleum jelly before reinserting it to ensure smooth operation and prevent future sticking.
- If the stopper seems stuck, try gently wiggling it while lifting. Avoid excessive force, which could damage the drain assembly.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not overtighten plastic nuts, as this can crack them and cause leaks. Hand-tighten plus a quarter turn with pliers is usually sufficient.
- ⚠️ Be cautious of sharp edges or old, brittle plumbing components when working under the sink.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why would I need to remove my sink stopper?**

You might need to remove your sink stopper for several reasons, including clearing a clog, thoroughly cleaning accumulated grime and hair, or replacing a broken or old stopper. Regular cleaning helps maintain good drainage and hygiene.

**What if my sink stopper doesn't have a pivot rod?**

If your sink stopper doesn't have a pivot rod, it's likely a lift-and-turn, push-pull, or drop-in style. These types typically unscrew or simply lift out directly from the drain opening without needing to work under the sink. Check for a knob or cap that can be twisted off.

**How often should I clean my sink stopper?**

It's a good practice to clean your sink stopper every few months, or whenever you notice slow drainage or an unpleasant odor coming from the drain. Hair, soap scum, and other debris can quickly accumulate and cause issues.

**Can I replace my old sink stopper with a different type?**

Yes, it's often possible to replace your old sink stopper with a different type, such as a simpler drop-in stopper if you find the pop-up mechanism cumbersome. Ensure the new stopper is the correct size for your drain opening to ensure a proper seal.

**What if the pivot rod is stuck or difficult to remove?**

If the pivot rod is stuck, it might be due to corrosion or mineral buildup. Try applying a penetrating oil (like WD-40) and letting it sit for a few minutes before gently trying to wiggle and pull it out with pliers. Avoid excessive force to prevent damage.

**How do I prevent clogs after removing and cleaning the stopper?**

To prevent future clogs, consider installing a drain screen or hair catcher over your drain opening. Regularly flush your drain with hot water and baking soda followed by vinegar, and avoid pouring grease or coffee grounds down the sink.

**Is it normal for water to leak when removing the pivot rod?**

Yes, it's quite normal for a small amount of residual water to leak out when you remove the pivot rod, especially if the sink was recently used. This is why having a bucket or towel underneath is crucial to catch any drips and prevent damage to your cabinet.
