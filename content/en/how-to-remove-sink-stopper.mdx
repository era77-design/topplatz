Removing a sink stopper is a straightforward process, typically involving disconnecting a pivot rod or simply twisting and lifting. This allows for thorough cleaning of the drain, retrieval of lost items, or replacement of a faulty stopper. Most stoppers can be removed with basic tools or even by hand.

## What You Need

- Pliers (slip-joint or needle-nose)
- Bucket or small container
- Towel or rag
- Gloves (optional)
- Flashlight (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Identify Your Stopper Type

Before you begin, determine the type of stopper in your sink. The most common is a 'pop-up' stopper, which has a lift rod on the faucet that controls it. Other types include 'push-pull' or 'lift-and-turn' stoppers, which you simply pull up or twist and pull to remove. This guide primarily focuses on the pop-up type, as others are usually self-explanatory.

### Step 2: Prepare Your Workspace

Clear out any items from under the sink to give yourself ample room to work. Place a bucket or small container directly under the drain pipe assembly. This will catch any water or debris that might escape when you disconnect parts of the drain system. Lay a towel or rag on the cabinet floor for added protection.

### Step 3: Locate the Pivot Rod and Clevis Strap

Look directly underneath your sink, behind the basin. You'll see the drainpipe assembly, and a horizontal rod (the pivot rod) extending from it into a vertical strap (the clevis strap). This clevis strap is usually held in place by a spring clip or a nut that connects it to the lift rod from the faucet. This is the mechanism that operates your stopper.

### Step 4: Disconnect the Pivot Rod

First, remove the spring clip that connects the clevis strap to the lift rod. Then, locate the large nut on the drainpipe where the pivot rod enters. Using pliers, carefully loosen and unscrew this nut counter-clockwise. Once the nut is loose, gently pull the pivot rod straight out from the drainpipe. Have your bucket ready, as a small amount of water or gunk might drip out.

### Step 5: Remove the Stopper from Above

With the pivot rod completely removed, return to the top of the sink. You should now be able to simply lift the sink stopper straight out of the drain opening. It might be coated in grime or hair, so be prepared to clean it. If it doesn't lift out easily, gently wiggle it while pulling upwards.

### Step 6: Clean and Inspect (Optional)

Once the stopper is out, take the opportunity to thoroughly clean the stopper itself and the inside of the drain opening. Remove any accumulated hair, soap scum, or debris. Inspect the rubber seals on the stopper and the pivot rod for any signs of wear or damage that might cause future leaks or poor drainage. This is a good time for maintenance.

### Step 7: Reassemble the Stopper

To put the stopper back, reverse the steps. Insert the stopper into the drain from above. Then, from underneath, carefully reinsert the pivot rod through the drainpipe opening, ensuring it goes into the hole on the bottom of the stopper. Tighten the pivot rod nut, reattach the clevis strap to the lift rod with the spring clip, and test by filling the sink and checking for leaks and proper stopper function.


## Tips & Tricks

- Keep a flashlight handy when working under the sink to clearly see the components.
- Take a photo of the assembly before you start disassembling, especially if you're unsure about reassembly.
- If the nut on the pivot rod is stubborn, a penetrating oil can help loosen it, but be careful not to get it on plastic parts.
- Regularly removing and cleaning your sink stopper can prevent slow drains and unpleasant odors.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the water supply to the sink if you plan on extended work or if you anticipate disconnecting more than just the pivot rod, to prevent accidental flooding.
- ⚠️ Be careful when using pliers not to overtighten plastic nuts, as this can cause them to crack or strip.
- ⚠️ Wear gloves to protect your hands from grime and potential sharp edges inside the drain.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is my sink stopper not holding water?**

A common reason is that the rubber seal on the stopper itself is worn or missing, or the pivot rod is not properly engaged with the stopper. Check these components for damage or misalignment when you remove the stopper.

**How do I remove a stopper that doesn't have a lift rod?**

For 'push-pull' or 'lift-and-turn' stoppers, simply grasp the stopper from the top, twist counter-clockwise (if it's a lift-and-turn type), and pull it straight up. There's usually no under-sink mechanism to disconnect.

**What if the pivot rod nut is stuck or stripped?**

If the nut is stuck, try applying a penetrating oil and letting it sit for a few minutes. If it's stripped, you may need to replace the entire drain assembly, or carefully use locking pliers if there's enough material to grip.

**Can I replace just the sink stopper?**

Yes, you can often replace just the stopper itself, especially if the rest of the drain assembly is in good condition. Make sure to purchase a stopper that matches the size and type of your existing drain for proper fit and function.

**How often should I clean my sink stopper?**

It's a good idea to remove and clean your sink stopper every few months, or whenever you notice slow drainage or an unpleasant odor coming from the sink. This prevents buildup of hair and soap scum.

**What if the stopper is stuck down?**

If the stopper is stuck down, the pivot rod might be bent, dislodged, or the mechanism under the sink could be jammed. Try wiggling the lift rod on the faucet, or carefully try to free the pivot rod from underneath the sink.
