To get ink out of clothes, act quickly and identify the ink type. Common household items like rubbing alcohol, hand sanitizer, or even hairspray can be effective. Always test your chosen solution on an inconspicuous area first to prevent damage.

## What You Need

- Rubbing alcohol (isopropyl alcohol)
- Clean white cloths or paper towels
- Cotton swabs
- Dish soap (liquid)
- Laundry detergent
- Old toothbrush (optional)
- Hairspray (optional, alcohol-based)
- Hand sanitizer (optional, alcohol-based)

## Steps

### Step 1: Identify the Ink Type and Fabric

Determine if the ink is water-based (like from a washable marker) or oil/alcohol-based (like from a ballpoint pen or permanent marker). Also, check the fabric care label for material type and washing instructions, as delicate fabrics may require more gentle treatment. Knowing the ink and fabric helps you choose the most effective and safest removal method.

> 💡 **Tip:** Water-based inks often respond to plain water and dish soap, while oil/alcohol-based inks require alcohol-based solvents.

### Step 2: Blot, Don't Rub

Place a clean white cloth or paper towel under the stained area. Using another clean cloth or cotton swab, gently blot the ink stain from the outside in. Rubbing can spread the ink and push it deeper into the fabric fibers, making it harder to remove. Continue blotting until no more ink transfers to the cloth.

### Step 3: Choose Your Stain Remover

For most common inks (ballpoint, gel), alcohol-based products are highly effective. Options include rubbing alcohol, alcohol-based hand sanitizer, or even hairspray with a high alcohol content. For water-based inks, plain water and dish soap might be sufficient. Test your chosen solution on an inconspicuous area of the garment first to ensure it doesn't cause discoloration or damage.

> 💡 **Tip:** Always test your chosen solution on an inconspicuous area of the garment first to check for colorfastness.

### Step 4: Apply and Blot

Saturate a clean cotton swab or white cloth with your chosen alcohol-based remover. Gently dab the ink stain, working from the outside edges towards the center to prevent the stain from spreading. As the ink lifts, blot it up with a fresh part of your clean cloth or paper towel placed underneath the stain. Repeat this process, changing cloths/swabs frequently, until no more ink is transferring.

### Step 5: Rinse and Apply Dish Soap

Once the majority of the ink is removed, rinse the treated area thoroughly under cold running water. Then, apply a small amount of liquid dish soap directly to the remaining stain. Gently rub the fabric together or use an old toothbrush to work the soap into the fibers, creating a lather. This helps break down any residual ink and grease.

### Step 6: Launder as Usual

Without rinsing out the dish soap, place the garment in your washing machine. Wash it according to the fabric care label's instructions, using your regular laundry detergent and the warmest water temperature safe for the fabric. Adding an oxygen-based bleach (safe for colors) to the wash can enhance stain removal for stubborn marks.

### Step 7: Check Before Drying

After the wash cycle is complete, carefully inspect the stained area *before* putting the garment in the dryer. If any trace of the ink stain remains, repeat the entire treatment process from step 3. Heat from a dryer can permanently set ink stains, making them almost impossible to remove later. Air-dry the garment if you're unsure, or if the stain is still faintly visible.


## Tips & Tricks

- Act immediately when an ink stain occurs; fresh stains are much easier to remove.
- Always test any stain remover on an inconspicuous area of the garment first to check for colorfastness.
- Avoid using hot water or putting the item in a dryer until you are certain the stain is completely gone, as heat can set ink stains permanently.
- For persistent stains, you may need to repeat the treatment process multiple times.
- Place a clean paper towel or cloth underneath the stain to absorb the ink and prevent it from bleeding onto other parts of the garment.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not use chlorine bleach on colored garments, as it can cause permanent discoloration.
- ⚠️ Never put an ink-stained garment in the dryer until the stain is completely removed, as heat will set the stain.
- ⚠️ Always work in a well-ventilated area when using alcohol-based solvents.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does hairspray really work on ink stains?**

Yes, hairspray can work on ink stains, particularly those from ballpoint pens. Its effectiveness comes from the alcohol content, which helps dissolve the ink. Ensure you use an alcohol-based hairspray and test it first.

**Can I use bleach to remove ink?**

Chlorine bleach can remove ink from white, bleach-safe fabrics. However, it should never be used on colored clothes as it will cause irreversible discoloration. For colored items, use an oxygen-based bleach if needed.

**What's the best way to get old, dried ink out?**

Removing old, dried ink is more challenging but still possible. Repeated applications of rubbing alcohol, followed by blotting and then soaking in a mixture of laundry detergent and water, can help break down the set-in stain. Patience and persistence are key.

**Will hot water set an ink stain?**

Yes, hot water and high heat from a dryer are notorious for setting ink stains into fabric fibers, making them much harder, if not impossible, to remove. Always use cold or lukewarm water for initial treatment and air-dry until the stain is gone.

**Is rubbing alcohol safe for all fabrics?**

Rubbing alcohol is generally safe for many common fabrics like cotton and polyester. However, it can damage or discolor certain delicate materials like acetate, rayon, or silk. Always perform a patch test on an inconspicuous area first.

**What if the ink stain is on dry-clean-only clothes?**

For dry-clean-only garments, it's best to take them to a professional dry cleaner as soon as possible. Point out the ink stain to them and specify the type of ink if you know it, as they have specialized solvents and techniques.

**How do I remove ballpoint pen ink versus permanent marker ink?**

Ballpoint pen ink often responds well to rubbing alcohol or hairspray. Permanent marker ink is more challenging due to its stronger dyes and solvents; while alcohol can help, it may require repeated applications or specialized stain removers.
