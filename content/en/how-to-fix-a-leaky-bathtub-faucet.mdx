A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by worn-out washers or O-rings inside the faucet handle. Fixing it typically involves turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet, replacing the faulty components, and reassembling it. This simple DIY repair can save water and prevent further damage to your bathroom.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips or flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Replacement washer/O-ring kit (specific to your faucet)
- Plumber's grease
- Utility knife (optional)
- Rag or towel
- Small container for parts

## Steps

### Step 1: 1. Turn Off the Water Supply

Locate the main water shut-off valve for your house or the specific bathroom you're working in. Turn the valve clockwise until the water is completely off. This prevents flooding and allows you to work safely on the faucet.

> 💡 **Tip:** After turning off the water, open the leaky faucet to drain any remaining water from the pipes and confirm the water is indeed off.

### Step 2: 2. Remove Faucet Handle(s)

Depending on your faucet type, you may need to pry off a decorative cap with a utility knife or screwdriver. Locate the handle screw(s) underneath and unscrew them. Carefully pull the handle(s) straight off the faucet body.

> 💡 **Tip:** Place all removed screws and small parts into a small container to ensure they don't get lost down the drain.

### Step 3: 3. Disassemble the Faucet Stem

With the handle removed, you'll see the faucet stem or cartridge. Use an adjustable wrench to loosen and remove the packing nut or retaining nut that holds the stem in place. Gently pull the entire stem assembly out of the faucet body.

> 💡 **Tip:** If the stem is stubborn, try wiggling it gently while pulling. Avoid excessive force to prevent damaging the faucet body.

### Step 4: 4. Replace Worn Components

Inspect the rubber washer at the bottom of the stem and any O-rings around the stem for signs of wear, hardening, or cracking. Use a screwdriver or utility knife to carefully remove the old, worn-out parts. Replace them with new, identical washers and O-rings from your repair kit, applying a thin coat of plumber's grease to the new parts.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take the old washers and O-rings to a hardware store to ensure you purchase the correct size and type of replacements.

### Step 5: 5. Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully insert the repaired stem assembly back into the faucet body, ensuring it's properly aligned. Tighten the packing nut or retaining nut with your adjustable wrench, but be careful not to overtighten it. Reattach the faucet handle(s) and secure them with their screws and decorative caps.

> 💡 **Tip:** Refer to photos you took during disassembly to help guide you through the reassembly process.

### Step 6: 6. Restore Water and Test

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Check for any leaks around the faucet base or handles immediately. Turn the faucet on and off a few times to ensure it's functioning correctly and that the leak has completely stopped. If a minor drip persists, a slight additional tightening of the packing nut may be needed.

> 💡 **Tip:** If the leak persists after tightening, you may have incorrect replacement parts or a more complex issue, possibly requiring professional help.


## Tips & Tricks

- Before starting, place a rag or stopper over the drain to prevent small parts from falling into the pipes.
- Take photos at each stage of disassembly; these can be invaluable when reassembling the faucet.
- If your faucet is very old, severely corroded, or the repair doesn't hold, consider a full replacement. [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet) can guide you.
- Always use the correct size tools to avoid stripping screws or damaging the faucet components.
- For stubborn nuts or corroded screws, a little penetrating oil applied beforehand can help loosen them.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always ensure the main water supply is completely off before disassembling any plumbing fixture to prevent accidental flooding.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten nuts and screws, as this can strip threads, damage components, or crack the faucet body.
- ⚠️ Exercise caution when using sharp tools like utility knives or screwdrivers to avoid personal injury.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do you fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, you typically need to shut off the water supply, remove the faucet handle, disassemble the stem, replace any worn-out washers or O-rings, and then reassemble the faucet. This addresses the most common causes of drips.

**What is the most common cause of a leaky bathtub faucet?**

The most common cause of a leaky bathtub faucet is a worn-out rubber washer or O-ring located within the faucet's stem assembly. Over time, these parts can harden, crack, or lose their elasticity, compromising the watertight seal.

**Can I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself?**

Yes, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet is a common DIY plumbing task that many homeowners can successfully complete. With basic tools and the correct replacement parts, you can often resolve the issue without needing to call a professional.

**How do I stop my bathtub faucet from dripping?**

To stop your bathtub faucet from dripping, you'll need to replace the internal components that form the seal, usually the rubber washers or O-rings. Begin by turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet, and carefully installing new, identical parts.

**What tools do I need to fix bathtub faucet leaks?**

To fix bathtub faucet leaks, you'll generally need a screwdriver (Phillips or flathead), an adjustable wrench, a utility knife, and a specific replacement washer/O-ring kit for your faucet model. Plumber's grease is also recommended for smooth reassembly.

**How long does it typically take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For most DIYers, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet can take approximately 15 minutes to an hour. The exact time depends on your experience level and the specific design and condition of your faucet.
