Fixing a leaky bathtub faucet typically involves replacing a worn-out washer, O-ring, or cartridge inside the handle assembly. This DIY task can save water and prevent further damage, often requiring only basic tools and a bit of patience. By identifying the type of faucet, you can quickly dismantle it and replace the faulty component.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver set (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Utility knife or putty knife
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge (match your faucet type)
- Plumber's grease
- Teflon tape (optional, for threaded connections)
- Clean rags
- White vinegar (for cleaning mineral deposits)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Locate the main water shut-off valve for your house, or the specific shut-off valves for the bathroom if available. Turn it clockwise until the water flow completely stops to prevent flooding. Test the faucet to ensure no water is coming out before proceeding with any disassembly.

> 💡 **Tip:** Place a stopper in the tub drain to prevent small parts from falling in.

### Step 2: Identify Your Faucet Type and Remove Handles

Bathtub faucets typically come in two main types: compression or cartridge. Look for screws under decorative caps on the handle(s) or a set screw on the side. Use a screwdriver to remove these, then gently pull the handles off. If the handles are stuck, a little wiggling or prying with a utility knife might be needed.

### Step 3: Access the Faucet Stem or Cartridge

With the handles removed, you'll expose the faucet stem or cartridge. For compression faucets, you'll see a packing nut holding the stem in place. For cartridge faucets, there might be a retaining clip or nut. Use an adjustable wrench to carefully loosen and remove the packing nut or clip, then pull out the stem or cartridge.

### Step 4: Inspect and Replace Worn Components

Examine the removed stem or cartridge for worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or seals. These are the most common culprits for leaks. Replace any brittle, cracked, or compressed components with new ones that match the original size and shape. If you have a cartridge faucet, it's often easier to replace the entire cartridge unit. If the problem is persistent or you decide a full replacement is better, consider consulting our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).

### Step 5: Clean and Lubricate Components

Before reassembling, clean any mineral deposits or grime from the faucet body and threads using a rag and a little white vinegar. Apply a thin coat of plumber's grease to new O-rings, washers, and the moving parts of the stem or cartridge. This lubrication helps ensure smooth operation and a better seal.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully reinsert the stem or cartridge into the faucet body, ensuring it's properly aligned. Tighten the packing nut or secure the retaining clip with your adjustable wrench, but don't overtighten, as this can damage components. Reattach the faucet handles and secure them with their screws and decorative caps.

### Step 7: Restore Water Supply and Test

Slowly turn the main water supply back on, checking for any immediate leaks around the faucet base. Once the water is fully restored, turn the faucet handles on and off several times to ensure proper function and that the leak has stopped. If a drip persists, you may need to tighten components slightly or re-inspect for a missed worn part.


## Tips & Tricks

- Always turn off the main water supply before attempting any faucet repair to prevent flooding.
- Keep a bucket and rags handy for any residual water when disassembling the faucet.
- When buying replacement parts, it's best to take the old part with you to the hardware store to ensure an exact match.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Do not overtighten nuts or screws, as this can strip threads, crack components, or make future repairs more difficult. Hand-tighten and then give a quarter-turn with a wrench.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I fix a leaky bathtub faucet that drips constantly?**

A constantly dripping bathtub faucet is usually caused by a worn-out washer, O-ring, or cartridge inside the faucet handle assembly. To fix it, you'll need to turn off the water supply, disassemble the handle, identify the faulty component, and replace it with a new one.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

You'll typically need a screwdriver set (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, pliers, and possibly a utility knife. For replacement, you'll need new washers, O-rings, or a cartridge specific to your faucet model.

**How do I fix bathtub faucet leaks if I'm not experienced with plumbing?**

Even if you're not an experienced plumber, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet is often a manageable DIY task. The process typically involves turning off the water, disassembling the handle, and replacing a worn-out washer, O-ring, or cartridge. Follow step-by-step guides carefully, and don't hesitate to call a professional if you encounter difficulties.

**Why is my bathtub faucet leaking from the base of the handle?**

A leak from the base of the handle often indicates a problem with the O-rings or packing around the faucet stem. These seals can wear out over time, allowing water to escape. Replacing these specific components should resolve the issue.

**How do I know what type of replacement part I need for my bathtub faucet?**

The best way to identify the correct replacement part (washer, O-ring, or cartridge) is to remove the old component and take it to a hardware store or plumbing supply shop. Alternatively, identify your faucet's brand and model number, often found on the faucet itself or in your home's original documentation, then search for parts online.

**How long does it typically take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For someone with basic DIY skills, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet usually takes about 30 minutes to an hour. This includes the time to gather tools, turn off the water, disassemble, replace parts, reassemble, and test.
