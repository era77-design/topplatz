A leaky bathtub faucet is a common household nuisance, wasting water and causing frustration. Often, the culprit is a worn-out washer or O-ring, an issue you can typically fix yourself with basic tools. This guide will walk you through the simple steps to diagnose and repair your dripping faucet.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Replacement washers or O-rings
- Plumber's grease
- Utility knife (optional)
- Rags or towels
- Penetrating oil (optional, for stuck parts)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Locate the main water shut-off valve for your house, or the specific valve for your bathroom if available. Turn the handle clockwise until the water flow completely stops to prevent flooding. Test the faucet to ensure no water comes out before proceeding with any disassembly.

### Step 2: Prepare the Faucet and Workspace

Place a rag or towel over the drain opening to catch any small screws or parts that might fall during the repair. Use a utility knife to carefully score around the faucet handle if it's sealed with caulk, then gently pry off any decorative caps to expose the handle screw.

### Step 3: Remove the Faucet Handle and Trim

Unscrew the handle screw with the appropriate screwdriver (usually Phillips or flathead). Gently pull the handle off. Next, remove any escutcheon plate, decorative trim, or bonnet nuts, exposing the valve stem or cartridge. An adjustable wrench might be needed for stubborn nuts, turning counter-clockwise.

### Step 4: Extract and Inspect the Valve Stem/Cartridge

Carefully pull out the valve stem or cartridge assembly. Pay close attention to how it's oriented for easier reassembly later. Inspect all rubber washers, O-rings, and springs for signs of wear, cracking, or deterioration, as these are the most common culprits for leaks.

### Step 5: Replace Worn Components

Take the worn washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge to a hardware store to find exact replacements. Before reinserting, apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings and threaded parts. This lubricates them, helps create a better seal, and can prevent future leaks.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully place the new components back into the valve body, ensuring they are correctly seated. Reinstall the valve stem or cartridge, followed by the bonnet nut, escutcheon, and finally the faucet handle. Hand-tighten parts initially, then use tools for a snug fit without overtightening, which can damage components.

### Step 7: Restore Water and Test

Slowly turn the water supply back on. Check for any immediate leaks around the faucet base or handle. Operate the faucet, turning it on and off several times, and observe for drips. If the leak persists, you may need to recheck your work or consider replacing the entire faucet.


## Tips & Tricks

- Take a photo of the faucet's internal components before disassembly; this will serve as a helpful reference during reassembly.
- Keep all removed parts organized in the order you took them off to simplify putting them back together.
- Always use plumber's grease on rubber O-rings and threads to ensure smooth operation and a better seal.
- If your faucet is very old, consider replacing the entire cartridge assembly instead of just individual washers, as other internal parts might also be worn.
- Clean any mineral deposits or debris from the faucet body while it's disassembled to improve performance.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the water supply completely before starting any faucet repair to prevent flooding and ensure your safety.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten screws or nuts, as this can strip threads, crack plastic components, or damage the faucet's internal parts.
- ⚠️ If you encounter difficulty or are unsure about any step, it's best to consult a professional plumber to avoid further damage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is my bathtub faucet leaking?**

Most bathtub faucet leaks are caused by worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet mechanism. These components naturally degrade over time due to use and water exposure, leading to annoying drips and constant water loss.

**How do I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself?**

You can fix a leaky bathtub faucet by first shutting off the water supply, then carefully disassembling the handle and valve to access and replace any worn-out washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge. Reassemble the faucet components in reverse order, and then test thoroughly for leaks.

**What tools do I need to fix a bathtub faucet?**

To fix a bathtub faucet, you'll typically need a screwdriver (both Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, appropriate replacement washers or O-rings for your faucet model, and plumber's grease. Rags or towels are also essential for covering the drain and cleanup.

**Can I fix a bathtub faucet without turning off the main water supply?**

No, it is crucial and highly recommended to turn off the water supply before attempting any faucet repair. Failing to do so can result in significant water leaks, potential flooding, and makes the repair process unsafe and messy. Use either your home's main shut-off valve or a specific bathroom shut-off valve.

**How long does it take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For a common washer or O-ring replacement, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet typically takes about 30 minutes to an hour, assuming you have the right replacement parts on hand. More complex repairs, like replacing a cartridge, might take slightly longer.

**What if my bathtub faucet is still leaking after I fix it?**

If your bathtub faucet is still leaking, double-check that all components are correctly installed and tightened, and that the replacement parts are the exact match for your faucet model. If the leak persists, the entire valve body might be corroded, or you might need to consider a full faucet replacement. [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet) can help if replacement is necessary.

**How do you fix bathtub faucet drip?**

To fix a bathtub faucet drip, you generally need to identify the type of faucet mechanism (e.g., compression, cartridge, ball, or ceramic disc) and replace the specific worn component responsible for the leak, such as a washer, O-ring, or the entire cartridge assembly. This usually resolves the constant dripping issue.
