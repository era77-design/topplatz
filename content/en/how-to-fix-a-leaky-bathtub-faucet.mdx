A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by worn-out washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge. Fixing it typically involves turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet handle, and replacing the faulty components. This DIY repair can save water, prevent stains, and avoid higher utility bills.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Utility knife (optional, for caulk)
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge (specific to your faucet type)
- Plumber's grease
- Old towels or rags
- Wire brush (for cleaning)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Locate the main water shut-off valve for your home, usually in the basement, utility closet, or near your water heater. Turn it clockwise until the water is completely off. If your bathtub has dedicated shut-off valves, use those instead. Test the faucet to ensure no water is flowing.

> 💡 **Tip:** Always confirm the water is off by turning on the faucet before starting any disassembly.

### Step 2: Drain and Protect the Area

After turning off the water, open the faucet to drain any remaining water from the pipes. Place an old towel or rag over the drain opening to prevent small parts from falling down it. This also protects the tub surface from scratches.

> 💡 **Tip:** Have a small container ready to hold screws and small parts as you remove them.

### Step 3: Disassemble the Faucet Handle

Most faucet handles have a decorative cap that pops off or unscrews, revealing a screw underneath. Remove this screw with a screwdriver and carefully pull off the handle. You may need to use a utility knife to score around the base if it's sealed with caulk. Once the handle is off, you'll expose the stem or cartridge.

### Step 4: Identify and Replace Worn Components

Depending on your faucet type (compression, cartridge, ball, or disc), you'll need to remove the internal mechanism. For compression faucets, use an adjustable wrench to unscrew the packing nut and pull out the stem. Replace the rubber washer at the bottom of the stem and the O-rings. For cartridge faucets, remove the clip or nut holding the cartridge in place and pull it straight out. Replace the entire cartridge. Clean any mineral deposits with a wire brush.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take the old parts to a hardware store to ensure you purchase exact replacements. A photo of the disassembled faucet can also be helpful.

### Step 5: Reassemble the Faucet

Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings or the new cartridge before inserting them. Carefully reinsert the stem or cartridge, ensuring it's properly seated. Reattach any clips or nuts, then place the handle back on, securing it with its screw. Don't overtighten components, as this can damage them.

### Step 6: Restore Water and Test for Leaks

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Return to the bathtub and gently turn the faucet on and off a few times to check for any drips or leaks. Observe the faucet for several minutes to ensure the repair holds. If you still notice a leak, you may need to re-evaluate the components or consult a professional.

### Step 7: Clean Up and Final Check

Once you've confirmed the leak is resolved, remove your towels and tools. Clean any water stains or residue from the tub. Enjoy your newly fixed, drip-free bathtub faucet! If the issue persists or seems more complex, consider professional help.

> 💡 **Tip:** If you find that replacing parts isn't enough, or if your faucet is very old, you might consider a full replacement. You can find guidance on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).


## Tips & Tricks

- Always turn off the main water supply before attempting any faucet repair to prevent flooding.
- Keep a small container handy to store screws and small parts; this prevents them from getting lost down the drain.
- When buying replacement parts, it's best to bring the old component to the hardware store to ensure a perfect match.
- Apply plumber's grease to new O-rings and threads to ensure a smooth fit and prolong their lifespan.
- Avoid overtightening screws and nuts, as this can strip threads or crack plastic components.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Never force any parts during disassembly or reassembly, as this can cause irreversible damage to your faucet or pipes.
- ⚠️ If you encounter significant resistance or are unsure about a step, it's safer to stop and consult a professional plumber.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, you typically need to turn off the water, disassemble the faucet handle, identify the worn-out component (like a washer, O-ring, or cartridge), replace it with a new one, and then reassemble the faucet. This process addresses the most common causes of leaks.

**What causes a bathtub faucet to leak?**

Most bathtub faucet leaks are caused by worn-out internal components such as rubber washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge. Over time, these parts can degrade, crack, or become stiff, losing their ability to create a watertight seal.

**Can I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself?**

Yes, many common bathtub faucet leaks can be fixed by a DIY enthusiast with basic tools and some patience. Identifying the type of faucet and the specific part causing the leak is key to a successful repair.

**What tools do I need to fix a bathtub faucet?**

To fix a bathtub faucet, you'll generally need a screwdriver (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, pliers, and possibly a utility knife. You'll also need the specific replacement parts like washers, O-rings, or a new cartridge.

**How long does it typically take to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

For a straightforward repair involving a common washer or O-ring replacement, fixing a leaky bathtub faucet can take anywhere from 30 minutes to an hour, depending on your experience and the faucet's complexity.

**What should I do if my bathtub faucet is still leaking after I've tried to fix it?**

If your bathtub faucet continues to leak after your repair attempt, double-check that all new parts are correctly installed and that the water supply is fully open. It might also indicate a more complex issue, such as a damaged valve seat or a need for a full faucet replacement, in which case consulting a professional plumber is advisable.

**Is it worth fixing a leaky bathtub faucet, or should I replace it?**

Fixing a leaky bathtub faucet is often a cost-effective solution, especially for minor issues like worn washers or O-rings. However, if the faucet is very old, extensively corroded, or if replacement parts are hard to find, a full faucet replacement might be a more durable and long-term solution.
