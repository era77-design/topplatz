A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by worn-out washers, O-rings, or a faulty cartridge inside the faucet handle assembly. Fixing it typically involves turning off the water supply, disassembling the faucet handle, replacing the faulty components, and then carefully reassembling the faucet.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Utility knife or small pry tool (optional)
- Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge (specific to your faucet type)
- Plumber's grease
- Rag or towel
- Teflon tape (optional)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, it's crucial to turn off the main water supply to your house or, if available, the specific shut-off valve for the bathroom. This prevents flooding once you start disassembling the faucet. Turn on the faucet briefly to drain any remaining water from the pipes.

> 💡 **Tip:** After turning off the water, open both the hot and cold handles on your bathtub faucet to ensure the water flow has completely stopped before proceeding.

### Step 2: Protect Your Bathtub

Place a rag or towel over the drain opening to prevent small parts like screws or washers from falling down the drain during the repair process. Having a clean, dry surface to work on will also make the job easier and prevent damage to your tub.

> 💡 **Tip:** Use a small container or magnetic tray to keep all removed screws and parts organized. This prevents them from getting lost.

### Step 3: Remove Faucet Handle(s)

Most faucet handles have a decorative cap that can be pried off to reveal a screw underneath. Use a flathead screwdriver or utility knife to gently remove this cap. Then, use a Phillips or flathead screwdriver to loosen and remove the screw, allowing you to pull the handle straight off.

> 💡 **Tip:** Some handles may simply pull off or unscrew without a visible cap or screw. Consult your faucet's manual if you're unsure how to remove it.

### Step 4: Disassemble the Faucet Body

Once the handle is off, you'll typically see an escutcheon (decorative plate) and a retaining nut. Carefully remove the escutcheon, then use an adjustable wrench to loosen and remove the large retaining nut. This will expose the faucet stem or cartridge, which is the core component that controls water flow.

> 💡 **Tip:** Take a photo with your phone at each stage of disassembly. This visual guide will be invaluable when you're ready to reassemble everything.

### Step 5: Identify and Replace Worn Parts

Carefully pull out the faucet stem or cartridge. Inspect it closely for worn-out rubber washers, O-rings, or a cracked plastic cartridge. These are the most common culprits for leaks. Replace these with new, identical parts. If your faucet uses a ceramic disc cartridge and it's faulty, you'll likely need to replace the entire cartridge. For more extensive replacements, refer to [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet - Easy DIY Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).

> 💡 **Tip:** Apply a thin coat of plumber's grease to new O-rings and washers before installation. This helps create a better seal and makes future repairs easier.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Once the new parts are in place, carefully reverse the disassembly process. Insert the stem or cartridge, hand-tighten the retaining nut, then use your adjustable wrench for a final snug tightening (do not overtighten). Replace the escutcheon, reattach the handle, and secure it with its screw and decorative cap.

> 💡 **Tip:** Ensure all components are aligned correctly before tightening. Misalignment can lead to further leaks or improper faucet function.

### Step 7: Restore Water and Test

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Check for any immediate leaks around the faucet base. Then, turn on the hot and cold water handles to test the faucet's operation and check for drips. If the leak persists, you may need to re-evaluate the parts or consider professional help.

> 💡 **Tip:** If you used Teflon tape on any threaded connections, ensure it's wrapped clockwise (the direction the threads tighten) for an effective seal.


## Tips & Tricks

- Before purchasing replacement parts, take photos of your faucet and the removed components, or bring the old parts to the hardware store to ensure you get an exact match.
- Avoid overtightening screws and nuts, as this can strip threads or crack plastic components, leading to more significant damage.
- Keep a small bucket or towel handy to catch any residual water that might come out when you disassemble the faucet.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always turn off the main water supply to your house before attempting any plumbing repair to prevent potential flooding and water damage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, you typically need to turn off the water supply, disassemble the faucet handle and body, identify and replace worn-out components like washers, O-rings, or the cartridge, and then reassemble everything. This usually resolves most common leaks.

**What causes a bathtub faucet to leak?**

The most common causes of a leaky bathtub faucet are worn-out rubber washers, deteriorated O-rings, or a faulty ceramic disc cartridge. Over time, these parts wear down from constant use and mineral deposits, losing their ability to create a watertight seal.

**How to fix bathtub faucet that drips?**

A dripping bathtub faucet is usually a sign of a worn washer or O-ring inside the handle assembly. Fixing it involves turning off the water, removing the handle, disassembling the stem, replacing the old rubber components with new ones, and then reassembling the faucet.

**Do I need a plumber to fix a leaky faucet?**

For most standard leaky faucet repairs involving replacing washers or O-rings, you can often do it yourself with basic tools. However, if the leak is persistent, you suspect a more complex issue, or you're uncomfortable with DIY plumbing, it's best to call a professional plumber.

**How long does it take to fix a leaky faucet?**

Fixing a leaky bathtub faucet typically takes between 30 minutes to an hour for a DIYer, depending on your experience level and the complexity of your faucet's design. Gathering the correct replacement parts might add extra time to the overall process.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky faucet?**

You'll generally need screwdrivers (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, a utility knife or small pry tool, and the specific replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge for your faucet. Plumber's grease and a rag are also useful to have on hand.

**Can I replace just the washer in my leaky faucet?**

Yes, if your faucet uses compression-style stems, replacing just the washer is often the solution for a leak. For cartridge-style faucets, you might need to replace the entire cartridge if it's faulty, as individual washers may not be replaceable within the cartridge itself.
