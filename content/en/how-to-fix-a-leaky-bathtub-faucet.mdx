A leaky bathtub faucet is often caused by a worn-out washer, O-ring, or a faulty cartridge inside the handle assembly. Fixing it is a common DIY task that can save water, prevent further damage, and eliminate annoying drips. This guide will walk you through the simple steps to diagnose and repair most standard leaky bathtub faucets.

## What You Need

- Screwdriver (Phillips and/or flathead)
- Adjustable wrench
- Utility knife or putty knife
- New washers, O-rings, or replacement cartridge (match your faucet model)
- Plumber's grease (silicone-based)
- Rag or towel
- Penetrating oil (optional, for stubborn screws)

## Steps

### Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Before starting any plumbing work, locate the main water shut-off valve for your house or the specific valve for your bathroom. Turn it completely off to prevent water from gushing out once you open the faucet. After shutting off the water, open the leaky faucet to drain any residual water from the pipes.

### Step 2: Remove the Faucet Handle

Most faucet handles are secured by a screw, often hidden under a decorative cap or a small plug. Use a utility knife or flathead screwdriver to gently pry off the cap, then use the appropriate screwdriver to remove the screw. Once the screw is out, pull the handle straight off the faucet body.

### Step 3: Remove the Decorative Trim (Escutcheon)

Behind the handle, you'll usually find a decorative plate or escutcheon that covers the valve mechanism. This might be held in place by screws or simply threaded on. Carefully remove any screws and then unscrew or pry off the trim to expose the valve stem or cartridge.

### Step 4: Access and Remove the Valve Stem or Cartridge

Depending on your faucet type, you'll either see a valve stem (common in older compression faucets) or a cartridge (common in newer single-handle faucets). Use an adjustable wrench to carefully unscrew the packing nut or retaining nut holding the valve stem in place. If it's a cartridge, you might need to remove a retaining clip or use pliers to pull it straight out. Note its orientation for reinstallation.

### Step 5: Replace Worn Components

For a compression faucet, inspect the rubber washer at the end of the valve stem and any O-rings. Replace all worn-out rubber components with new ones of the exact same size. For a cartridge faucet, replace the entire cartridge with a new one that matches your faucet's make and model. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to new O-rings or the cartridge to ensure smooth operation and a better seal.

### Step 6: Reassemble the Faucet

Carefully reinsert the valve stem or new cartridge into the faucet body, ensuring it's oriented correctly. Tighten the packing nut or secure the retaining clip. Reattach the decorative trim, then the faucet handle, making sure the screw is snug but not overtightened. Overtightening can strip threads or crack components.

### Step 7: Test for Leaks

Slowly turn the main water supply back on. Once the water is fully on, check for any leaks around the faucet handle and spout. Turn the faucet on and off a few times, checking both hot and cold water, to ensure the repair was successful and there are no drips. If the leak persists or the faucet needs a complete overhaul, you might consider replacing the entire unit. For a full replacement, consult our guide on [How to Change a Bathtub Faucet: A DIY Replacement Guide](/en/how-to-change-a-bathtub-faucet).


## Tips & Tricks

- Always have a small container or rag ready to catch any drips when disassembling the faucet.
- Take photos with your phone at each step of disassembly; this will be invaluable when reassembling the faucet.
- If you're unsure about the exact replacement part, take the old washer or cartridge to a hardware store for a perfect match.

## Warnings

- ⚠️ Always ensure the main water supply is completely turned off before attempting any repairs to avoid water damage or injury.
- ⚠️ Be careful when using tools, especially screwdrivers and wrenches, to avoid slipping and damaging the faucet finish or injuring yourself.
- ⚠️ Do not overtighten screws or nuts during reassembly, as this can strip threads, crack plastic components, or make future repairs more difficult.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

To fix a leaky bathtub faucet, first turn off the water supply. Then, carefully remove the handle, trim, and access the internal valve stem or cartridge. Replace any worn-out washers, O-rings, or the entire cartridge with new, matching parts. Reassemble the faucet and test for leaks.

**How to fix bathtub faucet?**

Fixing a bathtub faucet generally involves identifying the source of the leak, which is often a worn-out internal component like a washer or cartridge. You'll need to shut off the water, disassemble the faucet handle and trim, replace the faulty part, and then reassemble everything securely. Always ensure you have the correct replacement parts for your specific faucet model.

**Why is my bathtub faucet leaking?**

Most bathtub faucet leaks are caused by worn-out internal components. Over time, rubber washers, O-rings, or the entire faucet cartridge can degrade, crack, or lose their elasticity, preventing a tight seal and leading to drips. Mineral buildup from hard water can also contribute to component wear and tear.

**What tools do I need to fix a leaky bathtub faucet?**

You'll typically need basic tools like a Phillips and flathead screwdriver, an adjustable wrench, and possibly a utility knife or putty knife. Additionally, have replacement parts such as new washers, O-rings, or a compatible cartridge, along with plumber's grease, on hand.

**Can I fix a leaky bathtub faucet myself?**

Yes, most common leaky bathtub faucet issues, especially those caused by worn washers or cartridges, are straightforward DIY repairs. With the right tools and replacement parts, and by following step-by-step instructions, many homeowners can successfully fix their own leaky faucets.

**When should I call a plumber for a leaky faucet?**

You should call a plumber if you've attempted the repair yourself and the leak persists, if you encounter complex issues like stripped pipes or corroded parts, or if you're uncomfortable with the repair process. Additionally, if your faucet requires specialized tools or parts you can't find, a professional can help.

**How do I prevent my bathtub faucet from leaking again?**

To prevent future leaks, avoid overtightening faucet handles, which can prematurely wear out washers. Regularly clean your faucet to prevent mineral buildup, especially if you have hard water. Consider using high-quality replacement parts during repairs, as they tend to last longer.
